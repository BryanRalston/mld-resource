Next-Generation Gene Therapy

Evidence Confidence Guide
Established Peer-reviewed consensus Supported Published evidence Hypothesis Reasonable extrapolation Speculative Requires validation

Three independent research programs are developing next-generation gene therapies that aim to improve on Lenmeldy. Each takes a different approach to overcoming the limitations of current treatment. They are not competing alternatives — they attack different problems, and advances in one do not diminish the others.

This page explains the real science behind each program: what they do differently, how far along they are, and what they could mean for MLD families. We write for informed parents, not scientists — but we don’t simplify away the substance.

The gene therapy pipeline for MLD has never been this active. A decade ago, there was one research program. Today, three independent teams at world-class institutions are working on fundamentally different approaches. Each addresses a specific limitation of current treatment. For families diagnosed today, this means the science is moving forward on multiple fronts simultaneously.

CHOP EA1 Vector: A Better Lentiviral Vector Supported

▼

Researchers at Children’s Hospital of Philadelphia (CHOP) have developed an improved lentiviral vector called EA1 that could become the next evolution of the same basic approach used in Lenmeldy.

How It Works

EA1 uses the same ex vivo strategy as Lenmeldy: harvest the patient’s own blood-forming stem cells, genetically modify them in a lab to carry working copies of the ARSA gene, and reinfuse the corrected cells. The difference is the vector itself — the molecular vehicle that delivers the gene into the stem cells’ DNA.

What Makes It Better

  • 4x more ARSA activity: Cells modified with the EA1 vector produce approximately four times more ARSA enzyme than cells modified with the current Lenmeldy vector. More enzyme means more sulfatide clearance — potentially stronger protection for the nervous system, including the peripheral nerves that respond less completely to current therapy.
  • Superior vesicle-associated secretion: The EA1 vector produces more of the enzyme packaged in vesicles — tiny membrane-bound packages that cells release and other cells can readily absorb. This is significant because the way an enzyme is secreted affects how well neighboring cells can take it up. Better vesicle-associated secretion means better cross-correction: the modified cells are more effective at sharing the enzyme with unmodified cells throughout the brain and body.
  • Fewer genomic insertions: The EA1 vector achieved these higher enzyme levels with minimal lentiviral genomic integrations — meaning fewer places where the viral vector inserted itself into the cell’s DNA. Fewer insertion sites means a lower theoretical risk of insertional oncogenesis (the cancer risk associated with lentiviral gene therapy).

Lead Researchers

Stefano Rivella PhD, Lucas Tricoli PhD, and Adeline Vanderver MD. Published in Molecular Therapy Nucleic Acids, January 2025.

Current Status

The CHOP team is filing an Investigational New Drug (IND) application with the FDA to begin human clinical trials. An IND filing is a significant milestone — it means the preclinical data was strong enough for the researchers to formally seek permission to test in people.

What It Could Mean for Families

If the EA1 vector performs in humans as it has in the lab, it could become the next-generation standard of care for ex vivo gene therapy in MLD. Higher enzyme production could potentially:

  • Improve outcomes for patients who currently have marginal responses to Lenmeldy
  • Provide stronger protection for the peripheral nervous system, which remains a limitation of current treatment
  • Expand the effective treatment window to include patients with slightly more advanced disease
Important context: The EA1 vector is still preclinical — it has not yet been tested in humans. Promising laboratory results do not always translate directly to clinical improvement. However, the IND filing is a meaningful signal: it means the data cleared the bar that the researchers themselves set for moving forward into human testing.

AAV.GMU01: A Single Injection Into the Spinal Fluid Supported

▼

Sanofi is developing a completely different type of gene therapy for MLD — one that doesn’t require harvesting stem cells or undergoing chemotherapy at all. Instead of modifying cells outside the body, AAV.GMU01 delivers a working ARSA gene directly into the nervous system through a single injection.

How It Works

AAV.GMU01 is an in vivo gene therapy. “In vivo” means the genetic modification happens inside the patient’s body, not in a lab. The treatment uses an adeno-associated virus (AAV) vector — a harmless, engineered virus that carries a working copy of the ARSA gene. A single injection is delivered into the cisterna magna, a fluid-filled space at the base of the skull where cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) circulates. From there, the vector spreads throughout the central nervous system.

The key innovation is the capsid — the protein shell of the AAV vector that determines which cells it can enter. AAV.GMU01 uses a novel capsid engineered through peptide library screening in rhesus macaques (non-human primates). This capsid is 10 times more efficient than AAV.rh10, the vector used in earlier MLD gene therapy trials that failed. That means it can achieve equivalent ARSA expression with 10-fold lower viral exposure — less virus needed, which generally means better safety.

Results in Mice

  • ARSA restoration: Sulfatase activity returned to wild-type (normal) levels in the brain, spinal cord, dorsal root ganglia (DRGs), and sciatic nerve
  • Sulfatide clearance: The toxic sulfatide accumulation that causes MLD was normalized
  • Hearing preservation: Prevented the hearing loss that occurs in untreated MLD mice
  • Neuroinflammation reduction: Key markers of brain inflammation (GFAP, AIF1) were significantly reduced
  • Blood biomarker improvement: Neurofilament light chain (NfL) — a protein released when nerve cells are damaged — was reduced in blood, suggesting less ongoing nerve damage
  • Cross-correction: 84% or more of the brain showed evidence of enzyme activity spreading beyond the cells that were directly modified by the vector. This means the treated cells were effectively sharing the enzyme with their neighbors.
  • Broad cell targeting: The vector transduced (entered and modified) neurons, astrocytes, microglia, and oligodendrocytes — all four major cell types in the brain

Results in Non-Human Primates

  • Broad CNS distribution: The vector spread widely throughout the central nervous system via minimally invasive intracisternal magna (ICM) delivery
  • High ARSA levels: Achieved 10.7 times normal ARSA levels at the high dose
  • No immune activation: No evidence of the immune system attacking the vector or the treated cells
  • No behavioral or functional deficits: The primates showed no adverse neurological effects
  • DRG neuron degeneration: Observed at all dose levels. This is a known finding with many AAV vectors delivered to the CNS and is an active area of safety research across the entire AAV gene therapy field. It does not appear to have caused functional problems in the primates, but it will need to be carefully monitored in human trials.
  • No organ toxicity: No evidence of liver, kidney, or other organ damage

Current Status

Preclinical studies are complete. The next step is GLP (Good Laboratory Practice) toxicology studies — the formal safety testing required before human trials can begin. Patents have been filed (WO2023225481A1). Published in the Journal of Clinical Investigation, 2025.

What It Could Mean for Families

If AAV.GMU01 works in humans, it would represent a fundamentally different treatment experience:

  • No chemotherapy: The myeloablative conditioning (high-dose busulfan) that Lenmeldy requires — which is one of the hardest parts of the treatment process for families — would not be needed
  • No stem cell harvest: No apheresis, no bone marrow collection, no weeks waiting for cell manufacturing
  • A single injection: One procedure, delivered into the spinal fluid at the base of the skull
  • Potentially wider access: Because it doesn’t require the specialized stem cell manufacturing infrastructure that Lenmeldy does, it could be administered at more treatment centers
This is the approach that could change who can be treated. Some MLD patients cannot undergo myeloablative conditioning — because of their age, disease stage, or other health factors. An in vivo gene therapy that doesn’t require chemotherapy could open the door for patients who currently have no gene therapy option available to them.

AAV-PHP.eB: An IV Injection That Crosses the Blood-Brain Barrier Hypothesis

▼

Researchers at the Paris Brain Institute are developing what could be the simplest possible delivery method for MLD gene therapy: a standard intravenous (IV) injection. The challenge has always been getting treatment past the blood-brain barrier — a protective membrane that prevents most molecules in the bloodstream from entering the brain. This team believes they may have found a vector that can do it.

How It Works

AAV-PHP.eB is an AAV capsid variant that was engineered to cross the blood-brain barrier. Most AAV vectors, when injected intravenously, end up primarily in the liver and other organs — very little reaches the brain. AAV-PHP.eB is different: it was selected through directed evolution specifically for its ability to enter brain cells from the bloodstream. This means a simple IV injection could deliver the ARSA gene directly to the cells that need it most.

Results in Mice

  • Sulfatide normalization: Toxic sulfatide levels returned to normal in the brain
  • Neuroinflammation reduction: Significant reduction in the brain inflammation that drives MLD progression
  • Effective in early disease: Treated at 6 months (before significant symptoms), mice showed strong biochemical correction
  • Effective in advanced disease: Treated at 9 months (after disease has progressed significantly), mice still showed meaningful benefit

The advanced-disease results are particularly noteworthy. Most current MLD treatments — Lenmeldy, HSCT — are most effective when given before or very early in the symptomatic window. An approach that shows benefit even in more advanced disease stages would address one of the most painful limitations families face: the reality that many children are diagnosed too late for current treatments to help.

Lead Researcher

Françoise Piguet. Published in Molecular Therapy – Methods & Clinical Development, April 2024.

Current Status

Primate evaluation is planned before human trials can be considered. This is very early-stage research — successful mouse results are an important first step, but significant work remains.

What It Could Mean for Families

If a simple IV injection could treat MLD — even in patients who already have symptoms — it would transform the entire treatment paradigm. No chemotherapy. No spinal injection. No stem cell harvest. A standard IV drip, potentially accessible at any hospital with a pediatric infusion center.

Important caveat: This research is at a very early stage. The mouse blood-brain barrier differs significantly from the human blood-brain barrier. The AAV-PHP.eB capsid was specifically engineered for mouse models, and its ability to cross the BBB in primates and humans has not yet been confirmed. Some AAV capsids that work well in mice do not translate to larger animals. The primate studies will be the critical next test. Families should be aware of this approach but should not make treatment decisions based on it.

What This Means for Your Family Supported

▼

If Your Child Qualifies for Current Treatment

Do not wait for next-generation therapies. Lenmeldy and HSCT are available now, with strong evidence of benefit when given early. MLD is a progressive disease — the damage accumulates with every passing month. A treatment that exists today and works is always better than a treatment that might be better but is still years from availability. If improved therapies become available later, they may be able to build on the foundation that early treatment provides.

If Your Child Does Not Qualify for Current Treatment

These three programs are why staying connected to the research community matters. Each one addresses a limitation that may be the reason your child doesn’t qualify today:

  • Too advanced for Lenmeldy? The AAV-PHP.eB research showed benefit in mice with advanced disease. If this translates to humans, the treatment window could expand.
  • Cannot tolerate myeloablative conditioning? AAV.GMU01 requires no chemotherapy at all.
  • Marginal outcomes expected from current gene therapy? The CHOP EA1 vector’s 4x enzyme output could shift the risk-benefit calculation.

For All Families

Stay informed. Connect with organizations like Cure MLD and the MLD Foundation that track research developments and can notify you when new trials open. Consider participating in natural history studies and biorepositories — your child’s data contributes directly to the research that will help future families.

The science is moving. Three years ago, there was no approved gene therapy for MLD. Today, Lenmeldy is saving lives, and three independent programs are working to expand who can be treated and how well. The next wave is not a hope — it is active research with published results at serious institutions. There is genuine, evidence-based reason for optimism about where this field is headed.
