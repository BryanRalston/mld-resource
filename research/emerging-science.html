Beyond next-generation gene therapies, researchers are making important discoveries about MLD itself — how the disease works at a molecular level, what biomarkers can predict outcomes, and entirely new treatment strategies that don’t involve gene therapy at all.

Substrate Reduction Therapy: A Different Strategy Hypothesis

▼

Current treatments for MLD — gene therapy and HSCT — work by replacing the missing ARSA enzyme so the body can break down sulfatides. Substrate reduction therapy (SRT) takes a fundamentally different approach: instead of replacing the enzyme, it reduces production of the toxic substance itself.

The Target: CGT

The enzyme ceramide galactosyltransferase (CGT) is responsible for producing sulfatides in the body. If you can inhibit CGT, the body makes fewer sulfatides — and the reduced (but still deficient) ARSA enzyme activity may be enough to keep up with the smaller load. Think of it as turning down the faucet instead of trying to drain the tub faster.

The S202 Compound

Researchers have identified S202, a potent and selective small-molecule CGT inhibitor. In mouse models of MLD, S202 decreased both sulfatide and lysosulfatide levels — meaning it reduced the toxic accumulation that causes myelin damage.

Why This Approach Is Exciting

  • Small molecules cross the blood-brain barrier: Unlike large enzyme proteins that struggle to reach the brain, small molecules like S202 can cross the BBB — one of the biggest challenges in MLD treatment.
  • Could be an oral medication: A pill or liquid that a child takes daily would be transformative compared to the complexity of gene therapy or stem cell transplant.
  • Complementary, not competing: SRT could work alongside gene therapy or HSCT, providing additional protection rather than replacing current treatments.
  • Relevant for patients who missed the treatment window: For children diagnosed too late for optimal gene therapy outcomes, an oral SRT medication could still provide meaningful benefit by slowing sulfatide accumulation.

Current Status

SRT for MLD is in early preclinical development. There are no human trials yet. However, the concept is proven in related conditions — miglustat, an approved SRT for Gaucher disease Type 1, demonstrates that this strategy can work in lysosomal storage disorders.

Challenges

Sulfatides are not just toxic waste — they serve important biological functions in normal myelin structure and cellular signaling. Inhibiting their production too aggressively could create new problems. Finding the right balance — enough inhibition to reduce toxic accumulation, but not so much as to impair normal sulfatide functions — is the central challenge of this approach.

The precedent matters: SRT is not a theoretical concept. It is an approved, working treatment strategy for another lysosomal storage disorder. The science of applying it to MLD is real and advancing, even though human trials are still years away.

The Alpha-Synuclein Connection: MLD and Neurodegeneration Speculative

▼

A surprising discovery has opened an entirely new window into how MLD damages the brain — one that connects this rare childhood disease to the massive research ecosystems of Parkinson’s disease and Lewy body dementia.

The Discovery

Researchers found that MLD patients show alpha-synuclein protein aggregation in brain neurons — the same type of pathological protein clumping that characterizes Parkinson’s disease and Lewy body dementia. This was entirely unexpected in a myelin disorder.

Why It Happens

It turns out that ARSA — the enzyme deficient in MLD — is not just a sulfatide-digesting enzyme. It also functions as a molecular chaperone for alpha-synuclein, helping the protein fold into its correct three-dimensional shape. When ARSA is absent or deficient, alpha-synuclein misfolds and begins to aggregate.

The Evidence

Postmortem analysis of juvenile MLD cases revealed seed-competent conformers of alpha-synuclein in the brain. “Seed-competent” means the misfolded proteins can cause other alpha-synuclein molecules to misfold too — a self-propagating chain reaction. This finding was published as a preprint on bioRxiv in August 2024 and has not yet undergone formal peer review.

Why This Matters for Families

  • New understanding of disease mechanism: MLD brain damage may not be caused solely by demyelination. Alpha-synuclein aggregation adds a neuronal component that was not previously appreciated.
  • Access to a massive research ecosystem: Parkinson’s disease affects millions of people worldwide. Billions of dollars are invested in anti-synuclein therapies, several of which are already in clinical trials. If the alpha-synuclein connection in MLD is confirmed, treatments developed for Parkinson’s could potentially be adapted for MLD patients.
  • Explains persistent symptoms: Some MLD patients experience neurological difficulties that persist even after successful enzyme replacement. If alpha-synuclein aggregation contributes to brain damage independently of sulfatide accumulation, this could explain why correcting the enzyme deficiency alone doesn’t resolve all symptoms.
  • Opens new therapeutic targets: Anti-synuclein therapies — including antibodies, small molecules, and gene-silencing approaches already being tested in Parkinson’s trials — could represent an entirely new class of MLD treatment.
Important context: This is a very recent finding (2024 preprint) that has not yet been peer-reviewed or replicated by independent labs. The therapeutic implications remain speculative. But the biological connection between ARSA deficiency and alpha-synuclein misfolding is grounded in real molecular data, and it opens research directions that did not exist before this discovery.

Biomarkers: Measuring What We Can’t See Supported

▼

Biomarkers are measurable indicators that help doctors detect disease activity before symptoms appear and determine whether treatment is working. For MLD, where early intervention is critical and disease progression can be subtle, biomarkers are essential tools for clinical decision-making.

Neurofilament Light (NfL) — Blood Test

Neurofilament light chain is a protein released when neurons are damaged. It can be measured with a simple blood draw — no imaging or lumbar puncture required.

  • Elevated in MLD patients at diagnosis, including those who are still pre-symptomatic
  • Correlates with disease severity and MRI abnormalities — higher NfL generally reflects more active neuronal damage
  • Limitation: NfL levels can remain elevated even after treatment, suggesting ongoing subclinical processes that current therapies may not fully address. This means NfL is better at detecting damage than confirming recovery.

N-Acetyl-Aspartate (NAA) via MRS — Brain Scan

NAA is a chemical found almost exclusively in healthy neurons. It can be measured during a standard MRI session using a technique called magnetic resonance spectroscopy (MRS).

  • Described as the “most significant prognostic parameter” — baseline NAA levels strongly predict future motor function outcomes
  • Reflects neuronal health and integrity in real time
  • Higher NAA at the time of treatment = better prognosis

MLD-MRI Severity Score — Brain Imaging

A standardized 0–34 point scoring system that measures white matter abnormalities visible on MRI.

  • Correlates with both IQ and motor function — higher scores mean more extensive white matter damage
  • Provides a reproducible way to track disease progression across visits and institutions
  • Encouraging finding: In treated late-infantile patients, white matter signal on MRI can actually normalize after age 4 — meaning some degree of reversal is possible with early treatment

ADC (Apparent Diffusion Coefficient) — MRI Measure

ADC measures how freely water molecules move through brain tissue. Abnormal water diffusion patterns indicate structural changes in white matter.

  • Abnormal ADC values in central brain regions at diagnosis predict rapid disease progression
  • Helps clinicians identify which patients need the most urgent intervention
  • Can be calculated from standard MRI data without additional scanning time

Sulfatide Levels in Blood and CSF

Newer laboratory techniques — specifically LC-MS/MS (liquid chromatography–tandem mass spectrometry) — can now measure specific sulfatide species in dried blood spots with high precision.

  • Forms the foundation of the newborn screening 3-tier protocol: sulfatide quantification → ASA enzyme activity → genetic confirmation
  • Residual ASA activity below 1% correlates with early-onset MLD; higher residual activity predicts later onset
  • Can distinguish between true MLD and the benign ASA pseudodeficiency that affects 1–2% of the population
For families: Ask your medical team about NfL monitoring and regular MRI with MRS. These biomarkers help track your child’s response to treatment and detect changes early — often before symptoms shift. Biomarker data also strengthens the case for treatment adjustments if your child’s response is not progressing as expected.

Remyelination: Can Damaged Myelin Regrow? Supported

▼

One of the most important questions for MLD families is whether myelin damage is permanent. New research suggests that, under the right conditions, the answer is no — some repair is possible.

Evidence of Remyelination After Transplant

Postmortem studies of transplanted MLD patients have revealed higher numbers of oligodendrocytes — the cells responsible for producing myelin in the brain. More importantly, there is direct evidence of active remyelination: new myelin being laid down around previously damaged nerve fibers.

A New Understanding of How Treatment Works

The traditional explanation for why HSCT helps in MLD was “cross-correction” — transplanted cells migrate to the brain, produce ARSA enzyme, and share it with neighboring cells that lack the enzyme. But new findings challenge this model:

  • ARSA protein was NOT found in oligodendrocytes or astrocytes of transplanted patients — suggesting the enzyme is not being directly transferred to these cell types as previously assumed
  • Instead, the primary therapeutic mechanism appears to be anti-inflammatory: donor macrophages reduce the neuroinflammation that accompanies MLD, creating conditions where surviving oligodendrocytes can differentiate and begin rebuilding myelin
  • The benefit comes from calming the environment, not from directly supplying enzyme to every cell

What This Means

This finding is genuinely hopeful. If the therapeutic benefit is primarily anti-inflammatory, it suggests that damage may not be fully permanent if treated in time. White matter changes visible on MRI have been shown to stabilize and even improve after successful treatment — consistent with active remyelination.

It also means that future treatments targeting neuroinflammation specifically (not just enzyme levels) could enhance outcomes, and that the timing of treatment matters not only for preventing damage but also for maximizing the brain’s ability to repair what damage has already occurred.

The practical takeaway: Myelin repair is possible, but it depends on early treatment. The longer MLD progresses untreated, the fewer oligodendrocytes survive to participate in remyelination. This is another reason why early diagnosis — through newborn screening — is so critical.

The Peripheral Neuropathy Gap Supported

▼

This is the biggest unresolved treatment challenge in MLD today. Current therapies can effectively protect the brain, but the peripheral nerves — the nerves outside the brain and spinal cord that control movement, sensation, and autonomic function — remain vulnerable.

Gene Therapy (Lenmeldy)

When given pre-symptomatically, Lenmeldy does improve peripheral nerve conduction. Younger treatment age correlates with better nerve conduction outcomes — further evidence that the treatment window matters enormously.

HSCT

Stem cell transplant barely touches peripheral neuropathy. Even after successful treatment that stabilizes or improves brain function, peripheral nerve damage often continues to progress. Progressive demyelinating polyneuropathy has been documented in patients years after transplant, even when CNS outcomes were favorable.

Why the Peripheral Nerves Are Harder to Treat

The mechanisms that deliver enzyme to the central nervous system — transplanted cells crossing the blood-brain barrier, donor macrophages reducing neuroinflammation — are less effective in the peripheral nervous system, which has a different architecture and different immune environment. The peripheral nerves need higher circulating ARSA levels to achieve adequate enzyme delivery.

What This Means for Current Research

  • Higher ARSA output matters: Higher ARSA levels in blood cells (specifically CD15+ cells) are associated with improved nerve conduction — directly supporting the case for next-generation vectors like CHOP EA1 that produce more enzyme
  • This is why next-gen vectors with higher ARSA output are so important: More enzyme in circulation means better peripheral nerve protection
  • Dedicated peripheral nerve treatments are needed: The field recognizes that CNS-focused therapies alone are insufficient, and research specifically targeting the peripheral nervous system is an active area of investigation
For families: If your child has received HSCT or gene therapy, ask your medical team about ongoing peripheral nerve monitoring. Nerve conduction studies can detect changes before symptoms appear, and early detection of peripheral neuropathy progression may allow for supportive interventions that preserve function longer.

Newborn Screening: The Game Changer Established

▼

In December 2025, MLD was added to the Recommended Uniform Screening Panel (RUSP) — the federal list of conditions that all newborns in the United States should be screened for. This is the single biggest policy milestone in the history of MLD treatment.

States Already Screening

As of early 2026, several states have already implemented or are implementing MLD newborn screening:

  • Illinois
  • Maryland
  • Minnesota
  • Pennsylvania
  • Utah

New York has a pilot program underway. Other states will follow, but implementation takes time — typically 1–3 years after RUSP addition.

How the Screening Works

The screening uses a 3-tier protocol designed to minimize false positives while catching every true case:

  • Tier 1 — Sulfatide quantification: LC-MS/MS analysis of dried blood spots measures specific sulfatide species. Elevated sulfatides trigger the next step.
  • Tier 2 — ASA enzyme activity: Measures how much functional ARSA enzyme the baby produces. Low activity combined with high sulfatides is a strong indicator.
  • Tier 3 — Genetic confirmation: DNA sequencing of the ARSA gene to identify specific pathogenic variants and confirm the diagnosis.

The Evidence: Germany’s Pilot

A German pilot study screened 109,259 newborns and identified 2 cases of early-onset MLD and 1 case of late-onset MLD. The critical finding: zero false positives. Every baby flagged by the screening actually had the disease. This level of specificity is remarkable for a newborn screening test and validates the 3-tier protocol.

Why This Changes Everything

MLD treatment works best when given before symptoms appear. Without screening, most children are diagnosed only after symptoms have begun — often after significant and irreversible neurological damage. The tragic pattern has been: parents notice developmental regression, months of diagnostic uncertainty follow, and by the time MLD is confirmed, the optimal treatment window has partially or fully closed.

Newborn screening breaks this cycle. A baby identified through screening can be evaluated, genotyped, and — if appropriate — treated before any symptoms develop. For late-infantile MLD, this is the difference between gene therapy at 3 months of age (excellent prognosis) versus gene therapy at 18 months when symptoms have already begun (significantly reduced outcomes).

The combination of newborn screening and effective gene therapy is the closest thing to a “solution” for late-infantile MLD that currently exists. Screening finds the disease early. Gene therapy treats it before damage occurs. The challenge now is making both universally available — screening in every state and every country, and gene therapy accessible to every family that needs it.

What Families Can Do

If your state has not yet implemented MLD newborn screening, advocate for adoption. Contact your state newborn screening program, connect with Cure MLD’s advocacy network, and reach out to your state legislators. Every state that adds MLD screening is a state where future families will have the chance at early diagnosis that makes effective treatment possible.
