Genetic Counseling

What Is Genetic Counseling?

▼

Genetic counseling is a specialized medical service that helps individuals and families understand genetic conditions, their inheritance patterns, and the options available to them. A genetic counselor is a healthcare professional trained in both genetics and counseling — they combine technical knowledge with the ability to help you process complex, emotional information.

A genetic counseling session typically includes:

  • A detailed review of your family history, often drawn as a family tree (pedigree)
  • An explanation of how MLD is inherited and what it means for your family
  • Interpretation of genetic test results — what your specific ARSA mutations are and what they mean
  • Discussion of risks for future pregnancies and for extended family members
  • Presentation of reproductive options, including their benefits, limitations, and costs
  • Emotional support and referrals to additional resources
Genetic counseling is informational, not directive. A good genetic counselor presents options without judgment. They will not tell you what to do. Their role is to make sure you have accurate, complete information so you can make the decisions that are right for your family.

Why It Matters for MLD Families

▼

Genetic counseling serves several critical functions for families affected by MLD:

Identifies Your Family’s Exact Mutations

There are over 250 known pathogenic variants in the ARSA gene. Knowing exactly which mutations are present in your family is foundational — it enables everything else. Different mutations can correlate with different disease severity (though this is not always a reliable predictor), and knowing the specific variants is required for accurate carrier testing and prenatal diagnosis.

Enables Reliable Carrier Testing for Relatives

Once your family’s mutations are identified, carrier testing for siblings, aunts, uncles, and cousins becomes a straightforward, targeted genetic test. Without knowing the specific mutations, carrier testing relies on enzyme levels, which are unreliable due to pseudodeficiency overlap.

Opens Family Planning Options

For parents who want more children, genetic counseling provides a clear picture of the options available for future pregnancies. For carriers in the family who are of reproductive age, it provides information they need before starting their own families.

Family Planning Options

▼

When both parents are carriers of ARSA mutations, each pregnancy carries a 25% chance that the baby will have MLD. Several options exist to manage this risk:

Natural Conception with Prenatal Testing

Parents conceive naturally and test the pregnancy for MLD during the first or second trimester. Prenatal testing for MLD is well-established and can provide a definitive answer. Two methods are available:

  • Chorionic Villus Sampling (CVS) — performed at 10–13 weeks of pregnancy. A small sample of placental tissue is collected and tested for the known family mutations. Results are typically available within 1–2 weeks.
  • Amniocentesis — performed at 15–20 weeks of pregnancy. A sample of amniotic fluid is collected and tested. This can include measurement of sulfatides in the amniotic fluid and ARSA enzyme activity in cultured fetal cells, in addition to genetic testing for the known mutations.

Both procedures carry a small risk of miscarriage (approximately 0.1–0.3% for CVS and a similar range for amniocentesis). Your genetic counselor and obstetrician will discuss these risks in detail.

Preimplantation Genetic Testing (PGT)

PGT is performed during in vitro fertilization (IVF). After eggs are retrieved and fertilized in the laboratory, a few cells are removed from each embryo at the blastocyst stage (day 5–6) and tested for the known family ARSA mutations. Only embryos that are unaffected (either non-carriers or carriers, but not homozygous for disease-causing mutations) are transferred to the uterus.

PGT allows parents to begin a pregnancy knowing that the baby does not have MLD, without the need for prenatal testing or decisions during pregnancy. The main considerations are:

  • Requires IVF, which involves hormonal stimulation, egg retrieval, and laboratory fertilization
  • Not all IVF cycles result in pregnancy — success rates vary by age and other factors
  • Cost can be significant (IVF plus PGT testing), though some insurance plans provide partial coverage
  • The test is highly accurate but not 100% — confirmatory prenatal testing is sometimes recommended

Egg or Sperm Donation

Using donated eggs or sperm from a non-carrier eliminates the risk of MLD in the pregnancy. If one parent uses donated gametes, the child would be a carrier at most (inheriting one mutation from the carrier parent) but could not have MLD. Donors are typically screened for common genetic conditions, though targeted screening for ARSA mutations can also be requested.

Adoption

Some families choose to grow their family through adoption, removing genetic risk entirely. Your genetic counselor can discuss this option alongside the others.

There is no wrong choice. Each of these options has benefits and limitations, and the right choice depends on your values, circumstances, finances, and what feels right for your family. A genetic counselor can walk through each option in detail to help you make an informed decision.

Carrier Testing of Reproductive Partners

▼

Carrier testing is not just for blood relatives of the affected child. It is also important for the reproductive partners of confirmed carriers in the family.

MLD requires two mutated ARSA genes — one from each parent. If a carrier’s partner is also a carrier, their children face a 25% risk of MLD. If the partner is not a carrier, their children cannot have MLD (though they may be carriers themselves).

Partner testing is especially important when:

  • Consanguinity (related parents) — if partners share ancestry, the chance of both being carriers increases
  • Shared ethnic background — in populations with higher MLD prevalence, the probability of both partners being carriers is elevated
  • Limited family history — small families, adoption, or unknown parentage may make it impossible to assess risk through family history alone

Founder Variants and High-Risk Populations

▼

Certain populations have a higher prevalence of MLD due to founder effects — situations where a small ancestral population carried a specific mutation that became more common over generations in their descendants. Known founder populations include:

Population Prevalence Common Mutations
Yup’ik (Alaska Native) Significantly elevated Specific founder variant(s) in the ARSA gene
Navajo (Western) ~1 in 2,500 Specific ARSA variant common in this population
Habbanite Jewish ~1 in 75 Specific founder ARSA mutation

If your family belongs to one of these populations, genetic counseling is particularly important. The higher carrier frequency means that a carrier’s reproductive partner from the same population is more likely to also be a carrier. Targeted screening programs may be available.

Even outside of these populations, MLD occurs worldwide across all ethnicities. The general population carrier frequency is estimated at 1 in 100 to 1 in 200.

When to Seek Genetic Counseling

▼

Genetic counseling should happen early in your MLD journey. The information it provides enables better decisions at every stage:

  • At diagnosis: to confirm mutations, understand the implications, and plan sibling testing
  • Before family planning: to understand reproductive options and their timelines (some, like PGT-IVF, require advance preparation)
  • When extended family wants to know their status: to coordinate carrier testing
  • Before a new pregnancy: to review and arrange prenatal testing if desired

Do not wait until you are actively planning another pregnancy. The earlier you have the genetic information, the more time you have to consider your options and make arrangements.

How to Find a Genetic Counselor

▼

There are several ways to find a qualified genetic counselor:

  • Your treating hospital or treatment center — most major children’s hospitals and all Qualified Treatment Centers for Lenmeldy have genetic counselors on staff or readily available. Ask your care team for a referral.
  • National Society of Genetic Counselors (NSGC) — the NSGC maintains a searchable directory of genetic counselors at nsgc.org. You can filter by specialty (look for metabolic/lysosomal or pediatric genetics) and location.
  • Telehealth genetic counseling — many genetic counselors now offer remote sessions, which can be especially helpful for families in rural areas or those who cannot easily travel. Ask if telehealth is available.
  • Your insurance company — genetic counseling is typically covered by health insurance, especially when there is a documented genetic condition in the family. Contact your insurer to find in-network providers.
What to bring to your first appointment: your child’s genetic test results (if available), a list of all biological family members with their ages and health status, any prior genetic testing done in the family, and your questions. The more information you bring, the more productive the session will be.
