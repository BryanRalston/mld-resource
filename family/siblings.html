Why Sibling Testing Is Urgent Established

▼
Do not delay sibling testing. If a sibling is affected and pre-symptomatic, early treatment could change their entire life trajectory. Gene therapy outcomes are dramatically better when treatment begins before symptoms appear. Every week matters.

MLD is an autosomal recessive disease. Both parents carry one mutated copy of the ARSA gene. This means that for each biological sibling of a child diagnosed with MLD:

  • 25% chance (1 in 4) the sibling is also affected — they inherited two mutated copies and have MLD
  • 50% chance (2 in 4) the sibling is a carrier — they inherited one mutated copy, are healthy, but could pass it to their own children
  • 25% chance (1 in 4) the sibling is unaffected and not a carrier — they inherited two normal copies

A 25% chance may sound small, but it is not. It means that if you have four children and one has MLD, statistically one more sibling could also be affected. The urgency comes from the treatment window: Lenmeldy gene therapy is approved for pre-symptomatic children, and symptoms of late infantile MLD can begin as early as 6 months of age. A sibling who appears perfectly healthy today could be silently affected, and identifying them before symptoms start could mean the difference between full treatment and a missed opportunity.

Testing siblings is one of the single most important actions you can take after receiving a diagnosis.

Testing Options Established

▼

Sibling testing typically involves two complementary tests:

ARSA Enzyme Activity (Blood Test)

A blood sample is tested for the level of arylsulfatase A enzyme activity. In children with MLD, ARSA activity is typically very low or undetectable. This test is relatively quick (results in days to a couple of weeks) and is usually the first screening step.

Genetic Testing

Once the family’s specific ARSA mutations are known from the diagnosed child, siblings can be tested directly for those exact mutations. Genetic testing is the definitive confirmation. It is essential because enzyme levels alone cannot always distinguish between true MLD and pseudodeficiency (see below).

Ask your geneticist for both tests. Enzyme activity provides a fast initial screen, while genetic testing gives the definitive answer. Together, they provide a complete picture for each sibling.

For Newborns and Infants

If a sibling is a newborn, testing can often be done from a simple blood draw. In some states that screen for MLD in the newborn screening panel, your baby may already have been tested — but do not assume this. Confirm with your pediatrician and request targeted testing using the known family mutations, which is more sensitive than the general screening test.

Understanding Pseudodeficiency Established

▼
Low ARSA does not always mean MLD. Pseudodeficiency is a common source of confusion and anxiety for families. Genetic confirmation is essential before any conclusions are drawn.

Pseudodeficiency is a condition where a person’s ARSA enzyme levels are very low — sometimes as low as levels seen in MLD — but the person is completely healthy and will remain healthy. This happens because certain common ARSA gene variants reduce the amount of enzyme produced without causing disease. The remaining enzyme, although reduced, is still sufficient to prevent sulfatide buildup.

Pseudodeficiency alleles are surprisingly common in the general population — carried by roughly 7–15% of people in some populations. This means that in a family where one child has MLD, a sibling could have:

  • Low ARSA due to actual MLD (two pathogenic mutations)
  • Low ARSA due to pseudodeficiency (one or two pseudodeficiency alleles)
  • Low ARSA due to carrier status plus pseudodeficiency (one pathogenic mutation + one pseudodeficiency allele)

This is why genetic testing is essential. Only by identifying the specific mutations can your medical team determine whether low enzyme activity represents true MLD, pseudodeficiency, or a combination. Do not panic over low enzyme numbers alone — wait for the genetic results.

Results and What They Mean Established

▼
Result What It Means Action Needed
Affected
(homozygous for pathogenic variants)		 The sibling has two disease-causing ARSA mutations and has MLD. Immediate treatment evaluation. Contact Orchard Assist (833-672-4278) and your treatment center immediately. If the child is pre-symptomatic, they may qualify for Lenmeldy gene therapy.
Carrier
(one pathogenic variant)		 The sibling carries one mutated copy of ARSA. They are healthy and will not develop MLD. No medical treatment needed. Important information for the sibling’s future family planning — their reproductive partner should be offered carrier testing.
Unaffected non-carrier
(no pathogenic variants)		 The sibling inherited two normal ARSA gene copies. They do not have MLD and are not carriers. No action needed.
Pseudodeficiency
(low enzyme, no pathogenic variants)		 The sibling has low ARSA enzyme levels but this is caused by pseudodeficiency alleles, not MLD. They are healthy. No treatment needed. The low enzyme level is a benign finding. Make sure this result is clearly documented in the sibling’s medical record to prevent future confusion.

Extended Family Testing Established

▼

Once the specific ARSA mutations in your family are identified, testing extends beyond the immediate household:

Parents’ Siblings

Each of the affected child’s aunts and uncles (on both the maternal and paternal sides) has a 50% chance of being a carrier. If they are carriers and their reproductive partner is also a carrier (which is more likely in certain populations), their children could be at risk for MLD.

Cousins

If an aunt or uncle is confirmed as a carrier, their children should also be considered for testing, especially if the other parent has not been tested.

Carrier Testing

Once the family mutations are known, carrier testing for relatives is straightforward — a simple genetic test looking for the specific known mutations. This is much more reliable and less expensive than broad genetic screening. Encourage at-risk family members to get tested, especially those of reproductive age.

How to approach the conversation. Telling extended family about a genetic diagnosis can feel difficult. Some families find it helpful to write a letter or email that explains MLD, what carrier status means, and why testing is recommended. Your genetic counselor can help you draft this communication and may even be available to speak with family members who have questions.

Supporting Siblings Emotionally Supported

▼

Siblings of children with MLD face a unique emotional landscape. They may be dealing with fear, confusion, guilt, sadness, anger, or a mix of all of these — and they may not have the words to express it. How you support them will depend on their age, but some principles apply universally.

Age-Appropriate Explanations

Children need honest information delivered at a level they can understand. Very young children need simple language: “Your brother/sister has a sickness in their body that makes it hard for their nerves to work right. Doctors are helping.” Older children and teenagers can understand more detail and often want it — not knowing can be scarier than knowing.

Books and Resources

Cure MLD provides free books for siblings, including:

  • “Loie’s Disease” — a children’s book that explains MLD in age-appropriate terms
  • “Fixing Genes, Treating Disease” — explains gene therapy in a way children can understand

These are available free of charge through Cure MLD’s care packages. Request them at curemld.com.

Inclusion

When appropriate, include siblings in hospital visits, therapy sessions, and medical discussions. Being included helps reduce feelings of isolation and helplessness. Let them ask questions. Let them see that doctors are working hard to help. Exclusion, even when well-intentioned, can increase anxiety.

Watch for Warning Signs

Siblings may not always tell you how they are feeling. Watch for:

  • Behavioral changes — acting out, withdrawal, regression to younger behaviors
  • Anxiety — trouble sleeping, stomachaches, excessive worry
  • Guilt — “Did I cause this?” or “Why am I okay and they’re not?”
  • Academic decline — difficulty concentrating at school
  • Social withdrawal — pulling away from friends or activities

Sibling Support Groups

Many children’s hospitals and palliative care teams offer sibling support programs. Ask your care team about resources available in your area. Online groups and camps for siblings of children with serious illnesses can also be valuable — they give siblings a space where they are not “the kid with the sick brother/sister” but are surrounded by others who understand.

One-on-one time matters. When so much family energy is focused on the affected child, siblings can feel invisible. Regular, protected one-on-one time with each parent — even 20 minutes doing something the sibling chooses — sends a powerful message: “You matter too. You are not forgotten.”
