Home Modifications Supported

▼

As MLD progresses, your home may need adaptations to keep your child safe, comfortable, and as independent as possible. Not all modifications are needed immediately — your occupational therapist and care team can help you prioritize based on your child’s current needs and anticipated progression.

Mobility and Access

  • Ramps — for wheelchair or stroller access at entrances. Portable ramps are available for temporary or rental situations.
  • Wider doorways — standard wheelchairs require 32–36 inches of clearance. Door frame widening is a relatively straightforward modification.
  • Non-slip flooring — remove loose rugs and consider non-slip surfaces in areas where your child walks or is transferred.
  • Stairlifts or single-floor living — if your child cannot manage stairs, plan for living on a single level or install a stairlift.

Bathroom

  • Roll-in shower — eliminates the need to lift your child over a bathtub edge. A shower chair or bath seat is essential for safety.
  • Grab bars — installed near the toilet, shower, and bathtub. Must be properly anchored into wall studs.
  • Raised toilet seat — makes transfers easier and safer.
  • Handheld shower head — allows bathing a seated child comfortably.

Bedroom and Sleeping

  • Hospital bed or adjustable crib — provides positioning options, side rails for safety, and easier access for caregivers. Adjustable height reduces strain on your back during transfers and care.
  • Monitor — video and audio monitoring for when your child is sleeping in another room. Pulse oximeters with alarms may be recommended by your medical team.

Medical Equipment Storage

  • Suction equipment — if your child has difficulty managing secretions, portable and stationary suction machines may be needed.
  • Feeding supplies — if your child has a G-tube, you will need storage for formula, syringes, extension sets, and pump equipment.
  • Dedicated space — consider designating a closet or area for medical supplies. Keeping supplies organized and accessible reduces stress during emergencies and daily care routines.
Home modification funding. Your state’s Medicaid waiver program may cover certain home modifications. The VA provides grants for disabled veterans’ families. Habitat for Humanity and local charitable organizations sometimes assist with accessibility modifications. Your hospital social worker can help identify available resources.

Insurance & Benefits Navigation Established

▼

Navigating insurance with a rare, medically complex condition is one of the most frustrating and time-consuming parts of the MLD journey. Here are practical strategies to make it more manageable.

Get a Case Manager

Call your insurance company and ask to be assigned a dedicated case manager. Explain that your child has a rare, complex medical condition requiring coordination across multiple specialists and ongoing treatment. A case manager serves as your single point of contact and can help expedite authorizations, resolve claim issues, and coordinate benefits. Write down their name, direct phone number, and email.

Appeal Denied Claims

Claim denials are common for rare disease treatments, and many are initially denied simply because the reviewer is unfamiliar with MLD. Do not accept a denial as final. Your rights include:

  • Internal appeal — request a formal review by the insurance company, ideally by a medical reviewer with relevant expertise
  • External review — an independent third party reviews the denial. This is required to be available by the Affordable Care Act for most plans.
  • Letter of medical necessity — ask your treating physician to write a detailed letter explaining why the treatment or service is medically necessary, citing clinical evidence and guidelines
  • Peer-to-peer review — your doctor can request to speak directly with the insurance company’s medical director

Keep Records of Everything

Every phone call, every authorization number, every claim. Record:

  • Date and time of call
  • Name and title of person you spoke with
  • Reference or confirmation number
  • What was discussed and agreed upon

This documentation is invaluable when resolving disputes.

FMLA and Work Leave

The Family and Medical Leave Act (FMLA) provides eligible employees up to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per year to care for a family member with a serious health condition. Your child’s MLD diagnosis qualifies. FMLA can also be taken intermittently — a few hours at a time for medical appointments. Talk to your employer’s HR department early.

Some states have paid family leave programs that provide partial wage replacement during FMLA leave. Check your state’s Department of Labor website.

For detailed financial guidance including Medicaid, SSI, waiver programs, grants, and Orchard Assist coverage, see our Financial Resources section. Finances should never be a barrier to your child’s treatment.

School & Education Established

▼

Your child’s educational rights are protected by federal law, regardless of their medical condition. Understanding these rights ensures your child receives appropriate services and support.

Early Intervention (Birth to Age 3)

Every state in the U.S. offers a free Early Intervention (EI) program under Part C of the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). EI provides developmental services including:

  • Physical therapy
  • Occupational therapy
  • Speech-language therapy
  • Developmental monitoring and assessment
  • Family education and support

You can self-refer in most states — you do not need a doctor’s referral, though having one can speed the process. Services are provided at no cost to the family and are typically delivered in your home or community setting. Contact your state’s EI program as soon as possible after diagnosis.

IEP (Individualized Education Program) — Age 3 and Up

When your child turns 3, they transition from Early Intervention to school-based services under Part B of IDEA. If your child qualifies (which a child with MLD almost certainly will), the school district must develop an Individualized Education Program (IEP) that addresses their unique needs. The IEP can include:

  • Specialized instruction
  • Therapy services (PT, OT, speech) provided at school
  • Assistive technology
  • Nursing services if needed
  • Transportation accommodations
  • Modified curriculum and goals

You are a full member of the IEP team and have the right to advocate for what your child needs. If you disagree with the school’s proposed plan, you have the right to request mediation or due process.

School Nurse Plan

If your child attends school, work with the school nurse to create a comprehensive health plan that covers:

  • Your child’s diagnosis and current status
  • Medications and their administration schedule
  • Emergency protocols (seizures, aspiration, respiratory distress)
  • Activity restrictions if any
  • Contact information for all caregivers and specialists

Homebound Instruction

If your child cannot attend school — due to health, treatment, or recovery — they have the right to homebound instruction. The school district must provide a teacher who comes to your home to deliver instruction. This can be arranged through the IEP process. It can also be used temporarily during hospitalizations or recovery periods.

Equipment Management Supported

▼

Children with MLD often need specialized equipment as the disease progresses. Managing this equipment — obtaining it, maintaining it, and knowing when to upgrade — is a practical reality of daily life.

DME (Durable Medical Equipment) Through Insurance

Most medically necessary equipment is covered by insurance as Durable Medical Equipment (DME). This includes wheelchairs, standers, walkers, bath chairs, and hospital beds. The process typically involves:

  1. A prescription or letter of medical necessity from your physician or therapist
  2. Prior authorization from your insurance company
  3. Ordering through an approved DME supplier
  4. Fitting and adjustment by the supplier and/or therapist

DME can take weeks to months to arrive, especially for custom items like molded wheelchairs. Start the process early — before your child urgently needs the equipment.

Equipment Lending Programs

While waiting for insurance-covered equipment, or for items not covered by insurance, equipment lending programs can fill the gap:

  • Easter Seals — offers equipment lending and assistance programs in many communities
  • Local charities and foundations — many communities have organizations that lend or donate adaptive equipment
  • Hospital equipment closets — some children’s hospitals maintain loaner equipment for families
  • Online exchanges — groups where families pass along equipment their children have outgrown

Adaptive Equipment

  • Adaptive car seats — specialized car seats for children with positioning needs. Medical car seats are covered by some insurance plans with documentation of medical necessity.
  • Adaptive strollers — medical strollers provide more support than standard strollers and can accommodate older/larger children. They also serve as a wheelchair alternative for families not yet ready for a wheelchair.
  • Positioning equipment — wedges, rolls, standers, and other positioning aids prescribed by your physical or occupational therapist.

Respite Care Supported

▼

Respite care provides temporary relief for primary caregivers. It is not a luxury — it is a necessity for long-term sustainability. Caregiver burnout is real, and it hurts both you and your child. Accessing respite care proactively, before you are desperate, is the best approach.

Sources of Respite Care

  • State Medicaid Waivers

    Many states offer home and community-based waiver programs that include respite care hours for families of medically complex children. These programs are administered through your state’s Medicaid office. Eligibility is based on the child’s medical needs, not family income. Apply as early as possible — some programs have waiting lists.

  • Organizations

    Easter Seals, The Arc, and similar organizations provide respite services in many communities. Some offer in-home care; others provide facility-based respite programs.

  • Home Health Aides

    Skilled and unskilled home health aides can provide daily care coverage. Private duty nursing (for children with higher medical needs) may be covered by insurance or Medicaid. Work with your care team to determine the level of support your child requires.

  • Overnight Respite Programs

    Some organizations and facilities offer overnight or weekend respite stays, allowing caregivers an extended break. These can be particularly valuable for maintaining your health and your relationships.

Connect with other MLD families through Cure MLD’s ambassador program. Other families who’ve been where you are now are your most valuable practical resource. They can tell you which respite services are available in your area, which DME suppliers are responsive, which insurance strategies work, and a thousand other things you cannot find in a guidebook.

Medical Binder Essentials Supported

▼

A well-organized medical binder is one of the most practical tools you can create. You will reference it during appointments, emergency room visits, insurance calls, and conversations with new providers. Include:

Emergency Information Card

Keep a laminated card that can travel with your child at all times. It should contain:

  • Child’s full name, date of birth, and diagnosis
  • Current medications and dosages
  • Known allergies
  • Emergency contacts (parents, backup caregivers)
  • Primary physicians and specialists with phone numbers
  • DNR/POLST status if applicable
  • Any critical medical information (seizure protocol, airway management instructions, etc.)

Specialist Contact List

Name, specialty, clinic name, phone, fax, and next appointment date for every member of your child’s care team. Update this regularly.

Medication Schedule

Every medication, dose, frequency, time of administration, and prescribing physician. Include PRN (as-needed) medications with their indications and maximum doses.

Test Results Timeline

A chronological log of key test results: MRI findings, nerve conduction study results, developmental assessment scores, enzyme levels, blood work. This timeline gives any new provider an immediate picture of your child’s trajectory.

Insurance Information

Insurance card copies, policy numbers, group numbers, case manager contact information, prior authorization numbers for current services, and any relevant correspondence about coverage decisions.

Travel Considerations Supported

▼

Traveling with a medically complex child requires more planning, but it is possible and it is important. Whether traveling for treatment, visiting family, or simply getting away, these practical steps make it safer and less stressful.

Medical Letter for TSA and Airlines

Ask your doctor to write a letter on hospital letterhead explaining your child’s diagnosis, the medical equipment they travel with, and any medications that must remain in carry-on luggage. While TSA does not require this letter, it speeds the screening process significantly and reduces the chance of equipment or medication being questioned.

Portable Equipment

Pack duplicates of critical items when possible:

  • Portable suction machine with extra batteries and tubing
  • Sufficient medication supply plus extras (in case of travel delays)
  • Feeding supplies if applicable (formula, syringes, extension sets, pump)
  • Pulse oximeter if used at home
  • Comfort items and positioning aids

Hospital Locations Along Your Route

Before traveling, identify children’s hospitals or emergency departments along your route and at your destination. If your child has a complex medical history, consider calling ahead to the destination hospital’s emergency department to provide basic information, so they are prepared if you need to come in.

Travel for Treatment

If you are traveling to a Qualified Treatment Center for Lenmeldy gene therapy, extended stays will be necessary (the treatment process involves weeks to months near the center). Resources for treatment-related travel:

  • Orchard Assist — may provide travel and lodging support for eligible families
  • Ronald McDonald House — provides free or low-cost housing near children’s hospitals
  • Cure MLD travel grants — financial assistance specifically for MLD families traveling for treatment
  • Angel flights and medical transport — organizations that provide free or reduced-cost air travel for medical treatment
Airlines and medical equipment. Under the Air Carrier Access Act, airlines must accommodate passengers with disabilities and their medically necessary equipment. Most airlines allow portable medical devices in the cabin. Call the airline’s accessibility desk in advance to arrange seating, boarding assistance, and equipment stowage.
