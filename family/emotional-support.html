Emotional & Mental Health

Acknowledging the Grief

▼

A rare disease diagnosis is a form of loss. It is the loss of the expected normal — the future you imagined for your child, the milestones you took for granted, the assumption that everything would be okay. This grief is real, it is valid, and it deserves to be acknowledged.

You may grieve what your child has lost, what they may lose, what your family’s life was supposed to look like, and the innocence of not knowing what MLD is. All of this is a natural response to an unnatural situation.

The stages of grief — shock, denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance — are not linear. They do not arrive in order and they do not stay settled once you pass through them. You may feel acceptance on Tuesday and rage on Wednesday. You may feel numb for weeks and then break down at a grocery store because a song played. This is normal. This is what grief looks like when it is woven into daily life rather than contained in a defined period of mourning.

It’s okay to not be okay. And it’s okay to also have good days. There is no right way to feel. Laughing does not mean you are not taking this seriously. Crying does not mean you are falling apart. You are a human being in an extraordinarily difficult situation, and whatever you feel is the right thing to feel.

Common Emotional Responses

▼

MLD families commonly experience a range of intense emotions, sometimes all in the same day. Naming them can help you recognize that what you are feeling is shared by others who have been where you are.

Guilt

This is one of the most common and most painful emotions for carrier parents. “I gave my child this disease.” “If I had known I was a carrier, I could have prevented this.” “Why didn’t I push harder for testing sooner?”

These thoughts are understandable, but they are not true reflections of fault. MLD is an inherited condition that neither parent chose to pass on. You did not know you were a carrier — most carriers have no family history of the disease. Carrier testing was not widely available or recommended until very recently. You did nothing wrong.

Anxiety About the Future

The uncertainty of MLD — how fast will it progress, will treatment work, what will next year look like — can create chronic, exhausting anxiety. The internet can make this worse, as searching for information often surfaces worst-case scenarios without context. Try to focus on what is in front of you today while trusting your medical team to help you plan for tomorrow.

Isolation

Rare disease can be profoundly isolating. Most people have never heard of MLD. Friends and extended family may not understand the severity. Well-meaning comments like “I’m sure it will be fine” or “have you tried...” can feel dismissive. You may find yourself withdrawing because it takes too much energy to explain.

Grief

Grief in MLD is not a one-time event. It can resurface with each lost milestone, each new symptom, each medical setback, and each reminder of what could have been. This is sometimes called “chronic sorrow” — an ongoing grief that coexists with daily life. It does not mean you are depressed (though it can coexist with depression). It means you are a loving parent dealing with ongoing loss.

Helplessness

The feeling that you cannot fix this, that you cannot protect your child from what is happening — this is one of the hardest parts. Channel what you can into advocacy, care, and connection. You may not be able to cure MLD, but you can fight for the best possible treatment, the best possible care, and the best possible quality of life for your child.

And Also: Strength

MLD families consistently discover reserves of strength, determination, and love they did not know they had. You will surprise yourself. You will do things you never thought you could do. You will become an expert in a disease you had never heard of. You will advocate fiercely. You will find moments of joy in unexpected places. These positive experiences are real and valid too — do not feel guilty about them.

For Parents & Caregivers

▼

You cannot pour from an empty cup. This is not a cliché — it is a survival strategy. The demands of caring for a child with MLD are relentless: medical appointments, insurance battles, therapy schedules, medication management, physical care, emotional support for your other children, and somehow maintaining the rest of your life. If you do not take care of yourself, you will burn out, and your child needs you functional.

Professional Counseling

Consider working with a therapist or counselor, ideally one experienced with chronic illness, rare disease, or pediatric illness. This is not a sign of weakness — it is one of the smartest things you can do. Options include:

  • Individual therapy — a space that is entirely about you and your processing
  • Couples counseling — rare disease is extraordinarily hard on relationships (see below), and a counselor can help you communicate and support each other
  • Family therapy — can be helpful for processing the diagnosis as a family unit, including siblings

If you do not know where to start, ask your child’s palliative care team or social worker for referrals. Most children’s hospitals have psychosocial support services available to families.

Support Groups

Connecting with other MLD families can be one of the most powerful sources of support available to you. People who have lived this experience understand in a way that even the most loving friend or family member cannot.

  • Cure MLD Family Network — the Ambassador Program pairs you with an experienced MLD family. Contact curemld.com
  • MLD Foundation — family connections and events. Visit mld.foundation
  • United Leukodystrophy Foundation — support for all leukodystrophy families. Visit ulf.org
  • Online communities — Facebook groups and other online spaces where MLD families connect informally

Respite Care

You need breaks. This is not negotiable. Respite care — temporary relief for primary caregivers — is available through multiple channels:

  • State Medicaid waiver programs (many states offer respite hours for medically complex children)
  • Organizations like Easter Seals, The Arc, and local charities
  • Home health aides who can cover care while you rest, run errands, or simply breathe
  • Family members and trusted friends who are willing to be trained on your child’s care needs

Taking a break does not mean you love your child less. It means you are being strategic about sustaining your ability to care for them over the long haul.

Marriage and Partnership

Rare disease is hard on relationships. The constant stress, the different ways partners cope, the financial pressure, the disrupted routines, the exhaustion — all of these can strain even strong partnerships. Research shows that parents of children with serious illness are at elevated risk for relationship difficulties.

  • Communicate. Share how you are feeling, even when it is hard. Your partner is dealing with the same situation but may be processing it very differently.
  • Do not keep score. One partner may do more medical appointments while the other handles logistics. Both contributions matter.
  • Prioritize your relationship. Even small gestures — a 15-minute conversation without talking about MLD, a walk together, a text during the day — help maintain your connection.
  • Get help early. If you notice distance growing, do not wait until it becomes a crisis. Couples counseling is most effective when started early.

For Siblings

▼

Siblings of children with MLD carry a burden that is often invisible. They may not be sick themselves, but their world has been turned upside down. Their parents are distracted, stressed, and often absent for medical appointments. Their home may have been transformed with medical equipment. They may feel scared, confused, angry, guilty, or all of these at once.

Age-Appropriate Honesty

Children are perceptive. They know something is wrong, and the unknown is usually scarier than the truth. Tell them what is happening at a level they can understand:

  • Ages 2–5: “Your brother/sister has a sickness that makes it hard for their body to work the way it should. Doctors are helping them, and we are all going to take care of each other.”
  • Ages 6–10: You can explain more about what MLD is, how it affects the body, and what treatments are being tried. Use the books from Cure MLD (“Loie’s Disease,” “Fixing Genes, Treating Disease”) as conversation starters.
  • Ages 11+: Teenagers can handle and often want more detail. Include them in discussions when appropriate. Answer their questions honestly, including “I don’t know” when that is the honest answer.

Maintain Their Routines

As much as possible, try to keep siblings’ routines intact. Their activities, friendships, and school life provide a sense of normalcy that is psychologically protective. When disruptions are unavoidable, acknowledge them: “I know it’s hard that I missed your game. I’m sorry. Let’s talk about how it went.”

One-on-One Time

Regular, protected one-on-one time with each parent is essential. It does not have to be elaborate — 20 minutes reading together, a trip to get ice cream, a walk around the block. The message it sends is: “You are not invisible. You matter to me.”

School Support

Notify your child’s school counselor about the family situation. Teachers and counselors can watch for signs of distress and provide additional support. Some schools offer counseling groups for children dealing with family illness.

Warning Signs to Watch For

Siblings may not always express their distress directly. Watch for:

  • Changes in sleep patterns (nightmares, insomnia, sleeping too much)
  • Changes in appetite or eating habits
  • Declining school performance or loss of interest in activities
  • Increased aggression, defiance, or emotional outbursts
  • Withdrawal from friends and social activities
  • Physical complaints (stomachaches, headaches) without medical cause
  • Excessive worry about the affected sibling, the parents, or their own health
  • Expressions of guilt (“Is it my fault?” “Why not me?”)

If you notice these signs, take them seriously. Professional support — through a therapist, school counselor, or support group — can make a significant difference.

For Extended Family

▼

Grandparents, aunts, uncles, and close family friends often want desperately to help but do not know how. And sometimes, with the best of intentions, they can add stress rather than relieve it.

Educating Family Members

Most extended family members will have never heard of MLD. You may need to explain the disease, its implications, and what your family is facing. This can be exhausting, especially when you are still processing everything yourself. Some approaches that families have found helpful:

  • Share this website — it was built to explain MLD clearly to people who are not medical professionals
  • Write a single letter or email that covers the basics, and share it with everyone at once
  • Designate a “family point person” — one relative who stays informed and can field questions from others, reducing the number of conversations you need to have
  • Ask your genetic counselor if they are willing to speak with family members who have questions

Setting Boundaries

Well-meaning advice from people who do not understand MLD can be harmful. “Have you tried essential oils?” “My cousin’s friend had something similar and they’re fine now.” “Maybe if you just think positive...” These comments come from a place of caring, but they can feel dismissive and frustrating.

It is okay to set boundaries. You can say: “I appreciate that you care. Right now, the most helpful thing is [specific request]. We are following our medical team’s guidance for treatment.”

How Family Can Actually Help

Specific asks are infinitely more useful than “let me know if you need anything.” Most people genuinely want to help but need direction. Consider creating a list of concrete tasks:

  • Drive siblings to school or activities on specific days
  • Prepare meals (with a meal train schedule to avoid duplication)
  • Mow the lawn, shovel snow, handle household maintenance
  • Sit with the affected child for an hour so you can shower, nap, or take a walk
  • Accompany you to a medical appointment to take notes while you focus on the doctor
  • Research specific topics you assign (“Can you find out what our state’s Early Intervention referral process looks like?”)
  • Handle insurance phone calls (with your authorization) — these can take hours
  • Simply be present — sit with you without needing to fix anything
Tools like Meal Train, CaringBridge, or Lotsa Helping Hands can help coordinate support from friends and family. They allow people to sign up for specific tasks without you having to manage it all yourself. Let someone else set it up for you.

When You Need Immediate Help

▼

If you or someone in your family is in crisis — experiencing thoughts of self-harm, unable to function, or in acute emotional distress — please reach out immediately:

  • 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline: Call or text 988 (available 24/7)
  • Crisis Text Line: Text HOME to 741741
  • Your child’s hospital social worker or palliative care team — they are trained to help families in crisis and can connect you with immediate support

Asking for help is not weakness. It is wisdom. You deserve support.
