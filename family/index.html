Newly Diagnosed: Start Here

Take a Breath

▼

If you are reading this, your child — or a child you love — has been diagnosed with Metachromatic Leukodystrophy. We know you are overwhelmed. We know you are afraid. That is completely normal, and it is okay.

Take a breath. Then take another one.

You do not have to understand everything today. You do not have to make every decision right now. But there are a few things that are time-sensitive, and this page will walk you through them clearly, step by step.

Here is the most important thing to know right now: there is an FDA-approved gene therapy for MLD, and it works best when given before symptoms appear. If your child was diagnosed through newborn screening or sibling testing and is not yet showing symptoms, the window for treatment is open — and that is extraordinary news.

This page is organized by urgency. Start at the top and work your way down. You do not need to do everything in one day. But the first section — Urgent First Steps — should be started as soon as possible.

Urgent First Steps

▼
These steps are time-critical. Gene therapy takes 9–12 months from first contact to treatment completion, and symptoms of late infantile MLD can begin as early as 6 months of age. Starting the process now is essential.
  1. Call Orchard Assist TODAY: 833-672-4278

    Orchard Therapeutics manufactures Lenmeldy, the FDA-approved gene therapy for MLD. Their patient access team will begin the insurance authorization and treatment access process immediately. This process takes time — months, not weeks — so starting it as early as possible is critical. They will guide you through every step, including insurance coverage, copay assistance, and logistics.

  2. Get a referral to a Qualified Treatment Center

    Lenmeldy is only administered at five Qualified Treatment Centers in the United States:

    • Minneapolis — University of Minnesota Masonic Children’s Hospital
    • Atlanta — Children’s Healthcare of Atlanta (CHOA)
    • Philadelphia — Children’s Hospital of Philadelphia (CHOP)
    • Houston — Texas Children’s Hospital
    • San Francisco — UCSF Benioff Children’s Hospital

    Ask your pediatrician or geneticist for an urgent referral to the nearest center. If your doctor is unfamiliar with MLD or Lenmeldy, Orchard Assist can help facilitate the referral.

  3. Test all siblings IMMEDIATELY

    MLD is autosomal recessive. Every sibling of an affected child has a 25% chance of also being affected. If a sibling is affected but pre-symptomatic, they could also qualify for gene therapy — but only if identified in time. Do not wait. Request ARSA enzyme testing and genetic testing for all biological siblings right away. See our Siblings & Family Testing page for details.

  4. Contact Cure MLD

    Website: curemld.com | Email: info@curemld.com

    Cure MLD’s Ambassador Program pairs newly diagnosed families with another MLD family who has been through what you are going through. They understand in a way that no one else can. Cure MLD also sends care packages that include books, guides, and practical resources to help you get started.

  5. Request genetic counseling to identify exact mutations

    If full genetic sequencing of the ARSA gene has not yet been completed, request it now. Knowing the exact mutations in your family enables reliable carrier testing for siblings, extended family, and future family planning. See our Genetic Counseling page.

  6. Start building your care team

    Your child will benefit from a multidisciplinary care team including a neurologist, geneticist, physical therapist, occupational therapist, speech therapist, and more. Your treating center will help coordinate this, but you can begin conversations with your pediatrician now. See our Care Team Overview for guidance.

Within the First Week

▼

Once you have started the urgent steps above, turn your attention to these actions during your first week:

  • Get a comprehensive neurological baseline assessment. This includes a brain MRI, nerve conduction studies, developmental assessments, and possibly other tests. The baseline tells your medical team exactly where your child is right now, which is essential for tracking changes and measuring treatment effectiveness.
  • Contact your state’s Early Intervention program. Every state in the U.S. offers free developmental services for children from birth to age 3 under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), Part C. You do not need a doctor’s referral in most states — you can self-refer. Early Intervention can provide physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy, and developmental support at no cost to your family.
  • Start a medical binder. You are about to interact with many specialists, insurance companies, and support organizations. A well-organized binder will save you enormous stress. Include:
    • All test results (enzyme levels, genetic reports, MRI reports, nerve conduction studies)
    • Specialist contact information (names, phone numbers, fax numbers)
    • Medication lists and schedules
    • Insurance information, authorization numbers, and case manager contacts
    • A timeline of key dates (diagnosis, referrals, appointments)
  • Connect with the MLD Foundation. Website: mld.foundation. The MLD Foundation maintains doctor directories, supports research, and connects families. They can help you find experienced specialists in your area.
  • Join the MLD community. You are not alone. There are families all over the world who understand what you are going through. Cure MLD, the MLD Foundation, and the United Leukodystrophy Foundation all facilitate family connections. Online support groups, phone calls with other parents, and in-person meetups are available. Reaching out may feel difficult right now, but families consistently say that connecting with other MLD families was one of the most helpful things they did.

Within the First Month

▼

As the initial shock begins to settle and you have started the urgent and first-week actions, use this month to build the longer-term framework your family will need:

  • Establish a monitoring schedule. MLD requires regular follow-up assessments to track disease progression and treatment response. Work with your medical team to set up a schedule for MRIs, nerve conduction studies, developmental assessments, and other monitoring. See our Monitoring Schedule page for a detailed guide.
  • Look into financial assistance. MLD treatment is expensive — Lenmeldy is one of the highest-cost therapies ever approved. But financial help exists. Orchard Assist covers most out-of-pocket costs for eligible families. Medicaid, SSI, state waiver programs, and nonprofit grants are also available. Do not let cost discourage you from pursuing treatment. See our Financial Resources section.
  • Consider reaching out to Hunter’s Hope Leukodystrophy Care Network (LCN). Hunter’s Hope can help you find a certified leukodystrophy care center near you, which can coordinate your child’s ongoing care with specialized expertise. Website: huntershope.org
  • Begin thinking about genetic counseling for family planning. If you are considering having more children, or if your extended family members want to know their carrier status, genetic counseling provides the information and options you need. This does not have to happen immediately, but starting the conversation early gives you more time and more choices. See our Genetic Counseling page.
  • Take care of yourself. This is not optional and it is not selfish. You cannot be the advocate your child needs if you are running on empty. See our Emotional & Mental Health page for support resources, and know that it is okay to not be okay right now.

A Word About Hope

▼
Your child was diagnosed before symptoms. This is the best possible scenario for treatment. The window is open. Gene therapy outcomes for pre-symptomatic babies are remarkable — in clinical trials, 100% of pre-symptomatic late infantile children treated with Lenmeldy survived to age 6, 85% had normal cognition and language, and 71% were walking independently at age 5. These are children who, without treatment, would have lost the ability to walk, talk, and eventually all voluntary function. Early diagnosis changes everything.

If your child is already showing symptoms, do not lose hope. Treatment options including stem cell transplant and clinical trials may still be available, and supportive care can meaningfully improve quality of life. Every child’s situation is different, and your medical team will help you understand what is possible for your child specifically.
