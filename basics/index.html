What is MLD?

Overview

▼

Metachromatic leukodystrophy (MLD) is a rare, inherited disease that progressively destroys myelin — the protective fatty coating that surrounds nerve fibers throughout the brain, spinal cord, and peripheral nerves. Without healthy myelin, nerves cannot transmit signals properly, leading to a gradual loss of motor and cognitive function.

MLD belongs to a group of disorders called lysosomal storage diseases. Lysosomes are small structures inside every cell that act as recycling centers, breaking down waste materials so cells can reuse them. In MLD, a critical enzyme needed for this recycling process is missing or severely deficient, causing toxic waste to accumulate inside cells and damage the nervous system.

The name itself tells the story:

  • Metachromatic — refers to how the accumulated waste material (sulfatides) stains a different color than expected under a microscope. When scientists apply certain dyes to tissue from a person with MLD, the sulfatide deposits change the dye’s color, a phenomenon called metachromasia. This distinctive staining pattern is how the disease was originally identified.
  • Leuko — means “white,” referring to the white matter of the brain (nerve fibers covered in myelin)
  • Dystrophy — means abnormal or impaired development/deterioration

In plain language: MLD is a disease where toxic buildup destroys the white matter of the nervous system.

What Causes MLD?

▼

MLD is caused by a deficiency of an enzyme called arylsulfatase A (ARSA). This enzyme’s job is to break down a specific type of fat molecule called sulfatides (also called cerebroside sulfate or sulfated glycosphingolipids). Sulfatides are a normal and necessary component of myelin — the insulation around your nerve fibers.

Here is what happens in a healthy body versus in MLD:

In a Healthy Body

Myelin is constantly being maintained — old myelin components are broken down and recycled, and new myelin is built. The ARSA enzyme, located inside lysosomes, breaks sulfatides down into simpler molecules that the cell can reuse or dispose of safely. This is a normal, continuous process.

In MLD

When the ARSA enzyme is missing or severely deficient, sulfatides cannot be broken down. They accumulate to toxic levels inside lysosomes and eventually throughout the cell. This buildup is particularly devastating to oligodendrocytes — the specialized cells in the brain and spinal cord that produce and maintain myelin. As oligodendrocytes are damaged and die, the myelin they maintain breaks down in a process called demyelination.

The ARSA enzyme is produced by instructions encoded in the ARSA gene, located on chromosome 22 at position q13.33. When both copies of this gene carry mutations (one inherited from each parent), the body cannot produce enough functional ARSA enzyme, and MLD results.

Why does it affect the whole nervous system? MLD damages both the central nervous system (brain and spinal cord) and the peripheral nervous system (nerves that run through the arms, legs, and organs). This is because myelin exists in both systems, and sulfatides accumulate wherever myelin is present. This dual involvement explains why children with MLD experience both cognitive/brain-related symptoms and nerve-related symptoms like weakness and numbness in the limbs.

How Common is MLD?

▼

MLD is rare. It affects approximately 1 in 40,000 to 1 in 160,000 people worldwide. The wide range in estimates reflects that the disease is likely underdiagnosed, particularly in populations without widespread genetic screening, and because the adult form can be misdiagnosed as other conditions for years.

In the United States, it is estimated that roughly 20 to 40 children are born with MLD each year. Globally, the number is higher, but exact figures are difficult to establish because many countries lack comprehensive rare disease registries.

Some populations have a significantly higher prevalence due to founder effects (where a small original population carried the mutation, and it became more common in their descendants):

  • Habbanite Jewish community — approximately 1 in 75
  • Western Navajo populations — approximately 1 in 2,500
  • Certain Arab populations in Israel — approximately 1 in 8,000

In the general population, the carrier frequency is estimated at approximately 1 in 100 to 1 in 200 people. Most carriers have no idea they carry one mutated copy of the ARSA gene because carriers are completely healthy — you need two mutated copies to develop MLD.

The Three Types of MLD

▼

MLD is classified into three types based on when symptoms first appear. The type your child has is important because it determines how quickly the disease progresses and which treatments may be most effective.

Type % of Cases Onset Age Progression
Late Infantile 50–60% 6–30 months Rapid — most severe form
Juvenile 20–30% 4 years to adolescence Moderate
Adult 15–20% Teens and beyond Slowest — variable

In general, earlier onset means faster progression. The late infantile form is both the most common and the most rapidly progressive. However, there is variability even within each type, and every child’s journey is different.

What Happens in MLD?

▼

MLD is a progressive disease, meaning it gets worse over time. The exact course depends on the type (late infantile, juvenile, or adult), but the underlying process is the same: sulfatides accumulate, myelin is destroyed, and nerve function declines.

The Progression

Because myelin exists throughout the nervous system, MLD can affect virtually any neurological function. As the disease progresses, families may see changes in:

  • Motor function — walking, coordination, balance, fine motor skills, and eventually all voluntary movement
  • Speech and language — difficulty speaking, understanding language, or both
  • Cognition — learning, memory, attention, problem-solving
  • Vision and hearing — may be affected as the disease advances
  • Swallowing and feeding — difficulty eating safely, risk of aspiration
  • Bladder and bowel control — peripheral nerve involvement can affect these functions
  • Sensation — numbness, tingling, or pain from peripheral nerve damage

It is important to understand that while MLD is a devastating disease, your child is still your child at every stage. Many families describe their children as remaining emotionally connected and aware of love and comfort even as other abilities decline. The disease takes abilities, but it does not take who your child is.

Peripheral Neuropathy

One of the distinctive features of MLD compared to some other leukodystrophies is its significant peripheral nerve involvement. The peripheral nerves — the long nerve fibers that extend from the spinal cord to the muscles, skin, and organs — are also wrapped in myelin (produced by Schwann cells, rather than oligodendrocytes). Sulfatide buildup damages these nerves too, leading to:

  • Muscle weakness, especially in the legs
  • Decreased or absent reflexes
  • Numbness or abnormal sensations
  • Difficulty with balance and coordination

Peripheral neuropathy is often one of the earliest measurable signs of MLD, sometimes detectable on nerve conduction studies before other symptoms become obvious.

How is MLD Diagnosed?

▼

Diagnosing MLD typically involves a combination of tests:

  • Enzyme testing — measuring ARSA activity in blood or skin cells (low levels suggest MLD)
  • Urine sulfatide levels — elevated in people with MLD
  • Genetic testing — identifies the specific mutations in the ARSA gene
  • MRI of the brain — shows characteristic patterns of white matter damage
  • Nerve conduction studies — detect peripheral neuropathy
Important: Pseudodeficiency. Some people naturally have very low ARSA enzyme levels but are completely healthy — a condition called pseudodeficiency. This means that low enzyme levels alone are not enough to diagnose MLD. Genetic testing is essential to distinguish true MLD from pseudodeficiency. Your medical team will use a combination of enzyme testing, genetic testing, clinical evaluation, and imaging to make the diagnosis.

In December 2025, MLD was added to the Recommended Uniform Screening Panel (RUSP) for newborn screening in the United States. Several states have already begun screening. Early detection through newborn screening is transformative because it allows treatment to begin before symptoms appear.

Is There Treatment?

▼

Yes — and this is the most important thing to know. For the first time in history, there is an FDA-approved treatment for MLD.

Gene Therapy Is Here. In March 2024, the FDA approved Lenmeldy (atidarsagene autotemcel), a gene therapy for children with pre-symptomatic late infantile MLD or early symptomatic early juvenile MLD. Lenmeldy works by inserting a working copy of the ARSA gene into the child’s own stem cells, allowing the body to produce the missing enzyme. In clinical trials, pre-symptomatic children treated with Lenmeldy showed remarkable outcomes: 100% survival at age 6, 85% with normal cognition and language, and 71% walking independently at age 5. This treatment is real, available now, and covered by insurance.

Other treatment approaches include:

  • Hematopoietic stem cell transplant (HSCT) — using donor stem cells to provide a source of ARSA enzyme. This has been used for decades and can be beneficial, especially in the juvenile form when started early.
  • Clinical trials — several new therapies are in development, including improved gene therapy vectors and enzyme replacement approaches.
  • Supportive care — physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy, pain management, nutritional support, and specialized medical care to maintain quality of life.

The critical factor for all treatments is timing. Treatment outcomes are dramatically better when started before significant symptoms appear. This is why early diagnosis — through newborn screening, sibling testing, or prompt clinical evaluation — is so vitally important.

Living with MLD

▼

An MLD diagnosis changes a family’s world. There is no way around that. But families living with MLD consistently say two things:

  1. You are not alone. The MLD community, while small, is deeply connected and supportive. Organizations like Cure MLD, the MLD Foundation, and the United Leukodystrophy Foundation connect families, fund research, and provide tangible help — from care packages to travel grants to ambassador programs that pair newly diagnosed families with experienced ones.
  2. There is more hope now than ever before. Gene therapy is approved. Newborn screening is expanding. Clinical trials are underway. The landscape for MLD families today is fundamentally different from what it was even five years ago.

This resource exists to help you understand the disease, navigate the medical system, connect with support, and make informed decisions for your family. Take it one page at a time. You don’t need to absorb everything today.

Key Terms to Know

▼
Term What It Means
ARSA Arylsulfatase A — the enzyme that is deficient in MLD. It breaks down sulfatides.
Sulfatides A type of fat molecule found in myelin. When ARSA is deficient, sulfatides build up to toxic levels.
Myelin The protective fatty sheath around nerve fibers. Enables fast, efficient nerve signal transmission.
Demyelination The breakdown or loss of myelin. This is the core process in MLD.
Oligodendrocytes Cells in the brain and spinal cord that produce and maintain myelin.
Lysosomal storage disease A group of diseases where enzyme deficiency causes waste to accumulate in cell lysosomes. MLD is one of about 70 such diseases.
Autosomal recessive The inheritance pattern of MLD. Both parents must carry one mutated gene copy. Each child has a 25% chance of being affected.
Leukodystrophy A broader category of diseases that affect the white matter of the brain. MLD is one of more than 50 leukodystrophies.
Pseudodeficiency A condition where ARSA enzyme levels are very low but the person is healthy. Important to distinguish from true MLD.
