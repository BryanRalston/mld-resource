Understanding the Three Types Established

▼

MLD is classified into three types based on the age when symptoms first appear. This is the single most important factor in predicting how the disease will progress. In general, earlier onset means faster progression and more severe outcomes without treatment.

All three types are caused by the same underlying problem — deficiency of the ARSA enzyme leading to sulfatide accumulation — but the amount of residual enzyme activity varies. Children with no residual ARSA activity typically develop the late infantile form, while those with small amounts of remaining activity tend to develop symptoms later (juvenile or adult forms).

It is important to understand that these categories have blurred boundaries. A child whose symptoms begin at age 3 might be classified as late infantile or early juvenile depending on the clinician. What matters most is not the label, but the specific trajectory your child is on and how quickly intervention can begin.

A note about terminology: You may see different terms used in medical literature. “Late infantile” is sometimes called “early onset.” The juvenile form is sometimes split into “early juvenile” (ages 4–6) and “late juvenile” (ages 7–16). The adult form is sometimes called “late onset.” These all refer to the same spectrum of disease.

Late Infantile MLD Established

▼

The late infantile form is the most common type, accounting for 50–60% of all MLD cases. It is also the most rapidly progressive form. Symptoms typically begin between 6 and 30 months of age, often in a child who appeared to be developing normally up to that point.

Early Signs (6–18 months)

The earliest signs are often subtle and can be mistaken for normal developmental variation or other common conditions:

  • Developmental slowing or plateau — the child may stop gaining new skills at the expected rate, or skills that were emerging (like pulling to stand) may seem to stall
  • Hypotonia (“floppiness”) — decreased muscle tone, the child may feel limp when held or have difficulty supporting their head and trunk
  • Difficulty with walking — delayed walking, unsteady gait, frequent falls, or a child who was starting to walk may regress
  • Irritability — increased fussiness that doesn’t have an obvious cause
The critical difference: In typical development, children may have periods where progress slows while they consolidate skills. In MLD, the slowing is followed by regression — the loss of skills the child had already gained. Regression is the key warning sign that distinguishes MLD from normal developmental variation.

Progressive Phase (12–30 months after onset)

As the disease progresses, symptoms become more apparent:

  • Motor regression — loss of previously acquired motor skills, difficulty sitting, crawling, or walking
  • Muscle weakness — progressive weakness, especially in the legs
  • Tremors — shaking, particularly noticeable in the hands
  • Peripheral neuropathy — damage to nerves outside the brain and spinal cord, causing weakness, decreased reflexes, and abnormal sensation
  • Spasticity — muscles become stiff and tight (this often coexists with the weakness from peripheral neuropathy, creating a complex motor picture)

Advanced Phase (18–36+ months after onset)

  • Loss of speech — words and sounds that were developing are lost
  • Feeding difficulties — difficulty chewing and swallowing (dysphagia), risk of food or liquid going into the lungs (aspiration)
  • Seizures — occur in approximately 14–39% of children with late infantile MLD
  • Vision changes — may include optic atrophy (damage to the optic nerve)
  • Progressive loss of all voluntary function — the child gradually loses the ability to move, communicate, eat, and eventually awareness

Prognosis Without Treatment

Without treatment, late infantile MLD is fatal. Most children with this form pass away between ages 5 and 8, though some may live longer with dedicated supportive care. The cause of death is typically complications of the neurological decline, including respiratory failure or infection.

With treatment, the story changes. For children who receive Lenmeldy gene therapy before symptoms appear, clinical trial data shows dramatically different outcomes: 100% alive at age 6, 85% with normal cognition and language, and 71% walking independently at age 5. Juvenile MLD Established

▼

The juvenile form accounts for 20–30% of MLD cases. Symptoms typically begin between age 4 and adolescence. Some clinicians further divide this into early juvenile (ages 4–6) and late juvenile (ages 7–16). Progression is slower than the late infantile form but still significant.

Early Signs

Because children with juvenile MLD have already achieved more developmental milestones before the disease manifests, the first signs look quite different from the late infantile form:

  • School difficulties — declining grades, trouble concentrating, difficulty with tasks that were previously manageable, needing more time to complete work
  • Behavioral changes — personality shifts, increased irritability, emotional outbursts, social withdrawal, or unusual behavior
  • Clumsiness — increasing difficulty with coordination, stumbling, dropping things, handwriting deterioration
  • Speech changes — slurring, difficulty finding words, or speaking more slowly
Diagnostic challenge: These early signs are often attributed to learning disabilities, ADHD, behavioral problems, or emotional difficulties. It is not uncommon for children with juvenile MLD to be evaluated by psychologists, educational specialists, or child psychiatrists before the true diagnosis is identified. If your child is experiencing unexplained neurological decline, ask your doctor whether metabolic or genetic testing might be appropriate.

Progressive Phase

As the disease advances, motor symptoms become more prominent:

  • Gait disturbance — walking becomes increasingly difficult, the child may need assistive devices and eventually a wheelchair
  • Fine motor decline — difficulty with writing, eating with utensils, buttoning clothes
  • Cognitive decline — progressive loss of academic skills, reasoning, and memory
  • Speech deterioration — progresses from slurring to difficulty being understood, eventually to loss of verbal communication
  • Swallowing difficulties — increasing risk of aspiration, may eventually require tube feeding
  • Peripheral neuropathy — weakness, decreased reflexes, and sensory changes in the extremities

Prognosis Without Treatment

The juvenile form progresses more slowly than the late infantile form. Without treatment, life expectancy is approximately 10 to 20 years after symptom onset, though this varies considerably. Some individuals with late juvenile onset may live into their 20s or 30s.

Stem cell transplant (HSCT) has shown benefit for some children with early juvenile MLD, particularly when performed before significant cognitive decline. Lenmeldy gene therapy is approved for children with early symptomatic early juvenile MLD.

Adult MLD Established

▼

The adult form accounts for 15–20% of MLD cases. Symptoms typically begin in the teens or later, sometimes not until the 30s, 40s, or even 50s. This form has the slowest progression and the most variable course, with some individuals experiencing periods of relative stability.

Early Signs

The adult form is unique in that psychiatric and behavioral symptoms are often the first manifestation, sometimes appearing years before any motor or neurological signs:

  • Psychiatric symptoms — depression, anxiety, psychosis (hallucinations or delusions), personality changes, emotional instability
  • Behavioral changes — social withdrawal, poor judgment, impulsive behavior, difficulty maintaining relationships or employment
  • Cognitive decline — difficulty with complex tasks, declining work performance, trouble with planning and organization, memory problems
  • Substance use — in some cases, the disinhibition and judgment impairment may lead to substance use issues that further mask the underlying diagnosis
Misdiagnosis is common. Because the first symptoms are psychiatric, adult-onset MLD is frequently misdiagnosed as schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, depression, or other psychiatric conditions. The correct diagnosis may not be made for years — sometimes a decade or more. Key clues that should prompt testing for MLD include: psychiatric symptoms that don’t respond well to standard treatments, progressive cognitive decline alongside psychiatric symptoms, or the emergence of neurological symptoms (gait problems, neuropathy) in someone with a psychiatric diagnosis.

Progressive Phase

Motor and neurological symptoms eventually develop:

  • Gait problems — difficulty walking, balance issues, spasticity
  • Peripheral neuropathy — may be mild or absent initially, but often develops over time
  • Incontinence — loss of bladder and bowel control
  • Spasticity — progressive muscle stiffness
  • Continued cognitive decline — progressive loss of executive function, memory, and eventually broader cognitive abilities

Prognosis

The adult form has the most variable prognosis. Some individuals progress slowly over 20–30 or more years, with periods of relative stability where the disease appears to plateau. Others have a more steadily progressive course. Factors that may influence the rate of progression include the specific ARSA mutations involved and the amount of residual enzyme activity.

Treatment options for adult-onset MLD are more limited. Lenmeldy gene therapy is currently approved only for children. Stem cell transplant has been tried with variable results in adults. Clinical trials may offer additional options.

Determining Your Child’s Type Established

▼

Your child’s medical team will determine the type of MLD based on:

  • Age when symptoms first appeared — this is the primary factor
  • Rate of progression — how quickly symptoms are changing
  • ARSA enzyme activity level — lower levels are associated with earlier onset
  • Specific ARSA gene mutations — some mutations are known to be associated with particular types, though this is not always predictable
  • MRI findings — the pattern and severity of white matter changes
  • Nerve conduction study results — the degree of peripheral nerve involvement

If your child was diagnosed through newborn screening or sibling testing before symptoms appeared, the type may be predicted based on ARSA activity levels and genetic mutation analysis, but close monitoring is essential to confirm the trajectory.

Siblings may have the same type. If you have one child with late infantile MLD, another affected sibling is very likely to have the same type. This is because they share the same genetic mutations. However, there are rare cases of different types within a family, so your medical team will evaluate each child individually.
