How MLD Is Diagnosed Established

▼

Diagnosing MLD involves a combination of tests that work together to build a complete picture.[1][4] No single test is sufficient on its own. Your medical team will typically use several of the following to confirm a diagnosis:

  • ARSA enzyme activity testing — measures how much functional enzyme is present[1]
  • Urine sulfatide levels — checks for abnormal sulfatide accumulation
  • Genetic (molecular) testing — identifies specific ARSA gene mutations[1][3]
  • MRI of the brain — reveals characteristic patterns of white matter damage[4]
  • Nerve conduction studies — detect peripheral nerve involvement
  • Clinical evaluation — neurological exam, developmental assessment, and medical history

The path to diagnosis varies. Some children are diagnosed after parents notice developmental concerns and seek medical evaluation. Others are identified through newborn screening programs. And some are diagnosed because an older sibling was already found to have MLD, prompting testing of the entire family.

The diagnostic journey can be long. For families who reach diagnosis through clinical symptoms, the average time from first concerns to confirmed diagnosis can be months or even years, especially for the juvenile and adult forms. If you are in the process of seeking answers, know that this frustration is shared by many MLD families. Push for comprehensive metabolic and genetic testing if your child has unexplained neurological regression.

ARSA Enzyme Activity Testing Established

▼

Enzyme activity testing is typically the first laboratory test performed when MLD is suspected.[1][4] It measures how well the ARSA enzyme is working in a sample of your child’s blood or skin cells.

How It Works Established

  • Blood test (leukocyte assay): A blood sample is drawn and the white blood cells (leukocytes) are isolated. The ARSA enzyme activity in these cells is measured using a synthetic substrate that mimics sulfatides. The lab reports the activity level, typically in nanomoles per hour per milligram of protein.
  • Skin fibroblast assay: A small skin biopsy (usually from the forearm) is taken and the skin cells are cultured in a lab. ARSA activity is then measured in these cultured cells. This test takes longer (weeks for the cells to grow) but can be more accurate in some situations.

Interpreting Results Established

ARSA Activity Level What It Usually Means
Normal range MLD is unlikely (but saposin B deficiency is still possible in rare cases)
Reduced (intermediate) May indicate carrier status, pseudodeficiency, or early/mild MLD — further testing needed
Very low / absent Consistent with MLD — but could also be pseudodeficiency. Genetic testing is essential to distinguish.
Critical: The pseudodeficiency trap. Approximately 7–15% of the general population carries a pseudodeficiency allele[1] that causes very low ARSA enzyme activity in people who are completely healthy. This means that low ARSA enzyme activity alone does not diagnose MLD. Your medical team must combine enzyme results with genetic testing, clinical symptoms, urine sulfatides, and imaging to make an accurate diagnosis. Never accept a diagnosis based solely on enzyme levels.

Urine Sulfatide Testing Established

▼

Urine sulfatide testing measures the amount of sulfatide being excreted in the urine. Because the kidneys filter sulfatides from the blood, elevated urine sulfatides indicate that sulfatides are accumulating in the body — a hallmark of MLD.[1][4]

Why This Test Matters Established

  • People with MLD have significantly elevated urine sulfatide levels
  • People with pseudodeficiency have normal urine sulfatide levels (because their enzyme, while reduced, is still sufficient to prevent accumulation)
  • This makes urine sulfatide testing a valuable tool for distinguishing MLD from pseudodeficiency

How It Works Established

A urine sample is collected (often a 24-hour collection, though spot samples can be used) and sent to a specialized laboratory. The sulfatides are extracted and measured, usually by mass spectrometry or thin-layer chromatography. Results are reported as a total sulfatide level and sometimes as individual sulfatide species.

Limitations Established

  • Urine sulfatide levels can be mildly elevated in carriers (this does not indicate disease)
  • The test requires specialized laboratories and may not be available everywhere
  • Results should always be interpreted alongside enzyme testing and genetic testing, not in isolation

Genetic (Molecular) Testing Established

▼

Genetic testing examines the DNA sequence of the ARSA gene to identify the specific mutations causing the disease. This is considered the gold standard for confirming a diagnosis, especially when family mutations are already known.[1][3]

What Genetic Testing Does Established

  • Identifies the specific mutations in the ARSA gene (each person with MLD has two mutations, one from each parent)
  • Distinguishes MLD from pseudodeficiency — the pseudodeficiency variant involves specific, well-characterized DNA changes that are different from disease-causing mutations
  • Helps predict the type of MLD — certain mutations are associated with more or less residual enzyme activity, which correlates with disease severity
  • Enables accurate carrier testing for family members once the mutations are known
  • Makes prenatal testing and PGT possible for future pregnancies

Types of Genetic Testing Established

  • Targeted mutation analysis: Tests for specific known mutations. Fast and accurate when family mutations are already identified.
  • Full gene sequencing: Reads the entire ARSA gene to find any mutations. Used when the family mutations are not yet known.
  • Deletion/duplication analysis: Looks for larger missing or duplicated sections of the gene that standard sequencing might miss.
  • Gene panel testing: Tests ARSA along with other genes that can cause similar symptoms (such as PSAP for saposin B deficiency). Useful when the clinical picture is not yet clear.

When Family Mutations Are Known Established

Once the specific ARSA mutations in your family are identified, genetic testing becomes the most reliable and fastest method for:

  • Testing siblings of the affected child
  • Carrier testing for extended family members (aunts, uncles, cousins)
  • Prenatal testing in future pregnancies
  • Preimplantation genetic testing (PGT) with IVF
Keep your genetic report. Your child’s genetic testing report documents the exact mutations in your family. Keep copies of this report and share it with any healthcare providers, genetic counselors, or family members who need to be tested. It is the single most important document for all future genetic testing in your family.

MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging) Supported

▼

MRI of the brain is a critical tool for both diagnosing MLD and monitoring its progression.[4][6] MRI provides detailed images of the brain’s white matter, where the myelin damage in MLD occurs.

What MRI Shows in MLD Supported

The MRI findings in MLD are quite characteristic and can help distinguish it from other causes of white matter disease:

  • Symmetrical white matter changes: MLD typically produces a symmetrical pattern of abnormal signal in the white matter of both hemispheres of the brain[4]
  • Starts in specific regions: The changes usually begin in the periventricular white matter (the white matter closest to the brain’s fluid-filled ventricles) and the corpus callosum (the band connecting the two brain hemispheres)
  • Characteristic pattern: Often described as a “butterfly” or “tigroid” pattern, with alternating areas of normal and abnormal white matter[4][6]
  • Progresses outward: Over time, the white matter changes expand from the center of the brain outward toward the cortex
  • May show sparing of subcortical fibers early: The nerve fibers closest to the gray matter may appear normal early in the disease, even as deeper white matter is affected

MRI in Different Types of MLD Supported

Type MRI Pattern
Late Infantile Extensive white matter changes, often with brain volume loss (atrophy). Cerebellum often involved. Changes progress rapidly.
Juvenile Similar pattern but may be less extensive at diagnosis. Progression is slower. May show frontal predominance.
Adult Can be subtle at first. May show frontal white matter changes predominantly. Can be mistaken for other conditions.

MRI for Monitoring Supported

Once diagnosed, regular MRI scans (typically every 6–12 months, depending on the situation) are used to track the progression of white matter changes. After treatment, MRI is used to monitor for stabilization or improvement. Children who receive gene therapy may show stabilization or even improvement in their white matter on follow-up MRIs.

Note for parents: MRI is painless but requires the child to lie still for 30–60 minutes. Young children and infants typically need sedation or general anesthesia for the scan. While this can be stressful, MRI is a safe and essential tool for managing MLD. Talk to your child’s team about what to expect and how to prepare.

Nerve Conduction Studies Supported

▼

Nerve conduction studies (NCS), also called nerve conduction velocity (NCV) testing, measure how well electrical signals travel through the peripheral nerves — the nerves outside the brain and spinal cord. Because MLD affects peripheral nerves in addition to the brain’s white matter, these studies are an important part of the diagnostic workup.[4][6]

What They Measure Established

  • Conduction velocity: How fast the electrical signal travels along the nerve. In MLD, demyelination slows this velocity significantly.
  • Signal amplitude: How strong the electrical signal is. Reduced amplitude can indicate nerve damage beyond just myelin loss.
  • Distal latency: How long it takes for the signal to reach the end of the nerve. Prolonged latency indicates demyelination.

Why They Matter in MLD Supported

Peripheral neuropathy is often one of the earliest measurable signs of MLD, sometimes detectable on nerve conduction studies before obvious clinical symptoms appear or before MRI changes become prominent. This makes NCS valuable for:

  • Early detection: In children identified through newborn screening or sibling testing who are still pre-symptomatic, NCS can help assess whether there is already subclinical nerve involvement
  • Baseline assessment: Establishing nerve function before treatment begins, so changes can be tracked
  • Monitoring: Tracking peripheral nerve function over time, both to assess disease progression and treatment response
  • Supporting diagnosis: The presence of demyelinating peripheral neuropathy in a child with white matter changes on MRI strongly supports a diagnosis of MLD (as opposed to some other leukodystrophies that spare peripheral nerves)

What to Expect

During nerve conduction studies, small electrodes are placed on the skin over various nerves (typically in the arms and legs). Brief, mild electrical impulses are applied and the resulting nerve signals are recorded. The test is uncomfortable but not typically painful for older children and adults. For young children, sedation may be considered to ensure accurate results.

The Pseudodeficiency Challenge Supported

▼

Pseudodeficiency deserves special attention in the context of diagnosis because it is the single most common source of confusion, anxiety, and misdiagnosis in MLD testing.

The Problem Established

ARSA pseudodeficiency alleles are carried by 7–15% of the general population.[1] When a person carries one or two pseudodeficiency alleles, their ARSA enzyme activity can be very low — sometimes in the same range as people who actually have MLD. But pseudodeficiency does not cause any disease. The enzyme, while reduced on lab testing, is still sufficient for normal sulfatide metabolism in the body.

How to Distinguish MLD from Pseudodeficiency Established

Feature True MLD Pseudodeficiency
ARSA enzyme activity Very low or absent Very low (overlaps with MLD range)
Urine sulfatides Elevated Normal
Genetic testing Two disease-causing ARSA mutations Pseudodeficiency variant(s) — no disease-causing mutations
MRI White matter changes (if symptomatic) Normal
Clinical symptoms Neurological decline (if symptomatic) None
Nerve conduction Abnormal (often) Normal
The bottom line: If your child has been flagged with low ARSA enzyme activity — whether through clinical testing, newborn screening, or carrier screening — do not assume it is MLD. Request genetic testing, urine sulfatide measurement, and clinical evaluation. There is a reasonable chance it is pseudodeficiency, which requires no treatment. Your medical team should walk you through this process, but understanding the distinction can help reduce anxiety during what may be a stressful wait for results.

Newborn Screening for MLD Established

▼

In December 2025, MLD was added to the Recommended Uniform Screening Panel (RUSP)[17] — the federal list of conditions that all U.S. newborns are recommended to be screened for. This is a landmark moment for the MLD community, as early detection is the key to early treatment and the best possible outcomes.

States Currently Screening Established

Newborn screening in the United States is managed at the state level, so implementation is rolling out gradually. As of early 2026, the following states have implemented or are actively implementing MLD newborn screening:

  • New York — the first state to begin screening (November 2025)
  • Illinois
  • Maryland
  • Minnesota
  • Pennsylvania
  • Utah

More states are expected to adopt MLD screening in the coming months and years as the RUSP recommendation drives implementation nationwide.

How Newborn Screening Works Established

MLD newborn screening uses a three-tier approach based on dried blood spots (the same heel-prick blood sample collected from all newborns within 24–48 hours of birth):

  1. Tier 1 — Sulfatide detection: The dried blood spot is analyzed using LC-MS/MS to measure sulfatide levels. Samples with elevated sulfatides are flagged for further testing.
  2. Tier 2 — ARSA enzyme activity: Flagged samples are tested for ARSA enzyme activity to confirm low enzyme levels, further narrowing down which babies may be affected.
  3. Tier 3 — Genetic confirmation: Babies whose Tier 2 results are concerning are referred for confirmatory testing, which includes genetic sequencing of the ARSA gene, repeat enzyme testing, urine sulfatide levels, and clinical evaluation. This step distinguishes true MLD from pseudodeficiency and carrier status.
Expect false positives. Because pseudodeficiency is relatively common (7–15% of people), the initial screening tier will flag many more babies than actually have MLD. If your newborn is flagged, it does not mean they have MLD. Many flagged babies will turn out to have pseudodeficiency or carrier status. The follow-up tiers are designed to sort this out. While waiting for results can be extremely stressful, know that the system is built to catch true cases while minimizing unnecessary alarm.

Why Newborn Screening Changes Everything Supported

Newborn screening is transformative for MLD because of the treatment timing window. For children with late infantile MLD:

  • Symptoms typically begin between 6 and 30 months
  • Gene therapy (Lenmeldy) has the best outcomes when given before symptoms appear
  • Without newborn screening, most children are diagnosed after symptoms begin — often too late for optimal treatment
  • With newborn screening, affected babies can be identified at birth and treated within the critical pre-symptomatic window

Prenatal Testing Established

▼

For families with a known MLD history (a previously affected child, known carrier status in both parents), prenatal testing can determine whether a pregnancy is affected by MLD.[1][3]

Available Methods Established

Amniocentesis (15–18 weeks gestation) Established

A small sample of amniotic fluid is withdrawn using a needle guided by ultrasound. Testing can include:

  • Sulfatide levels in amniotic fluid: Elevated sulfatides indicate an affected fetus
  • ARSA enzyme activity in cultured amniotic cells: Cells from the amniotic fluid are grown in culture and their ARSA activity is measured
  • Genetic testing: The fetal DNA can be tested for the family’s known ARSA mutations

Chorionic Villus Sampling / CVS (10–13 weeks gestation) Established

A small sample of tissue is taken from the placenta (chorionic villi). This provides fetal DNA earlier in pregnancy than amniocentesis. Testing typically involves:

  • Genetic testing: DNA from the chorionic villi is tested for the family’s known ARSA mutations
  • ARSA enzyme activity: Can be measured in the chorionic villi tissue

Preimplantation Genetic Testing (PGT) Established

Used in conjunction with in vitro fertilization (IVF). A few cells are removed from each embryo at the blastocyst stage (day 5–6) and tested for the family’s known mutations. Only unaffected embryos are transferred for pregnancy. This allows families to have unaffected children without facing the decision of whether to continue an affected pregnancy.

Planning ahead: If you know you are carriers and are planning a future pregnancy, talk to a genetic counselor well in advance. Some testing options (particularly PGT-IVF) require advance preparation, including development of a family-specific genetic probe. Starting these conversations early gives you the most options.

Diagnostic Pathway Summary Established

▼

The path to an MLD diagnosis depends on how the child comes to medical attention. Here are the three most common pathways:

Pathway 1: Clinical Symptoms Established

A child presents with developmental regression or neurological symptoms. The medical team investigates:

  1. Neurological examination and developmental assessment
  2. MRI of the brain — shows white matter changes suggestive of leukodystrophy
  3. ARSA enzyme testing — low or absent activity
  4. Urine sulfatide testing — elevated
  5. Genetic testing — confirms two disease-causing ARSA mutations
  6. Nerve conduction studies — may show demyelinating neuropathy
  7. Diagnosis confirmed. Immediate referral to treatment center.

Pathway 2: Newborn Screening Established

A newborn’s heel-prick blood spot is flagged for low ARSA activity:

  1. Tier 1 screening detects elevated sulfatide levels
  2. Tier 2 testing confirms low ARSA enzyme activity
  3. Tier 3: Genetic testing — confirms two disease-causing mutations
  4. Confirmatory enzyme testing and clinical evaluation
  5. Diagnosis confirmed. Baby is pre-symptomatic. Immediate referral to treatment center — this is the optimal window for gene therapy.

Pathway 3: Sibling Testing Established

An older child is diagnosed with MLD, and siblings are tested:

  1. Targeted genetic testing for the family’s known ARSA mutations
  2. ARSA enzyme activity testing for confirmation
  3. If affected: MRI, nerve conduction studies, and clinical assessment to determine current status
  4. If affected and pre-symptomatic: Immediate referral to treatment center.
Every pathway leads to the same critical action: If your child is diagnosed with MLD and is pre-symptomatic or early symptomatic, contact a treatment center immediately. Call Orchard Assist at 833-672-4278 to begin the process. The earlier treatment begins, the better the outcomes. Do not wait.

Questions to Ask Your Medical Team Supported

▼

If your child has been diagnosed with MLD or testing is underway, here are important questions to discuss with your medical team:

About the Diagnosis

  • What are my child’s specific ARSA gene mutations? Can I get a copy of the genetic report?
  • Has pseudodeficiency been ruled out?
  • What type of MLD does my child have (late infantile, juvenile, or adult)?
  • What is my child’s current disease stage — pre-symptomatic, early symptomatic, or later?
  • What do the MRI and nerve conduction study results show?

About Treatment

  • Is my child eligible for Lenmeldy gene therapy? If not, why, and are there alternatives?
  • How urgently do we need to act? What is the timeline?
  • Which treatment center do you recommend, and how do we get a referral?
  • Should we contact Orchard Assist (833-672-4278) to begin navigating access?

About Family

  • Have all siblings been tested? How quickly can this be done?
  • Should other family members (aunts, uncles, cousins) be tested for carrier status?
  • Can you refer us to a genetic counselor to discuss future family planning?
  • Is our state doing newborn screening for MLD? If so, will future babies be screened automatically?
Bring a notebook. Medical appointments during a new diagnosis can be overwhelming. Write down your questions in advance, bring a notebook or use your phone to take notes, and don’t hesitate to ask the team to repeat or explain anything. You are not expected to absorb everything at once. You are expected to advocate for your child — and asking questions is how you do that.
