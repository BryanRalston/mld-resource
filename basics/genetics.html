The ARSA Gene Established

▼

MLD is caused by mutations in the ARSA gene, which provides the instructions for making the arylsulfatase A enzyme. This gene is located on chromosome 22 at position q13.33 — meaning it sits near the end of the long arm of chromosome 22.

The ARSA gene is relatively small:

  • Size: approximately 3.2–3.5 kilobases (kb) of coding DNA
  • Structure: 8 exons (the portions of the gene that code for the protein)
  • Protein product: a 509 amino acid precursor that is processed into the mature ARSA enzyme

Over 250 different mutations in the ARSA gene have been identified in people with MLD. These mutations include missense mutations (single amino acid changes), nonsense mutations (premature stop signals), splice site mutations (errors in how the gene is read), and small deletions or insertions. The type and combination of mutations a person carries influences how much residual enzyme activity they have, which in turn affects the severity and type of MLD.

Common Mutations

While many different ARSA mutations exist, a few are particularly common in certain populations:

  • c.465+1G>A (also called IVS2+1G>A or allele I) — a splice site mutation that produces no functional enzyme. The most common mutation in European populations with late infantile MLD.
  • p.Pro428Leu (also called allele A) — a missense mutation that produces a small amount of residual enzyme activity. More commonly associated with juvenile or adult onset.

These two mutations account for a significant proportion of MLD alleles in people of European descent. Having two copies of allele I (or two “null” alleles) typically results in late infantile MLD. Having one null allele and one “leaky” allele (like allele A, which produces some enzyme) often results in later onset.

Why specific mutations matter: Knowing your family’s exact ARSA mutations is valuable for several reasons: it helps predict the type and course of MLD, it enables accurate carrier testing for other family members, and it allows for prenatal testing and preimplantation genetic testing (PGT) in future pregnancies. Ask your genetic counselor to ensure your family’s mutations are fully documented.

Saposin B Deficiency (Rare Variant) Established

▼

In rare cases, MLD is caused not by mutations in the ARSA gene itself, but by mutations in the PSAP gene. The PSAP gene encodes a protein called prosaposin, which is cut into four smaller proteins called saposins A, B, C, and D. Saposin B is an activator protein that the ARSA enzyme needs in order to function properly.

Think of it this way: ARSA is the key, and saposin B helps guide the key to the lock. Without saposin B, even a perfectly normal ARSA enzyme cannot do its job. The result is the same: sulfatides cannot be broken down, they accumulate, and the clinical picture is identical to ARSA-deficiency MLD.

Saposin B deficiency accounts for only a very small percentage of MLD cases, but it is important to be aware of because:

  • Standard ARSA enzyme testing may show normal enzyme levels, which can lead to a missed diagnosis
  • The PSAP gene is on chromosome 10 (not chromosome 22), so it follows a separate set of mutations
  • Genetic testing of the PSAP gene is needed to confirm this variant
If enzyme levels are normal but MLD is suspected: In the rare situation where a child has symptoms consistent with MLD, elevated urine sulfatides, but normal ARSA enzyme levels, saposin B deficiency should be investigated. Tell your medical team about this possibility. Genetic testing of the PSAP gene can confirm or rule it out.

How MLD Is Inherited Established

▼

MLD follows an autosomal recessive inheritance pattern. Let’s break that down:

  • Autosomal means the ARSA gene is on one of the 22 numbered chromosomes (specifically chromosome 22), not on the X or Y sex chromosomes. This means MLD affects boys and girls equally.
  • Recessive means a person must inherit two defective copies of the gene — one from each parent — to develop the disease. One working copy is enough to produce sufficient ARSA enzyme for normal health.

What This Means for Your Family

If your child has MLD, it means that both you and your partner are carriers. As carriers, you each have one working copy and one mutated copy of the ARSA gene. You are healthy because one working copy produces enough enzyme. But when both of you pass along your mutated copy, your child receives no working copies and develops MLD.

The Probabilities for Each Pregnancy

When both parents are carriers, each pregnancy has the following chances:

  • 25% (1 in 4) — the child inherits two working copies (AA). They are unaffected and not a carrier.
  • 50% (2 in 4) — the child inherits one working copy and one mutated copy (Aa). They are a carrier, like the parents — completely healthy, but carrying one mutated gene.
  • 25% (1 in 4) — the child inherits two mutated copies (aa). They are affected with MLD.
Each pregnancy is independent. If you already have one child with MLD, the chance for each subsequent pregnancy is still 25% — not higher and not lower. Having one affected child does not “use up” the probability. The 25% chance applies fresh to every pregnancy. This is why testing all siblings is essential, regardless of whether they appear healthy.

Pseudodeficiency: A Critical Complication Established

▼

One of the most confusing aspects of MLD genetics is the existence of ARSA pseudodeficiency. This is a common genetic variant (not a disease-causing mutation) that causes ARSA enzyme activity to be very low — sometimes in the range seen in MLD patients — in a person who is completely healthy and will never develop MLD.

Pseudodeficiency alleles are remarkably common:

  • Carried by approximately 7–15% of the general population (depending on ethnicity)
  • ARSA enzyme activity may be reduced to 5–20% of normal — a range that overlaps with true MLD
  • People with pseudodeficiency have no sulfatide accumulation and no myelin damage because the enzyme, while reduced, is still sufficient for normal function

Why This Matters

Pseudodeficiency creates several important challenges:

  • Enzyme testing alone can’t distinguish MLD from pseudodeficiency. A blood test showing low ARSA activity could mean MLD, carrier status combined with pseudodeficiency, or pure pseudodeficiency. This is why genetic (DNA) testing is essential.
  • Carrier testing becomes complicated. If a person is both a carrier for MLD and has pseudodeficiency, their enzyme levels may look like they have MLD even though they’re healthy.
  • Newborn screening may flag pseudodeficiency. As MLD is added to newborn screening panels, some babies with pseudodeficiency will initially test positive, requiring follow-up genetic testing to distinguish them from truly affected infants.
Bottom line: Enzyme testing is an important first step, but it must always be followed by genetic (DNA) testing to confirm a diagnosis of MLD. If your child has been identified as having low ARSA activity, do not panic — it could be pseudodeficiency. Your medical team will order genetic testing to clarify. This is a standard part of the diagnostic process.

Populations with Higher Prevalence Supported

▼

While MLD occurs in all ethnic groups worldwide, certain populations have a significantly higher prevalence due to founder effects — situations where a small ancestral population happened to carry the mutation, and as that population grew, the mutation became more common in their descendants.

Population Estimated Prevalence Compared to General Population
Habbanite Jewish community ~1 in 75 500–2,000x higher
Western Navajo ~1 in 2,500 16–64x higher
Certain Arab populations in Israel ~1 in 8,000 5–20x higher
General population 1 in 40,000–160,000 (baseline)

The carrier frequency in the general population is estimated at approximately 1 in 100 to 1 in 200. In higher-prevalence populations, the carrier frequency is correspondingly higher. For example, in the Habbanite Jewish community, a substantial portion of the population carries at least one mutated ARSA allele.

If you belong to one of these communities, or if there is a family history of neurological disease, genetic counseling may be especially valuable for family planning.

What This Means for Your Family Established

▼

Once your family’s specific ARSA mutations are identified through genetic testing, several important doors open:

Sibling Testing

Every sibling of a child with MLD has a 25% chance of being affected and a 50% chance of being a carrier. Testing siblings is urgent because:

  • An affected sibling who is still pre-symptomatic could be eligible for Lenmeldy gene therapy, which has the best outcomes when given before symptoms appear
  • Early detection can mean the difference between a child who walks, talks, and thrives versus one who does not
  • Even if a sibling is a healthy carrier, knowing their status is important for their future family planning

Carrier Testing for Extended Family

Once the family’s mutations are known, reliable carrier testing becomes available for:

  • Aunts, uncles, and cousins of the affected child
  • Grandparents (to determine which side of the family each mutation came from)
  • The partners of known carriers, to assess risk for their future children

Carrier testing for known family mutations is straightforward, accurate, and typically covered by insurance when there is a documented family history.

Future Pregnancies

For parents who already have a child with MLD and are considering future pregnancies, several options exist:

  • Prenatal testing — testing during pregnancy using amniocentesis (typically at 15–18 weeks) or chorionic villus sampling/CVS (typically at 10–13 weeks). Amniocentesis can measure sulfatide levels in amniotic fluid and ARSA enzyme activity in cultured fetal cells. CVS can provide earlier results through genetic testing of the placental tissue.
  • Preimplantation genetic testing (PGT) — used with in vitro fertilization (IVF). Embryos are tested for the family’s known ARSA mutations before being transferred. Only unaffected embryos (either non-carrier or carrier) are implanted. This allows families to have unaffected children without facing the decision of terminating an affected pregnancy.
  • Natural conception with early postnatal testing — conceiving naturally and testing the baby immediately after birth (or even through newborn screening, where available). If the baby is affected, early treatment can begin before symptoms appear.
Genetic counseling is essential. A genetic counselor specializing in metabolic diseases can help you understand your family’s specific situation, explain all available options, coordinate testing for family members, and provide emotional support as you navigate these decisions. Ask your child’s treatment center for a referral, or contact the National Society of Genetic Counselors to find a counselor near you.

Understanding Carrier Status Established

▼

Being a carrier of MLD means having one working copy and one mutated copy of the ARSA gene. Carriers:

  • Are completely healthy — they do not develop MLD and never will
  • May have slightly lower ARSA enzyme levels than non-carriers, but well within the range needed for normal function
  • Can pass the mutated gene to their children — this only matters if their partner is also a carrier of the same gene
  • Usually have no idea they are carriers until a child is diagnosed or they undergo genetic testing

If you are a carrier, the risk to your future children depends entirely on your partner’s status:

Your Status Partner’s Status Risk to Each Child
Carrier Carrier 25% affected, 50% carrier, 25% non-carrier
Carrier Not a carrier 0% affected, 50% carrier, 50% non-carrier
Carrier Unknown Very low risk (partner would need to be among the ~1 in 100–200 carriers in the general population)

This is why partner testing is valuable for known carriers who are planning families. If your partner is not a carrier, your children cannot develop MLD (though they may still be carriers).

This Is Not Your Fault

▼

If you are reading this as a parent of a child with MLD, you may be experiencing guilt. This is a nearly universal response, and it is important to hear clearly: you did nothing wrong.

You did not cause MLD. You did not know you were carriers. In most cases, there is no family history, no warning sign, and no way you could have known. Carrier status for rare recessive diseases is extraordinarily common — every person carries mutations in several genes that could cause disease if their partner happens to carry mutations in the same genes. This is a fundamental fact of human genetics, not a flaw.

Your energy is better spent on what you can do now: getting your child the best possible care, testing siblings, connecting with support organizations, and making informed decisions about the future. Those are the things that make a real difference.

You are your child’s greatest advocate. The fact that you are here, learning about this disease, understanding the genetics, and exploring every option — that is exactly what your child needs from you. The science is better than it has ever been. The support networks are real and active. And you are not alone in this.
