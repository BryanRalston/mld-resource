Seizures & Pain Management

Seizures in MLD

▼

Seizures occur in approximately 14–39% of patients with MLD, depending on the disease type and stage. While not every child with MLD will experience seizures, they are common enough that families should understand what to watch for and how they are managed.

Seizures in MLD result from the progressive damage to the brain’s white matter. As demyelination disrupts the normal electrical signaling pathways in the brain, abnormal electrical discharges can occur, triggering seizures.

Types of Seizures in MLD

Focal seizures predominate in MLD, meaning they start in one area of the brain rather than involving the entire brain simultaneously. Focal seizures may look different depending on which brain region is affected:

  • Focal motor seizures — rhythmic jerking or stiffening of one arm, one leg, or one side of the body
  • Focal seizures with impaired awareness — your child may stare, become unresponsive, or make repetitive movements (lip smacking, hand rubbing) without being aware of their surroundings
  • Focal to bilateral tonic-clonic — a seizure that starts in one area and then spreads to involve the whole body, with stiffening followed by rhythmic jerking

Generalized seizures (involving the whole brain from the start) can also occur but are less typical.

What a Seizure Looks Like in a Young Child

Seizures can be frightening to witness, especially for the first time. In a young child with MLD, a seizure may present as:

  • Sudden stiffening of the body or a limb
  • Rhythmic jerking movements of the arms, legs, or face
  • Eyes rolling upward or to one side
  • Sudden loss of muscle tone (going limp)
  • Staring blankly and not responding to your voice or touch
  • Lip smacking, chewing movements, or other repetitive behaviors
  • Brief pauses in breathing, sometimes with a bluish tint around the lips
  • Loss of bladder or bowel control

After a seizure (the “postictal” period), your child may be very sleepy, confused, or irritable for minutes to hours. This is normal and expected.

EEG Findings

An electroencephalogram (EEG) records the electrical activity of the brain. In MLD, the EEG typically shows:

  • Background slowing — the overall electrical rhythm of the brain is slower than normal, reflecting widespread white matter disease
  • Focal or multifocal discharges — abnormal spikes or sharp waves arising from one or multiple brain regions

Your neurologist may order an EEG if seizures are suspected, to confirm the diagnosis, identify the seizure type, and guide medication choices.

Seizure Management

▼

The good news is that seizures in MLD generally respond well to standard anti-epileptic medications. Your neurologist will choose medications based on your child’s seizure type, age, other medications, and overall health.

Commonly used anti-epileptic medications in MLD include levetiracetam (Keppra), lamotrigine (Lamictal), oxcarbazepine (Trileptal), and valproic acid (Depakote), among others. Your neurologist will monitor for side effects and adjust doses as needed.

When to Call 911

Call emergency services immediately if:

  • A seizure lasts longer than 5 minutes
  • Your child is not breathing or turns blue
  • This is your child’s first seizure
  • Your child is injured during the seizure
  • A second seizure follows without your child fully regaining consciousness
  • Your child does not return to their usual state within 30 minutes after the seizure

Seizure First Aid

  • Stay calm — note the time the seizure starts
  • Keep your child safe — move away from hard or sharp objects, cushion their head
  • Turn them on their side (recovery position) to prevent choking
  • Do NOT put anything in their mouth, restrain their movements, or try to hold them down
  • Time the seizure — this information is critical for your medical team
  • Record it on video if possible — this helps your neurologist identify the seizure type
  • Administer rescue medication if one has been prescribed and the seizure meets the criteria your neurologist specified
Ask about a seizure action plan. Your neurologist should provide you with a written seizure action plan that specifies when to administer rescue medication, when to call 911, and what information to bring to the emergency department. Share this plan with all caregivers, babysitters, and your child’s school.

Sources of Pain in MLD

▼

Pain is a significant concern in MLD and can come from multiple sources. Understanding where pain may be originating helps your medical team target treatment effectively.

Spasticity

Spasticity — increased muscle stiffness and tightness — is one of the most common and distressing symptoms of MLD. It occurs because the brain loses the ability to properly regulate muscle tone as white matter is damaged. Spasticity can cause:

  • Constant muscle tension and discomfort
  • Painful muscle spasms (sudden, involuntary contractions)
  • Joint pain from muscles pulling joints into abnormal positions
  • Difficulty with positioning, dressing, and hygiene
  • Sleep disruption from nocturnal spasms

Neuropathic Pain

Neuropathic pain arises from damage to the peripheral nerves themselves. It is a direct consequence of demyelination in the peripheral nervous system and can be severe. Neuropathic pain may feel like:

  • Burning, tingling, or “pins and needles” sensations
  • Shooting or electric shock-like pain
  • Hypersensitivity to touch (allodynia) — even gentle touching causes pain
  • Deep, aching pain in the limbs

Neuropathic pain can be particularly difficult to manage because it does not respond well to standard pain relievers like acetaminophen or ibuprofen. It requires specific medications that target nerve pain pathways.

GI Dysmotility

Stomach and intestinal discomfort can result from autonomic nervous system dysfunction in MLD. The nerves that control gut movement are affected, leading to:

  • Constipation (sometimes severe)
  • Abdominal bloating and cramping
  • Gastroesophageal reflux (GERD)
  • Nausea

Positioning-Related Pain

Children who spend extended periods in one position (due to limited mobility) can develop pain from:

  • Pressure points — sustained pressure on bony prominences
  • Joint stiffness and contractures from lack of movement
  • Muscle fatigue from compensatory postures

Skin Breakdown Near Orthotics

Braces, splints, and orthotics can cause skin irritation, pressure sores, and pain if they do not fit properly. Regular skin checks are essential — look for redness, blistering, or raw areas every time you remove an orthotic. Report any skin changes to your orthotist immediately for adjustment.

Pain Management Approaches

▼

Effective pain management in MLD usually requires a combination of approaches tailored to the specific source of pain.

Managing Spasticity

Approach How It Works
Baclofen (oral) A muscle relaxant that reduces spasticity by acting on the spinal cord. Commonly the first-line medication. Dose is gradually increased to find the right balance between reducing stiffness and maintaining function.
Intrathecal baclofen pump For severe spasticity, a small pump is surgically implanted under the skin and delivers baclofen directly into the spinal fluid. This allows much lower doses with fewer side effects and more effective relief.
Botulinum toxin (Botox) injections Targeted injections into specific muscles to temporarily reduce spasticity in those areas. Effects last 3–6 months. Particularly useful for isolated problem areas.
Stretching and range-of-motion exercises Daily stretching helps maintain flexibility and reduce muscle tightness. Your physical therapist will design an appropriate routine.
Positioning and seating Proper positioning in wheelchair, bed, and other settings reduces spasticity triggers and improves comfort.
Warm baths and heat Warmth can temporarily relax tight muscles and provide comfort.

Managing Neuropathic Pain

  • Gabapentin (Neurontin) — an anti-seizure medication that is also highly effective for nerve pain. Often the first choice for neuropathic pain in children.
  • Pregabalin (Lyrica) — similar to gabapentin with a slightly different pharmacological profile. May be tried if gabapentin is insufficient.
  • Duloxetine or amitriptyline — antidepressant medications that also modulate pain pathways. May be used in combination with other agents.
  • Gentle touch and desensitization techniques — for children with allodynia, gradual exposure to touch may help reduce hypersensitivity over time.

Managing GI Pain

  • Dietary modifications — increased fiber and fluids for constipation, smaller and more frequent meals for reflux
  • Motility agents — medications that help the gut move contents along more effectively
  • Acid-reducing medications — proton pump inhibitors (PPIs) or H2 blockers for reflux
  • Stool softeners and laxatives — for constipation management
  • Abdominal massage — gentle massage techniques can help stimulate gut movement

General Comfort Measures

  • Positioning — regular repositioning (at least every 2 hours) to prevent pressure-related pain
  • Temperature regulation — many children with MLD have difficulty regulating body temperature; maintaining a comfortable environment helps overall comfort
  • Gentle massage — light, soothing touch can reduce anxiety and promote relaxation
  • Music and sensory comfort — familiar sounds, gentle music, and soft lighting can create a calming environment
  • Consistent routines — predictability can reduce stress and anxiety, which can worsen pain perception

Recognizing Pain in Your Child

▼
Children with MLD may not be able to communicate pain verbally. As the disease progresses and affects speech and cognition, your child may lose the ability to say “I hurt” or point to where it hurts. Learning to read behavioral and physiological cues is essential.

Signs that your child may be in pain include:

  • Facial expressions — grimacing, furrowed brow, clenched jaw, frowning
  • Vocalizations — crying, moaning, whimpering, screaming, or changes in typical vocal patterns
  • Body movements — guarding (protecting a body part), tensing, arching, pulling away from touch, restlessness
  • Behavioral changes — increased irritability, withdrawal, sleep disruption, decreased appetite, inconsolable crying
  • Vital sign changes — increased heart rate, rapid breathing, sweating
  • Changes in tone — increased spasticity or rigidity can be a sign of pain or discomfort

Validated pain assessment tools designed for non-verbal children, such as the FLACC scale (Face, Legs, Activity, Cry, Consolability) or the r-FLACC (revised FLACC for children with cognitive impairment), can help you and your medical team systematically evaluate pain. Ask your palliative care team about using these tools at home.

Any sudden change in your child’s comfort level or behavior should prompt a full medical evaluation. Pain in children with MLD can come from expected sources (spasticity, neuropathy) but can also signal new problems (infection, fracture, dental issues, reflux flare) that need specific treatment.
