Why Feeding Matters in MLD Established

▼

Swallowing difficulties (dysphagia) are one of the most significant medical concerns as MLD progresses. The nerves and muscles involved in chewing, swallowing, and protecting the airway are controlled by the brainstem and cranial nerves, which are affected by demyelination. As these nerves lose their myelin coating, the carefully coordinated sequence of swallowing becomes impaired.

Safe, adequate nutrition is essential for your child’s health, growth, immune function, and overall quality of life. Addressing feeding challenges proactively — rather than waiting for a crisis — gives your family more time to make informed decisions and helps prevent serious complications like aspiration pneumonia.

A feeding therapist (SLP) is an essential team member. A speech-language pathologist with expertise in swallowing should be part of your care team from early on. Ask for a referral before problems arise, so you have a relationship and a baseline established when concerns develop.

Signs of Swallowing Difficulty Established

▼

Swallowing problems may develop gradually. Being aware of the warning signs allows you to report them to your medical team early. Watch for:

  • Coughing or choking during meals — this may indicate food or liquid is entering the airway (aspiration)
  • Wet, gurgly, or “bubbly” voice after eating or drinking — suggests liquid is pooling near the vocal cords
  • Prolonged feeding times — meals that used to take 20 minutes now take 45 minutes or longer
  • Food refusal or decreased interest in eating — may indicate discomfort or difficulty with swallowing
  • Weight loss or poor weight gain — not getting enough calories from oral intake
  • Drooling — difficulty managing saliva can signal declining oral motor control
  • Recurrent respiratory infections — may indicate silent aspiration (food or liquid entering the lungs without visible coughing)
  • Food or liquid coming out of the nose — suggests impaired coordination of the soft palate during swallowing
  • Pocketing food — food remaining in the cheeks rather than being swallowed
Any sudden change in feeding ability should prompt immediate medical evaluation. A rapid decline in swallowing may indicate disease progression, an acute illness, or a new complication. Do not wait for the next scheduled appointment — call your medical team right away.

Swallowing Assessment Established

▼

When swallowing concerns arise, your medical team will likely recommend a formal assessment to understand exactly what is happening during the swallowing process.

Video Fluoroscopic Swallow Study (VFSS)

The VFSS, also called a modified barium swallow study, is the gold standard for evaluating swallowing in children with neurological conditions. During this test:

  • Your child eats and drinks foods mixed with a contrast material (barium) that shows up on X-ray
  • A real-time X-ray video shows exactly how food and liquid move through the mouth, throat, and into the esophagus
  • The speech-language pathologist and radiologist observe whether food or liquid enters the airway (aspiration)
  • Different food textures and liquid thicknesses are tested to determine which are safest

The results guide specific recommendations about which food textures and liquid consistencies are safe for your child to eat by mouth.

Fiberoptic Endoscopic Evaluation of Swallowing (FEES)

An alternative to VFSS, FEES uses a thin, flexible camera passed through the nose to directly visualize the throat during swallowing. It does not involve radiation and can be done at the bedside, making it a good option for children who cannot travel easily to radiology.

Clinical Swallowing Evaluation

Before instrumental studies, your SLP will conduct a clinical evaluation — observing your child eating and drinking, assessing oral motor function, and noting any signs of difficulty. This evaluation determines whether instrumental testing is needed and helps guide ongoing management.

Diet Modifications Supported

▼

Based on swallowing assessment results, your SLP and medical team may recommend modifications to make eating safer:

Texture Changes

  • Pureed foods — smooth, uniform consistency that requires minimal chewing (like applesauce or mashed potatoes)
  • Minced and moist foods — very small, soft pieces in moisture (like well-cooked pasta in sauce)
  • Soft, bite-sized foods — soft foods cut small enough to reduce choking risk
  • Avoiding mixed textures — foods that combine liquid and solid (like cereal in milk or chunky soup) can be particularly challenging for impaired swallowing

Thickened Liquids

Thin liquids (water, juice, milk) are often the hardest consistency to swallow safely because they move fast and are difficult to control. Thickened liquids move more slowly, giving the swallowing muscles more time to coordinate. Your SLP will recommend the appropriate thickness level:

  • Slightly thick — thicker than water but still pourable
  • Mildly thick — like a smoothie consistency
  • Moderately thick — like a thick milkshake
  • Extremely thick — pudding-like, holds its shape

Commercial thickeners are available that can be added to any liquid without significantly altering the taste.

Positioning During Feeds

  • Keep your child upright at 90 degrees during and for at least 30 minutes after eating
  • Use appropriate seating with adequate head and trunk support
  • A slight chin tuck (looking slightly downward) during swallowing can help protect the airway — your SLP will guide you on whether this is appropriate for your child
  • Reduce distractions during meals to help your child focus on the eating process

Gastrostomy Tube (G-Tube) Established

▼

A gastrostomy tube — commonly called a G-tube — is a small tube placed directly through the abdominal wall into the stomach. It allows liquid nutrition, medications, and fluids to be delivered directly to the stomach, bypassing the mouth and throat entirely.

When a G-Tube Is Recommended

A G-tube is typically recommended when:

  • Swallowing becomes unsafe — aspiration (food or liquid entering the lungs) poses a serious risk of pneumonia
  • Oral intake is insufficient — your child cannot take in enough calories and fluids by mouth to maintain growth and hydration
  • Meals have become stressful or exhausting — taking so long that they dominate daily life and leave no energy for other activities
  • Neurological decline has begun affecting the brain regions that control swallowing
A G-tube is not a sign of “giving up.” This is one of the most important things to understand. A G-tube is a tool that ensures your child gets the nutrition and hydration they need to stay healthy, grow, and feel well. It prevents aspiration pneumonia — one of the most dangerous complications of swallowing difficulty. Many families report that a G-tube improved their child’s quality of life by removing the stress and danger from mealtimes.

Oral Feeding Alongside a G-Tube

Having a G-tube does not mean your child can never eat by mouth again. Many children with G-tubes continue to enjoy some oral feeding for pleasure — small tastes of favorite foods, as long as it can be done safely. Your SLP and medical team will help you determine what level of oral feeding is appropriate.

The G-tube handles the “job” of nutrition, which frees mealtimes from the pressure of getting enough calories in. What remains can simply be about enjoyment, connection, and family time around the table.

The G-Tube Procedure

G-tube placement is a common, well-established procedure typically performed by a pediatric surgeon or gastroenterologist. It is usually done under general anesthesia and takes 30 to 60 minutes. Most children go home the same day or the following day. Your medical team will teach you how to care for the tube site, administer feedings, and troubleshoot common issues.

Timing Matters

Many families and medical teams recommend placing a G-tube before it becomes urgent. Having the G-tube in place while your child is still eating well gives you time to learn the system without pressure, and it is ready when you need it. Discuss timing with your care team so the decision is made thoughtfully, not in a crisis.

Aspiration Pneumonia Prevention Established

▼

Aspiration pneumonia is the biggest feeding-related risk for children with MLD. It occurs when food, liquid, saliva, or stomach contents enter the lungs, introducing bacteria and causing infection. Aspiration pneumonia can be serious and life-threatening.

Prevention strategies include:

  • Follow swallowing study recommendations — stick to the food textures and liquid thicknesses your SLP has recommended
  • Proper positioning during and after meals
  • Good oral hygiene — keeping the mouth clean reduces bacteria available to enter the lungs
  • Timely G-tube placement when oral feeding becomes unsafe
  • Managing reflux — if your child has gastroesophageal reflux (GERD), treating it reduces the risk of stomach contents entering the lungs
  • Saliva management — for children who cannot manage their own saliva, medications (like glycopyrrolate) can reduce saliva production

Signs of aspiration pneumonia include fever, rapid or labored breathing, wet-sounding cough, irritability, and decreased feeding. If you notice these signs, contact your medical team immediately.

GI Issues and Nutrition Goals Supported

▼

Constipation and GI Dysmotility

Constipation is very common in children with MLD, caused by a combination of factors:

  • Reduced physical activity and mobility
  • Autonomic nervous system dysfunction (the nerves that control gut movement are affected)
  • Medications (some anti-epileptic drugs and pain medications can cause constipation)
  • Inadequate fluid intake
  • Dietary changes (especially if transitioning to tube feeding)

Management may include increased fluids, fiber supplementation, stool softeners, or motility agents. Your gastroenterologist will tailor the approach to your child’s needs. Untreated constipation can cause significant discomfort, poor appetite, and even vomiting, so it is important to address it proactively.

Nutrition Goals

Regardless of whether your child is eating by mouth or receiving tube feedings, the nutrition goals are the same:

  • Adequate calories to maintain growth (or appropriate weight for non-growing children)
  • Adequate hydration — dehydration can happen quickly, especially if swallowing is difficult
  • Vitamin and mineral supplementation — vitamin D, calcium, and other nutrients may need supplementation, especially for children with limited mobility and sun exposure
  • Close monitoring of height, weight, and growth curves — plotted at every medical visit to track trends over time

A registered dietitian with experience in pediatric complex medical care can be a valuable addition to your care team, helping to optimize your child’s nutrition whether by mouth or by tube.

Weight monitoring goes both ways. While inadequate nutrition is a common concern, some children on tube feedings may gain too much weight if caloric intake is not carefully managed. Your dietitian and gastroenterologist will help calibrate the right amount to keep your child healthy and comfortable.
