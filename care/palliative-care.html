What Palliative Care Really Is Established

▼

Palliative care is NOT the same as hospice or end-of-life care. This is the single most important misconception to correct. Palliative care is a specialized medical approach focused on providing relief from symptoms, reducing stress, and improving quality of life for both the child and the family — at every stage of a serious illness, including from the very beginning.

Palliative care works alongside curative and disease-modifying treatments, not instead of them. A child receiving gene therapy, stem cell transplant, or active medical management absolutely benefits from palliative care at the same time. The two are complementary, not contradictory.

Think of palliative care as an extra layer of support that wraps around your entire family — managing symptoms that may not be adequately addressed by individual specialists, providing emotional and spiritual support, and helping you navigate the difficult decisions that come with a complex medical journey.

Many treated children are thriving. Palliative care supports the best possible quality of life at every stage — including for children who are doing well after treatment. It is not a sign that things are getting worse. It is a sign that your medical team is committed to your child’s total well-being, not just disease management.

What Palliative Care Provides Supported

▼

Symptom Management

Palliative care specialists are experts in managing the symptoms that affect daily life in MLD:

  • Pain — developing and adjusting pain management plans, including for neuropathic pain, spasticity, and GI discomfort
  • Spasticity — coordinating with neurology and rehabilitation for optimal spasticity management
  • GI issues — addressing constipation, reflux, nausea, and feeding discomfort
  • Seizures — collaborating with neurology on seizure control and rescue medication plans
  • Sleep — addressing sleep disruption, which is common and exhausting for the whole family
  • Agitation and irritability — identifying and treating causes, using both medication and environmental approaches
  • Secretion management — helping manage excess saliva and respiratory secretions

Because palliative care specialists see the whole picture, they can often identify connections between symptoms that individual specialists might miss — for example, recognizing that increased irritability is actually being caused by uncontrolled reflux rather than disease progression.

Emotional and Spiritual Support

Palliative care teams include social workers, chaplains, child life specialists, and psychologists who support the emotional and spiritual needs of your entire family:

  • Processing grief, fear, anger, and uncertainty
  • Supporting your marriage or partnership under extreme stress
  • Helping you find meaning and connection during a difficult journey
  • Spiritual care that respects your family’s beliefs and traditions
  • Connecting you with community resources and peer support

Sibling Support

Siblings of children with serious illnesses face unique emotional challenges. Palliative care teams can provide:

  • Age-appropriate explanations of their brother’s or sister’s condition
  • A safe space to express their own fears, frustrations, and sadness
  • Help understanding that changes in family routine are not their fault
  • Connection with sibling support groups or camps
  • Strategies for maintaining normalcy and joy in their own lives

Goals-of-Care Conversations

Palliative care specialists are skilled at facilitating thoughtful conversations about your goals and values for your child’s care. These conversations are not about “giving up” — they are about making sure your child’s medical care reflects what matters most to your family. Topics might include:

  • What does a good day look like for your child?
  • What are your hopes and fears?
  • What would your child want if they could tell you?
  • How do you want to balance treatment intensity with quality of life?

Care Coordination

With so many specialists involved in MLD care, the palliative care team can serve as an integrating force — helping ensure that all providers are aligned, that medications do not conflict, and that the overall plan makes sense as a unified whole.

Respite Care

Caregiving for a child with MLD is physically and emotionally exhausting. Palliative care teams can connect you with respite care services — trained caregivers who can provide temporary relief so you can rest, attend to your own health, spend time with your other children, or simply take a break. Asking for respite is not weakness. It is essential for sustainable caregiving.

Respiratory Care Supported

▼

Respiratory issues can develop as MLD progresses, particularly due to weakened swallowing reflexes, reduced cough strength, central hypotonia (reduced muscle tone affecting breathing muscles), and aspiration. Proactive respiratory management is an important component of supportive care.

When to Involve Pulmonology

A referral to pulmonology (lung specialist) is recommended when:

  • Swallowing concerns arise (increased aspiration risk also means increased respiratory risk)
  • Central hypotonia develops (weak trunk and breathing muscles)
  • Your child has recurrent respiratory infections or pneumonia
  • Snoring, pauses in breathing during sleep, or other signs of sleep-disordered breathing appear
  • Oxygen levels are lower than expected

Sleep Evaluation

Sleep apnea (pauses in breathing during sleep) can develop due to both central (brain-related) and obstructive (airway-related) causes. A formal sleep study (polysomnogram) can identify whether treatment is needed. Options may include:

  • Positional adjustments during sleep
  • Supplemental oxygen if oxygen levels drop during sleep
  • Non-invasive ventilation (BiPAP or CPAP) to support breathing during sleep

Airway Management

  • Suctioning — for children who have difficulty managing secretions, portable suction machines can clear the mouth and throat
  • Chest physiotherapy — techniques to help mobilize and clear secretions from the lungs
  • Cough assist devices — mechanical devices that simulate a strong cough to clear airway secretions
  • Humidification — moist air can help keep secretions thin and easier to clear

Temperature and Skin Care Supported

▼

Temperature Dysregulation

Temperature dysregulation is common in advanced MLD. The autonomic nervous system, which controls body temperature along with many other involuntary functions, can be affected by demyelination. Your child may:

  • Run fevers without an identifiable infection (“central fevers”)
  • Have difficulty maintaining normal body temperature in warm or cold environments
  • Sweat excessively or not sweat at all
  • Have cold hands and feet due to poor circulation

Management includes:

  • Maintaining a comfortable, stable room temperature
  • Using layered clothing that can be adjusted easily
  • Monitoring temperature regularly so you can distinguish central fevers from infection
  • Working with your medical team to develop guidelines for when a fever needs medical evaluation versus environmental management

Skin Care

Vigilant skin monitoring is essential, particularly for children with limited mobility. Skin breakdown can occur quickly and cause significant pain and infection risk.

Areas that need close attention:

  • Under orthotics and braces — check every time you remove them; look for redness that does not resolve within 20 minutes
  • Bony prominences — hips, heels, elbows, shoulder blades, and the base of the spine are high-risk areas for pressure injuries
  • Areas affected by incontinence — prolonged exposure to moisture from urine or stool breaks down skin quickly
  • Skin folds — neck, groin, and underarms where moisture can collect

Prevention strategies:

  • Regular repositioning (every 2 hours minimum)
  • Pressure-relieving surfaces (specialized mattresses, wheelchair cushions)
  • Barrier creams for areas at risk from moisture exposure
  • Properly fitted equipment and orthotics
  • Good nutrition and hydration (healthy skin heals better)
  • Gentle cleaning and thorough drying during bathing and diaper changes

Advance Care Planning Established

▼

Advance care planning is the process of thinking about, discussing, and documenting your wishes for your child’s medical care — especially for situations where decisions may need to be made quickly. These are difficult conversations, but having them early, while they feel less urgent, is far better than making these decisions during a crisis.

Advance Directives

An advance directive is a legal document that outlines your preferences for your child’s medical care. For pediatric patients, parents typically make these decisions on behalf of their child. Components may include:

  • Goals of care — what is most important to your family (comfort, function, life extension, or some combination)
  • Specific medical interventions — your preferences regarding CPR, mechanical ventilation, hospital transfer, and other interventions
  • Location of care — whether you prefer home-based care, hospital care, or a combination
  • Decision-makers — who should make medical decisions if you are unable to

Goals-of-Care Conversations

Your palliative care team can help facilitate these conversations in a supportive, non-judgmental environment. Key principles:

  • These conversations are ongoing — your goals may change as your child’s condition evolves. Advance care plans can be updated at any time.
  • There are no wrong answers — your family’s values and beliefs should guide these decisions.
  • Both parents/guardians should participate — making sure you are aligned reduces conflict during difficult moments.
  • Include trusted family and friends if you wish — your support network can provide perspective and emotional support.
  • Document your decisions and share them with all members of your medical team so your wishes are known and respected.
Planning is not giving up. Advance care planning is an act of love. It ensures that your child’s care reflects your family’s values and that decisions are made thoughtfully, not reactively. Many families find that having these conversations actually reduces anxiety because the uncertainty of “what would we do if...” is replaced with a clear, shared understanding.

Quality of Life Supported

▼

At every stage of MLD — from diagnosis through treatment and beyond — every child deserves comfort, dignity, and moments of joy. Quality of life is not determined by what abilities your child has or has lost. It is about ensuring that their daily experience includes safety, comfort, connection, and happiness.

Practical ways to support quality of life:

  • Sensory experiences — music, gentle touch, familiar voices, nature sounds, soft textures, warm baths
  • Social connection — time with family, siblings, friends, and pets. Your child benefits from being included in family life, not isolated from it.
  • Comfort and positioning — making sure your child is physically comfortable throughout the day and night
  • Maintaining routines — predictable daily rhythms provide security and familiarity
  • Going outside — fresh air, sunshine, and a change of scenery matter, even if your child cannot actively participate in outdoor activities
  • Celebrations and milestones — birthdays, holidays, and everyday moments of joy are for every child
  • Communication — even when verbal communication is not possible, your child is aware of your presence, your voice, and your love. Keep talking, singing, and reading to them.

The MLD community is full of families who have found profound meaning, connection, and even beauty in their journey — not despite the challenges, but in the way they have met them. You are not alone in this, and there are families who understand exactly what you are going through. Reach out to the organizations listed in our Resources section to connect with them.

Accessing Palliative Care Established

▼

Most children’s hospitals have a pediatric palliative care team. To access these services:

  • Ask your neurologist or primary care physician for a palliative care referral — you do not need to wait until your child is severely ill
  • Contact your hospital’s palliative care department directly — many accept self-referrals from families
  • Ask your social worker to help arrange a consultation

If your hospital does not have a dedicated pediatric palliative care team, organizations like the Center to Advance Palliative Care (CAPC) can help you find palliative care resources in your area.

Insurance typically covers palliative care services. Under the Affordable Care Act’s concurrent care provision, children on Medicaid can receive both curative treatment and hospice services simultaneously — meaning you never have to choose between treatment and comfort.

Ask early. The earlier you integrate palliative care into your child’s team, the more benefit your family receives. Palliative care specialists who have known your child and family over time can provide much more personalized and effective support than those meeting you for the first time during a crisis.
