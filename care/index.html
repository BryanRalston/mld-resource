Care Team Overview

Why a Multidisciplinary Team Matters

▼

MLD affects virtually every part of the nervous system — the brain, spinal cord, and peripheral nerves. Because the disease touches so many body systems, no single specialist can manage it alone. Your child needs a team of experts who communicate with each other and coordinate their recommendations into a unified care plan.

According to the 2024 US Consensus Guidelines for MLD management, a comprehensive multidisciplinary approach is the standard of care. This means specialists from neurology, genetics, rehabilitation, nutrition, and many other fields working together — not in isolation.

This may feel overwhelming at first. You do not need to find every specialist on day one. Building your care team is a process, and your treating hospital will help you get started. The most important thing is to know what expertise your child may need so you can advocate for referrals when the time comes.

Recommended Specialists

▼

The following specialists are recommended as part of a comprehensive MLD care team. Not every child will need every specialist at every stage, but understanding each role will help you plan ahead.

Specialist Role in MLD Care
Pediatric Neurologist Typically the team lead. Monitors brain and nerve function, manages seizures, tracks disease progression through MRI and nerve conduction studies, and coordinates overall neurological care.
Metabolic / Genetic Specialist Expert in inborn errors of metabolism and lysosomal storage diseases. Helps interpret enzyme levels, monitor biomarkers, and guide treatment decisions specific to metabolic disease.
Geneticist & Genetic Counselor Interprets genetic test results, explains inheritance patterns to the family, coordinates sibling testing, and provides guidance on family planning and reproductive options.
Transplant Specialist If your child is a candidate for gene therapy (Lenmeldy) or hematopoietic stem cell transplant (HSCT), the transplant team manages the entire treatment process — from conditioning to engraftment to long-term follow-up.
Physiatrist A physical medicine and rehabilitation (PM&R) specialist. Oversees the rehabilitation plan, prescribes adaptive equipment (wheelchairs, standers, orthotics), and coordinates therapy services to maximize your child’s function and comfort.
Physical Therapist (PT) Works on gross motor skills, strength, flexibility, balance, and mobility. Helps prevent contractures and guides equipment selection. Relevant at every disease stage.
Occupational Therapist (OT) Focuses on fine motor skills, daily living activities, sensory processing, and adaptive techniques. Helps with feeding, dressing, and play skills.
Speech-Language Pathologist (SLP) Addresses communication, language development, cognitive-linguistic skills, and swallowing safety. Introduces augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) devices when needed.
Gastroenterologist Manages feeding difficulties, reflux, constipation, GI dysmotility, and nutritional concerns. Evaluates need for gastrostomy tube (G-tube) placement when swallowing becomes unsafe.
Ophthalmologist Monitors vision, which can be affected as demyelination progresses. Evaluates for optic nerve changes and visual processing issues.
Psychiatry / Behavioral Health Supports emotional and behavioral challenges that may arise, particularly in juvenile and adult-onset MLD. Can also support parents and siblings coping with the diagnosis.
Palliative Care Specialist Provides symptom management, quality-of-life support, and goals-of-care guidance at every stage — not just end of life. Supports the whole family, including siblings.
Social Worker Connects the family with community resources, insurance navigation, financial assistance, respite care, educational services, and emotional support programs.

Additional specialists may be needed depending on your child’s specific symptoms, including pulmonology (breathing and airway management), orthopedics (scoliosis, hip issues), audiology (hearing), endocrinology (bone density, growth), and dermatology (skin care).

The Care Coordinator

▼

With so many specialists involved, one of the most valuable members of your team is the care coordinator — a single person whose job is to connect all the pieces. A good care coordinator:

  • Schedules appointments and helps group them together to reduce travel burden
  • Ensures specialists communicate with each other about your child’s care
  • Tracks referrals, test results, and follow-up recommendations
  • Helps you understand what each specialist is recommending and how it fits the bigger picture
  • Serves as your primary point of contact when questions or concerns arise between appointments
  • Connects you with community resources, support programs, and financial assistance

At specialized leukodystrophy care centers, a care coordinator is part of the standard team. If your child is seen at a hospital without a dedicated coordinator, your social worker or primary neurologist’s nurse may be able to fill this role. You can also ask your hospital’s patient services department about care coordination for children with complex medical needs.

You are the most important coordinator. Even with a formal care coordinator, you as the parent or caregiver are the constant across all appointments and settings. You know your child better than any specialist. Your observations, questions, and instincts are a critical part of the care team.

Hunter’s Hope Leukodystrophy Care Network

▼

The Leukodystrophy Care Network (LCN), supported by Hunter’s Hope Foundation, is a network of 16 certified leukodystrophy care centers across the United States. These centers have demonstrated expertise in diagnosing and managing leukodystrophies, including MLD.

What makes an LCN center different from a general hospital:

  • Dedicated leukodystrophy teams — neurologists, geneticists, and therapists who specialize in these diseases
  • Built-in care coordinators — every LCN center has a coordinator to help manage your child’s complex care
  • Multidisciplinary clinics — many centers offer “one-stop” clinic days where your child can see multiple specialists in a single visit
  • Research participation — LCN centers often participate in clinical trials and registries, giving your family access to emerging therapies
  • Peer connection — these centers see enough leukodystrophy families that they can connect you with others on a similar journey

Even if you cannot travel to an LCN center regularly, an initial evaluation there can provide your local team with detailed recommendations and a care plan to follow. Many LCN centers also offer telemedicine consultations.

To find an LCN center near you, visit huntershope.org/family-care/leukodystrophy-care-network.

Building Your Care Team

▼

If you are newly diagnosed or still assembling your team, here is a practical starting path:

Step 1: Start with Your Treating Hospital

Your diagnosing physician or pediatric neurologist is usually the starting point. Ask them for referrals to the key specialists listed above. Most children’s hospitals have many of these specialists in-house.

Step 2: Contact an LCN Center

Even if you plan to receive most care locally, reaching out to a Leukodystrophy Care Network center can provide expert guidance. They can review your child’s case, recommend a monitoring schedule, and advise your local team. Many families do an annual or biannual visit to an LCN center while maintaining local care in between.

Step 3: Connect with a Treatment Center

If your child is a candidate for gene therapy or HSCT, you will need to connect with one of the qualified treatment centers. These are specialized hospitals with the infrastructure and expertise to deliver these complex therapies. See Qualified Treatment Centers for the current list.

Step 4: Build Your Therapy Team

Physical therapy, occupational therapy, and speech-language therapy should be started as early as possible. For children under age 3, your state’s Early Intervention program can provide in-home therapy services, often at no cost. For children age 3 and older, school-based services through an IEP (Individualized Education Program) can provide therapies during the school day.

Step 5: Integrate Palliative Care Early

Palliative care is not just for end of life — it provides symptom management, emotional support, and care coordination from the very beginning. Ask for a palliative care referral early. See Palliative & Supportive Care for more.

Keep a binder or digital folder. Start organizing your child’s medical records, test results, specialist contacts, medication lists, and insurance information in one place. Whether you prefer a physical binder or a digital system, having everything organized will save you time and stress at every appointment. Include a one-page summary of your child’s diagnosis, current medications, allergies, and key contacts that you can hand to any new provider.

Communication Between Providers

▼

One of the biggest challenges of multidisciplinary care is making sure all your specialists are talking to each other. Here are practical strategies that families have found helpful:

  • Request visit summaries — after each specialist appointment, ask for a written summary of findings and recommendations. Share these with your other providers.
  • Use a shared patient portal — if your specialists are within the same hospital system, they may share an electronic health record. Ask about this when setting up care.
  • Bring your care plan to every appointment — a current list of all providers, medications, therapies, and recent test results helps every specialist see the full picture.
  • Ask for provider-to-provider communication — if two specialists are making recommendations that seem to conflict, ask them to speak directly with each other.
  • Designate a “quarterback” — identify one provider (usually the neurologist or care coordinator) who will be responsible for integrating all recommendations and making sure nothing falls through the cracks.

MLD is rare enough that some of your local specialists may not have treated it before. That is okay. What matters is their willingness to learn, to consult with experts, and to work as part of your child’s team. You can help by sharing resources from this site and connecting them with LCN centers for guidance.
