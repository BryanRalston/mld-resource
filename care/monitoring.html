Why Monitoring Matters Established

▼

Regular monitoring is essential for every child with MLD — whether they are pre-symptomatic, have been treated, or are living with active disease. Monitoring serves several critical purposes:

  • Tracking disease progression — detecting changes early so interventions can be adjusted
  • Evaluating treatment effectiveness — for children who have received gene therapy or HSCT, monitoring shows whether the treatment is working
  • Guiding therapy and support — assessment results help therapists set appropriate goals and adapt their approach
  • Catching complications early — gallbladder disease, scoliosis, hip problems, and other issues can be addressed before they become serious
  • Informing family planning — baseline data helps families and physicians make decisions about future care
Monitoring is especially important for treated children. If your child has received gene therapy (Lenmeldy) or a stem cell transplant, close monitoring tracks whether the treatment has engrafted, whether enzyme levels are rising, and whether neurological function is stable or improving. Successful treatment does not mean monitoring stops — it means monitoring shifts to confirming that the treatment continues to work.

Recommended Monitoring Schedule Supported

▼

The following schedule is based on the 2024 US Consensus Guidelines for early-onset (late infantile and early juvenile) MLD. Your child’s specific schedule may vary based on their age, disease type, treatment status, and clinical team recommendations.

Assessment Timing Purpose
Brain MRI Within 3 months of birth (for pre-symptomatic infants); then every 6 months ages 1–7; annually thereafter Detects white matter changes, tracks demyelination progression, evaluates treatment response
Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS) Within 3 months of birth; then every 6 months in early childhood; less frequently as disease stabilizes or if treated Measures peripheral nerve function, detects neuropathy (often one of the earliest signs of MLD)
Developmental Assessment Every 3–4 months in early childhood; frequency adjusted by clinical status Tracks motor milestones, cognitive development, language, and adaptive behavior using standardized tools
Gallbladder Ultrasound Annually Gallbladder involvement (polypoid lesions, dysfunction) is common in MLD and may require surgical intervention
Ophthalmology Baseline evaluation; then as concerns arise or annually Monitors for visual changes, optic nerve involvement, and visual processing issues
Feeding & Swallowing Assessment Serial evaluation — frequency increases as disease progresses or concerns arise Ensures safe swallowing, identifies aspiration risk, guides decisions about diet modification or G-tube
Bone Density (DEXA Scan) Periodic screening, especially for non-ambulatory children Children with limited mobility are at higher risk for low bone density and fractures
Vitamin D Level Periodic screening with bone density Vitamin D deficiency is common in children with limited sun exposure and mobility; important for bone health
Scoliosis Screening Regular clinical assessment; imaging (X-ray) as indicated Spinal curvature can develop due to muscle imbalance and reduced mobility
Hip X-rays Regular screening, especially in non-ambulatory children Hip subluxation or dislocation can occur due to spasticity and positioning
Audiology Annual hearing evaluation Hearing can be affected by demyelination; early detection allows for hearing support

Understanding Each Test Established

▼

Brain MRI

An MRI (magnetic resonance imaging) scan creates detailed pictures of the brain without radiation. In MLD, the MRI shows characteristic patterns of white matter changes — bright areas on certain MRI sequences (T2/FLAIR) that indicate where myelin is damaged or lost. These changes typically start in specific brain regions and spread over time.

What “normal” looks like: In a healthy brain, the white matter appears uniform and symmetrical. For a treated child whose therapy is working, the MRI may show stable white matter or only mild changes that do not progress.

What “concerning” looks like: Expanding areas of abnormal signal, new regions becoming involved, or signs of brain volume loss suggest the disease is progressing. Your neurologist will compare each new MRI to previous scans to track any changes.

Nerve Conduction Studies

Nerve conduction studies (NCS) measure how quickly and efficiently electrical signals travel through peripheral nerves. Small electrodes are placed on the skin, and brief electrical impulses are delivered to stimulate the nerve. The response is recorded and measured.

What “normal” looks like: Signals travel at expected speeds (conduction velocities) with normal amplitude. In treated children, stable or improving nerve conduction values are a positive sign.

What “concerning” looks like: Slowed conduction velocities (the signal is traveling more slowly because myelin is damaged) and reduced amplitudes (fewer nerve fibers are working) indicate peripheral neuropathy is present or worsening.

Developmental Assessment

Standardized developmental testing evaluates your child’s motor skills, cognitive abilities, language, and adaptive behavior compared to age-appropriate norms. Common tools include the Bayley Scales of Infant Development, Gross Motor Function Measure (GMFM), and age-appropriate IQ or developmental quotient assessments.

What “normal” looks like: Scores within the expected range for age, or stable scores over time (for treated children, stability is success).

What “concerning” looks like: Declining scores over time, loss of previously acquired skills, or widening gap between your child’s abilities and age expectations.

Gallbladder Ultrasound

An ultrasound uses sound waves to create images of the gallbladder. It is painless, non-invasive, and does not involve radiation. Gallbladder involvement is surprisingly common in MLD — sulfatides can accumulate in the gallbladder wall, leading to polypoid lesions (small growths) and dysfunction.

What “normal” looks like: A smooth-walled gallbladder without polyps or thickening.

What “concerning” looks like: Polyps, wall thickening, or gallbladder dysfunction. In some cases, surgical removal (cholecystectomy) may be recommended, especially if there is a risk of malignancy or the polyps are growing.

Preparing your child for tests. Many of these tests can be stressful for young children. Ask your medical team about strategies like sedation for MRI (which requires staying still), numbing cream for blood draws, and distraction techniques during nerve conduction studies. Child Life specialists at many hospitals can help prepare your child and reduce anxiety.

Post-Treatment Monitoring Supported

▼

Children who have received gene therapy (Lenmeldy) or hematopoietic stem cell transplant (HSCT) require additional long-term monitoring specific to their treatment.

After Gene Therapy (Lenmeldy)

  • ARSA enzyme levels — periodic blood tests to confirm the body is producing the ARSA enzyme from the gene therapy. Levels should rise above the normal range (supraphysiologic levels are expected and desired).
  • Cognitive and motor assessments — regular standardized testing to track whether neurological function is stable or improving.
  • Cancer screening — because gene therapy uses a lentiviral vector to insert genetic material into cells, there is a theoretical risk of insertional oncogenesis (the inserted gene disrupting a cancer-related gene). The FDA recommends cancer monitoring for at least 15 years after treatment. This typically includes annual blood counts and clinical evaluations.
  • Brain MRI — continued monitoring for white matter changes and to confirm disease stability.
  • Engraftment and chimerism — blood tests to confirm the modified stem cells have engrafted and are contributing to blood cell production.

After Stem Cell Transplant (HSCT)

  • Engraftment monitoring — blood tests to confirm donor cells have taken hold and are functioning.
  • Graft-versus-host disease (GVHD) screening — monitoring for signs that donor cells are attacking the recipient’s tissues, which can affect the skin, liver, or GI tract.
  • Immune reconstitution — tracking the rebuilding of the immune system, which was suppressed during conditioning chemotherapy. Vaccinations are typically restarted on a specific schedule after transplant.
  • ARSA enzyme levels — periodic testing to confirm donor cells are producing ARSA enzyme.
  • Cognitive, motor, and neurological assessments — same as for gene therapy recipients.
Do not skip follow-up appointments. Even if your child is doing well after treatment, continued monitoring is essential. Some complications can emerge months or years later, and early detection makes them much more manageable. Your treatment center will provide a detailed follow-up schedule — bring this to your local providers so everyone stays aligned.

Creating a Monitoring Calendar Hypothesis

▼

With so many tests and assessments to track, creating a monitoring calendar can help you stay organized and ensure nothing is missed. Here are some practical suggestions:

  • Work with your care coordinator to map out the recommended schedule for the next 6–12 months.
  • Group appointments together when possible — for example, scheduling a brain MRI and developmental assessment on the same day or within the same week reduces the number of trips.
  • Set reminders for recurring tests like annual gallbladder ultrasound and hearing evaluations so they do not slip through the cracks.
  • Track results over time — keeping a simple log of key values (nerve conduction velocities, MRI summary, developmental scores) helps you and your medical team see trends.
  • Bring your monitoring history to every appointment so new providers can see the full picture.

Many families find it helpful to maintain a shared document or spreadsheet that both parents and caregivers can access, especially when coordinating between multiple care settings.
