Therapy & Mobility

Why Therapy Is Essential

▼

Physical therapy is the single most relevant therapy across all stages of MLD — 100% of patients benefit from it, whether they are pre-symptomatic, post-treatment, or living with advanced disease. It is not optional. It is a core part of your child’s medical care.

The goals of therapy evolve as your child’s needs change, but the underlying purpose remains constant: maximize your child’s function, comfort, and quality of life at every stage.

Start therapies early, even if your child seems fine after treatment. Maintaining function is always easier than trying to regain it. Children who have received gene therapy or HSCT still benefit enormously from ongoing therapy to support their development, address any residual neuropathy, and prevent complications like contractures.

Physical Therapy Goals by Stage

▼

Pre-Symptomatic and Early Stage

For children who have been diagnosed through newborn screening or sibling testing and have not yet developed symptoms — or who have just begun to show early signs:

  • Maintain and build strength, flexibility, and endurance
  • Support achievement of motor milestones (rolling, sitting, crawling, standing, walking)
  • Establish a baseline of function for future comparison
  • Begin stretching routines to prevent tightness before it develops
  • Monitor balance and coordination closely — subtle changes here may be the earliest signs of progression
  • Introduce aquatic therapy if available — the buoyancy of water supports movement and reduces joint stress

Moderate Stage

As motor function begins to change, therapy goals shift toward adaptation and preservation:

  • Adapt activities to match your child’s current abilities — focus on what they can do, not what they have lost
  • Prevent contractures — regular stretching and range-of-motion exercises to keep joints from becoming fixed in one position
  • Maintain mobility as long as possible through supported walking, gait trainers, and adaptive equipment
  • Weight-bearing activities — standing (even with support) is important for bone health, digestion, and respiratory function
  • Introduce adaptive equipment as needed (see equipment section below)
  • Energy conservation — teach techniques to accomplish tasks with less fatigue
  • Fall prevention — environmental modifications and strategies to reduce injury risk

Advanced Stage

In later stages, therapy focuses on comfort and quality of life:

  • Positioning — proper positioning in bed, wheelchair, and other seating to prevent skin breakdown, promote comfort, and support breathing
  • Comfort-focused movement — gentle stretching, range of motion, and massage to reduce spasticity and discomfort
  • Respiratory support — chest physiotherapy and positioning to support lung function and clear secretions
  • Joint protection — preventing further contracture progression and managing those already present
  • Caregiver training — teaching safe lifting, transfer, and positioning techniques to protect both your child and yourself

Occupational Therapy

▼

While physical therapy focuses on gross motor function (large movements like walking, standing, and sitting), occupational therapy (OT) addresses the skills needed for daily living:

  • Fine motor skills — grasping, manipulating objects, using utensils, drawing, and writing
  • Self-care skills — feeding, dressing, bathing, and toileting (adapted to your child’s abilities)
  • Sensory processing — helping your child regulate responses to sensory input (touch, movement, sound, visual stimulation)
  • Play and participation — adapting activities so your child can participate in play, social interaction, and age-appropriate activities
  • Visual-motor skills — hand-eye coordination for tasks like reaching, pointing, and using switches or communication devices
  • Adaptive equipment recommendations — specialized utensils, seating supports, and environmental modifications

OT therapists are also essential partners in evaluating and setting up adaptive equipment for your home, including bath chairs, adapted car seats, and environmental controls.

Speech-Language Therapy

▼

Speech-language pathologists (SLPs) play a dual role in MLD care — supporting communication and managing swallowing safety.

Communication

  • Language development — supporting both understanding (receptive language) and expression (expressive language)
  • Cognitive-linguistic skills — attention, memory, problem-solving, and processing speed
  • Augmentative and alternative communication (AAC) — introducing communication tools early, before verbal communication becomes difficult. Starting AAC while your child can still participate in learning the system leads to much better outcomes.
  • Social communication — supporting interaction with family, peers, and caregivers

Swallowing

SLPs also assess and manage swallowing function, which is discussed in detail on the Feeding & Nutrition page. They work closely with your gastroenterologist and nutritionist to ensure your child can eat safely.

AAC is not giving up on speech. Many families worry that introducing a communication device means accepting that their child will not speak. The opposite is true — AAC supports speech development and gives your child a way to communicate their wants, needs, and feelings regardless of their verbal abilities. Starting early makes a significant difference.

Equipment Progression

▼

As your child’s needs change, adaptive equipment helps them stay active, comfortable, and engaged. Your PT, OT, and physiatrist will recommend equipment based on your child’s current abilities and anticipated needs. Here is the typical progression:

Equipment Purpose When Typically Introduced
Gait Trainers / Walkers Support independent walking when balance or strength is impaired When walking becomes unsteady or fatiguing
Standing Frames / Standers Allow weight-bearing in a supported standing position for bone health, digestion, and social engagement When independent standing becomes difficult
Manual Wheelchair Independent or assisted mobility for longer distances and community access When walking becomes unsafe or excessively fatiguing
Power Wheelchair Independent mobility when upper body strength is insufficient for manual propulsion When the child cannot effectively propel a manual wheelchair
Activity / Positioning Chairs Proper trunk support for seating during meals, school, and activities When standard seating no longer provides adequate support
Bath Chairs Safe, supported seating for bathing and hygiene When sitting in a standard tub becomes unsafe
Adapted Car Seats Safe vehicle transport with appropriate positioning and support When standard car seats no longer provide adequate support
Communication Devices (AAC) Speech-generating devices, communication boards, eye-tracking systems When verbal communication becomes difficult — ideally introduced early while the child can learn the system
Orthotics and Braces (AFOs, SMOs) Support ankle/foot alignment, prevent deformity, improve gait pattern When foot drop, ankle instability, or gait abnormalities appear
Patient Lifts Safe transfer between surfaces (bed to wheelchair, wheelchair to bath) When manual lifting becomes unsafe for the child or caregiver
Plan ahead for equipment. Insurance approval, ordering, and fitting can take weeks to months. Work with your therapy team to anticipate equipment needs and begin the process early. Many families benefit from having equipment trials (loaning a piece of equipment to try before ordering) to ensure a good fit.

Accessing Therapy Services

▼

Early Intervention (Birth to Age 3)

Every state in the United States has an Early Intervention (EI) program, mandated by federal law under Part C of the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA). These programs provide:

  • In-home therapy services (PT, OT, speech) at no cost or very low cost to the family
  • An Individualized Family Service Plan (IFSP) that outlines your child’s goals and services
  • Service coordination to help connect you with community resources
  • Developmental assessments and progress monitoring

To access EI services, ask your pediatrician or neurologist for a referral, or contact your state’s EI program directly. You do not need a formal diagnosis to receive an evaluation — a concern about your child’s development is sufficient.

School-Based Services (Age 3+)

At age 3, children transition from Early Intervention to school-based services under Part B of IDEA. If your child qualifies, they will receive an Individualized Education Program (IEP) that may include:

  • School-based PT, OT, and speech therapy
  • Adapted physical education
  • Assistive technology (including AAC devices)
  • Classroom accommodations and modifications
  • A one-on-one aide if needed

Outpatient and Private Therapy

In addition to EI and school services, many families pursue outpatient therapy through hospitals or private practices. This is especially important when school-based services alone are not sufficient to meet your child’s needs. Your insurance may cover outpatient therapy with a prescription from your physician.

You can have both. Early Intervention, school-based services, and outpatient therapy are not mutually exclusive. Many children with MLD receive services from multiple sources simultaneously. Your care coordinator or social worker can help you navigate which services your child is eligible for.

Home Exercise and the Family’s Role

▼

Therapy does not only happen in a clinic. Your therapists will teach you exercises, stretches, and techniques to practice at home. Consistent home programs make a meaningful difference in outcomes.

  • Make it routine — build exercises into daily activities like diaper changes, bath time, and play
  • Make it fun — children are more likely to participate when exercises feel like play
  • Be flexible — some days your child will have more energy and tolerance than others. Adjust intensity and duration accordingly.
  • Take care of yourself — caregiving is physically demanding. Ask your therapist about proper body mechanics for lifting, carrying, and transferring to protect your own back and joints.

Remember: you are not expected to be a therapist. You are expected to be a parent. Do what you can, when you can, and trust that your love and consistency matter more than perfect technique.
