Stem Cell Transplant (HSCT)

Evidence Confidence Guide
Established Peer-reviewed consensus Supported Published evidence Hypothesis Reasonable extrapolation Speculative Requires validation

Hematopoietic stem cell transplant (HSCT) — commonly called bone marrow transplant — has been used to treat MLD for decades, long before gene therapy became available.[1] It remains an important treatment option, particularly for patients who do not qualify for Lenmeldy gene therapy.

HSCT for MLD uses allogeneic transplant (cells from a healthy donor), as opposed to the autologous approach used by Lenmeldy (the patient’s own modified cells). This fundamental difference shapes both the benefits and the risks of the procedure.

For late infantile pre-symptomatic babies: Lenmeldy gene therapy is the recommended treatment, not HSCT. The clinical data for gene therapy in this population is substantially stronger. HSCT is generally recommended when gene therapy is not an option — either because of the patient’s age, MLD type, or clinical situation.

How HSCT Works for MLD Established

▼

The goal of HSCT in MLD is to replace the patient’s bone marrow — which produces immune cells that lack functional ARSA enzyme — with healthy donor bone marrow that produces cells with normal ARSA activity.[4]

The Process Established

  1. Donor identification: A suitable HLA-matched donor is identified. This can be a matched sibling (ideal), a matched unrelated donor from a bone marrow registry, or in some cases a haploidentical (half-matched) family donor or cord blood unit.
  2. Conditioning: The patient receives chemotherapy (and sometimes radiation) to destroy their existing bone marrow and suppress the immune system enough to prevent rejection of the donor cells.
  3. Transplant: The donor’s stem cells are infused intravenously. They travel to the bone marrow, engraft, and begin producing new blood cells.
  4. Engraftment: Over weeks, the donor cells establish themselves and begin producing the full range of blood cells, including immune cells that carry functional ARSA enzyme.[1]
  5. Enzyme delivery: This is the critical step for MLD. Donor-derived monocytes and macrophages gradually migrate from the bloodstream into the central nervous system. Once there, they release ARSA enzyme, which is taken up by surrounding brain cells through a process called cross-correction.[4]

The Critical Time Gap Established

Here is the fundamental challenge of HSCT for MLD: it takes 6 to 12 months for donor-derived macrophages to migrate into the brain in sufficient numbers to provide meaningful enzyme delivery.[15] During this window, the disease continues to progress unchecked. This is why HSCT works best when started as early as possible — ideally before symptoms appear — to give the donor cells time to establish before significant damage occurs.

This time gap is one of the key reasons why Lenmeldy gene therapy is preferred for late infantile MLD, where the disease progresses rapidly. The supranormal ARSA levels achieved by gene therapy may provide faster and more robust enzyme delivery.

When HSCT Is Recommended Established

▼

HSCT is currently considered the appropriate treatment in the following situations:

Primary Candidates Established

  • Pre-symptomatic or early-symptomatic late juvenile MLD: Children with later-onset juvenile MLD who are not eligible for Lenmeldy (which is approved for children age 6 and under)
  • Pre-symptomatic or early-symptomatic adult-onset MLD: Adults diagnosed before significant neurological decline. Adult-onset MLD tends to progress more slowly, which may give HSCT more time to take effect.

Secondary Considerations Established

  • Early juvenile MLD patients outside Lenmeldy criteria: For example, children over age 6 who are pre-symptomatic
  • When gene therapy is not accessible: In regions or situations where Lenmeldy is not available, HSCT may be the best available option

When HSCT Is NOT Recommended Established

  • Late infantile MLD (pre-symptomatic): Lenmeldy gene therapy produces superior outcomes in this group
  • Any MLD type with significant existing neurological involvement: Once substantial symptoms have developed, HSCT cannot reverse the damage, and the 6–12 month gap before enzyme delivery means the disease will continue progressing during that window
HSCT is not effective once significant neurological damage has occurred. The same fundamental limitation applies to both HSCT and gene therapy: no current treatment can rebuild myelin that has already been destroyed. The earlier treatment begins, the better the outcome.

Outcomes and Evidence Supported

▼

HSCT outcomes for MLD are more variable and less predictable than gene therapy outcomes. This reflects both the heterogeneity of the patient population and the limitations of the allogeneic approach.

What the Data Shows Supported

  • Survival: Transplant-related mortality is approximately 10–15%, primarily due to graft failure, infections, and graft-versus-host disease.[15] Survival rates have improved with modern supportive care but remain a significant consideration.
  • Disease stabilization: In published case series, some children and adults who received HSCT pre-symptomatically or very early in symptom onset showed long-term stabilization of neurological function. Some have remained stable for over a decade.
  • Variable response: Outcomes range widely. Some patients stabilize completely; others continue to decline despite successful engraftment. Factors that influence outcomes include timing of transplant, donor match quality, MLD type, and individual variation in how quickly donor cells migrate to the brain.[1][15]
  • Enzyme levels: HSCT achieves normal (not supranormal) ARSA levels in the blood.[4] The amount of enzyme that reaches the brain depends on how effectively donor macrophages cross the blood-brain barrier.
  • Peripheral nervous system: HSCT, like gene therapy, may have incomplete effects on peripheral nerve function. Some patients experience ongoing peripheral neuropathy despite stable central nervous system function.

Juvenile and Adult-Onset MLD Supported

For juvenile and adult-onset MLD, HSCT remains the treatment with the most clinical experience.[1] Because these forms progress more slowly than late infantile MLD, the 6–12 month window for donor cell migration is less of a limitation. Some patients with juvenile or adult-onset MLD have shown decades of stability after HSCT.[15]

Risks and Complications Established

▼

Graft-Versus-Host Disease (GVHD) Established

This is the most significant risk unique to allogeneic HSCT. Because the transplanted cells come from a donor, the donor’s immune cells may recognize the patient’s body as foreign and attack it. GVHD can affect the skin, liver, gut, and other organs.

  • Acute GVHD: Occurs within the first 100 days after transplant. Can range from mild (skin rash) to severe (organ damage). Immunosuppressive medications are used to prevent and treat it.
  • Chronic GVHD: Can develop months to years after transplant. May require long-term immunosuppressive therapy. Can significantly impact quality of life.

Graft Failure Established

The donor cells may fail to engraft or may be rejected by the patient’s immune system. Graft failure requires a second transplant attempt or alternative treatment approach.

Infections Established

The conditioning regimen and ongoing immunosuppression leave patients vulnerable to serious infections. Viral, bacterial, and fungal infections are all risks during the recovery period.

Conditioning-Related Toxicity Established

Similar to gene therapy, the chemotherapy conditioning carries risks including:

  • Veno-occlusive disease (liver toxicity)
  • Organ damage
  • Infertility
  • Secondary cancers (long-term risk from chemotherapy and/or radiation)

Mortality Supported

The overall transplant-related mortality for HSCT in MLD is approximately 10–15%.[15] This is higher than the mortality risk associated with Lenmeldy gene therapy (which avoids GVHD), and it is a significant factor in treatment decision-making.

Donor Matching Established

▼

Finding a suitable donor is one of the first steps in the HSCT process and can be one of the most challenging.

Types of Donors Established

  • Matched sibling donor: An unaffected sibling who is HLA-matched. This is considered the ideal donor source — but siblings of MLD patients have only a 25% chance of being both unaffected and HLA-matched.[1]
  • Matched unrelated donor (MUD): Found through national and international bone marrow registries (such as Be The Match). The likelihood of finding a match varies significantly by ethnic background.[4]
  • Cord blood: Stem cells from donated umbilical cord blood. Cord blood requires less stringent HLA matching but contains fewer cells, which can lead to slower engraftment.
  • Haploidentical donor: A half-matched family member (usually a parent). This is an option when no fully matched donor is available, though it carries higher risks of GVHD and graft failure.

Important Note About Carrier Siblings Established

Siblings who are carriers of MLD (one mutated ARSA gene, one functional) can serve as donors. Carriers produce enough ARSA enzyme for normal function. However, siblings must be tested to confirm they are not affected (two mutated genes) before being considered as donors.

One advantage of Lenmeldy: Gene therapy uses the patient’s own cells, eliminating the need to find a donor entirely. For families struggling to find a matched donor, Lenmeldy (if the child qualifies) removes this obstacle.
