The Treatment Journey

Understanding what lies ahead can help reduce the anxiety that comes with a treatment this significant. This page walks through the Lenmeldy gene therapy process from first contact to long-term follow-up — what happens at each stage, how long it takes, and what families can expect.

The total journey from initial contact to completion of treatment spans roughly 9 to 12 months.[7] It is a marathon, not a sprint, and it will require patience, resilience, and strong support. But families who have been through it consistently say the same thing: it was worth it.

Every child’s experience is unique. The timeline and details below are based on the general treatment protocol. Your child’s treatment center will provide a specific plan tailored to their situation. Ask questions at every step — your care team expects and welcomes them.

The Treatment Timeline Established

▼

Phase 1 — Evaluation & Stem Cell Collection

Duration: 1–2 weeks (hospitalization required)

This is the starting point. Once your child has been referred to a qualified treatment center and initial eligibility is confirmed, the first hospitalization begins.

  • Comprehensive neurological assessment: The medical team will perform a thorough evaluation of your child’s motor function, cognitive development, vision, hearing, and nerve function. This establishes a baseline that all future progress will be measured against.
  • Brain MRI: Imaging to assess the current state of white matter in the brain.
  • Mobilization: Your child will receive medication (typically G-CSF/filgrastim) to mobilize hematopoietic stem cells from the bone marrow into the bloodstream, making them accessible for collection.
  • Central venous catheter placement: A central line (large IV) is surgically placed to allow for the apheresis procedure and subsequent treatments. This is typically done under general anesthesia.
  • Apheresis (stem cell collection): Blood is drawn through the central line, passed through a machine that separates out the CD34+ hematopoietic stem cells, and the remaining blood is returned to the child. This process takes several hours per session and is typically performed over 4–5 days.[7] Young children are sedated during the procedure.
  • Catheter removal and discharge: Once enough cells have been collected, the central line is removed and the family goes home to wait for the manufacturing phase.

Phase 2 — Manufacturing

Duration: 3–4 months (no hospitalization — family is home)

This is the waiting phase, and it can feel like the hardest part for families who know the clock is ticking. But this time is essential — each dose of Lenmeldy is custom-made for one specific patient.

  • Cells shipped to manufacturing facility: Your child’s collected stem cells are cryopreserved and shipped to Orchard Therapeutics’ specialized manufacturing site.[7]
  • Genetic modification: In the lab, the cells are transduced with a lentiviral vector carrying functional copies of the ARSA gene. This is a precise, carefully controlled process.
  • Expansion and quality testing: The modified cells are expanded (grown in number) and subjected to extensive quality control and safety testing. Every batch must meet strict release criteria.
  • Custom product: The final product is uniquely made for your child — it cannot be used for any other patient.

During this time, stay in close contact with your treatment center. They may schedule interim check-ups to ensure your child remains eligible and healthy for the next phase.

Phase 3 — Conditioning & Infusion

Duration: 4–6 weeks (hospitalization required)

This is the most intensive phase of treatment. Your child will be hospitalized for the entire duration.

  • Pre-treatment testing: Bloodwork, organ function tests, and a repeat neurological assessment to confirm your child is ready for conditioning.
  • Central venous catheter placed: A new central line is placed for the conditioning and infusion.
  • Busulfan-based chemotherapy conditioning: Over approximately 4 days, your child will receive busulfan (a chemotherapy drug) to destroy the existing bone marrow.[7][12] This makes room for the new, corrected cells and prevents the old, defective cells from competing with them. This phase is similar to what cancer patients experience before a bone marrow transplant.
  • Immune suppression: The conditioning wipes out your child’s immune system temporarily. Strict infection precautions begin — hand washing, limited visitors, possible isolation protocols.
  • Infusion day: Approximately one week after conditioning begins, the genetically modified cells are thawed and infused intravenously. The infusion itself is relatively quick — often under an hour — and resembles a blood transfusion. This is the moment the corrected cells enter the body.
The conditioning phase is difficult. Expect side effects: nausea, vomiting, mouth sores, fatigue, hair loss, and susceptibility to infections. Your child will feel sick. This is temporary, and the medical team is experienced in managing these effects, but families should prepare emotionally for several hard weeks.

Phase 4 — Engraftment & Recovery

Duration: Weeks to months (hospital, then transition to outpatient)

After infusion, the modified cells need to find their way to the bone marrow, settle in, and start producing new blood cells. This process is called engraftment.

  • Neutrophil engraftment: The first sign that the new cells are working. This typically occurs between 26 and 42 days after infusion (median 33 days).[12] Neutrophils are infection-fighting white blood cells, and their return signals that the bone marrow is beginning to function.
  • Platelet engraftment: Platelets (needed for blood clotting) typically recover between 25 and 46 days (median 26 days).[12] Until platelet counts recover, your child may need platelet transfusions.
  • Close monitoring: Daily blood counts, regular checks for infections, and careful management of any complications. Your child will remain in the hospital until engraftment is confirmed and stable.
  • Isolation precautions: The immune system is severely compromised during this period. Strict hand hygiene, limited visitors, and protective measures are essential.
  • Transition to outpatient: Once blood counts are stable and the child is eating, taking medications by mouth, and free of active infections, they can be discharged. Frequent outpatient visits continue for weeks afterward.

Phase 5 — Long-Term Monitoring

Duration: Years (at least 15 years recommended)[6]

Treatment does not end at discharge. Long-term follow-up is essential to track your child’s progress and screen for any late effects.

  • ARSA enzyme levels: Regular blood tests to confirm that enzyme production remains at therapeutic (ideally supranormal) levels.
  • Developmental monitoring: Cognitive assessments, motor function evaluations, and developmental milestone tracking at regular intervals.
  • Brain MRI: Periodic imaging to monitor white matter integrity and confirm that demyelination is not progressing.
  • Nerve conduction studies: To assess peripheral nerve function, which may respond differently than central nervous system function.[6]
  • Annual blood tests for at least 15 years: To screen for any abnormalities that could suggest insertional oncogenesis (the theoretical risk that the viral vector could activate a cancer gene). No cases have been observed to date, but monitoring is a precaution.[6][12]
  • Vaccinations: Once the immune system has sufficiently recovered, your child will need to be re-vaccinated, as the conditioning chemotherapy erases prior immunity.

Preparing for the Hospital Stays Established

▼

Your family will spend significant time in the hospital — particularly during Phase 1 (1–2 weeks) and Phases 3–4 (4–8+ weeks). Being prepared can make a real difference in how manageable those weeks feel.

Packing list suggestions from families who have been through it:
  • For your child: Favorite blanket or stuffed animal, comfort items from home, tablet with downloaded shows/games (hospital Wi-Fi can be unreliable), noise-canceling headphones, small toys that can be sanitized easily
  • For parents: Comfortable clothes for sleeping in hospital chairs, a good water bottle, phone chargers (bring two), snacks you actually enjoy, a notebook to track questions for the medical team, earplugs and an eye mask
  • For your sanity: Photos or artwork from home, a small speaker for music, a book or podcast for the long quiet hours, contact list of friends and family for support
  • Documents: Insurance cards, ID, contact list for your medical team, any advance directives or care plans, a list of your child’s current medications and allergies

Planning Ahead

  • Housing: If the treatment center is far from home, ask about Ronald McDonald House or other hospital-affiliated family housing. Treatment centers can often help arrange accommodations.
  • Siblings: Arrange care for other children during hospital stays. Consider how to keep siblings connected through video calls.
  • Work: Talk with your employer early. FMLA (Family and Medical Leave Act) may apply. Some families have found that writing a brief letter to their employer explaining the situation helps.
  • Emotional support: Line up your support network before you need it. A social worker at the treatment center can connect you with resources, counseling, and family support programs.
  • Meals: Many hospitals have meal programs for families of inpatients. Ask your care coordinator about what is available.

What to Expect Emotionally Supported

▼

The treatment journey is not just a medical process — it is an emotional one. Understanding what other families have experienced can help you feel less alone in it.

  • Before treatment: A mix of urgency, hope, fear, and information overload. It is normal to feel all of these at once. Lean on your treatment team to answer questions as they come up.
  • During stem cell collection: Relief at starting the process, anxiety about the procedures. Seeing your young child sedated for apheresis is hard.
  • The manufacturing wait: This can be the most psychologically difficult period. The clock is ticking, and there is nothing you can do but wait. Stay connected with your care team and your support network.
  • During conditioning: Watching your child go through chemotherapy is painful. Remind yourself why you are here. The medical team has done this before and is watching everything closely.
  • Infusion day: Often feels anticlimactic — the actual infusion is quick and quiet. But it is the most important hour of the entire journey.
  • Engraftment wait: Counting neutrophils every morning. Celebrating small numbers. Anxiety about infections. Relief when engraftment is confirmed.
  • Going home: Joy mixed with a new kind of anxiety. The hospital felt scary but also safe. Transitioning to monitoring from home takes adjustment.
  • Months and years after: Watching your child reach milestones they were not expected to reach. Gratitude mixed with survivor’s guilt if you know other MLD families who did not have the same opportunity. It is all normal.

Your treatment center likely has social workers, psychologists, and family support coordinators. Use them. They are there for exactly this.
