Treatment Overview

Timing is everything. Once significant symptoms appear, current treatments cannot reverse the neurological damage that has already occurred. This is why early diagnosis and treatment are critical. If your child is pre-symptomatic, the treatment window is open — but every week matters.

The treatment landscape for Metachromatic Leukodystrophy has changed dramatically. Where families once faced a diagnosis with no options, there are now two established treatment paths — and an active research pipeline working toward even better ones. But the single most important factor in treatment outcomes is when treatment begins, not which treatment is chosen.

This section walks through everything you need to know: what treatments exist, who qualifies, what the process looks like, where to go, and what is coming next.

The Most Important Thing to Understand

▼

MLD destroys myelin — the protective coating around nerve fibers in the brain and throughout the body. Once myelin is destroyed and the underlying nerve cells are damaged, that damage is permanent with current medicine. No treatment available today can rebuild destroyed myelin or restore lost neurological function.

What treatment can do is stop the destruction from continuing. It provides the missing ARSA enzyme so that sulfatides — the toxic fatty substances that build up and destroy myelin — are properly broken down. When treatment is given early enough, children can develop normally or near-normally because the damage never occurs in the first place.

This is why the difference between pre-symptomatic and symptomatic treatment outcomes is so stark:

Pre-symptomatic treatment outcomes (Lenmeldy gene therapy, late infantile MLD):
  • 100% alive at age 6 (compared to 58% of untreated children)
  • 85% maintained normal cognition and language development
  • 71% walking independently at age 5

These children are growing up. Going to school. Playing with siblings. This was not possible a decade ago.

The message is clear: if there is any possibility of early diagnosis — through newborn screening, sibling testing, or family history — the opportunity to treat before symptoms appear can change everything.

Two Main Treatment Options

▼

There are currently two established treatments for MLD. They work differently, they are suited for different situations, and they carry different risks. Understanding both will help you have informed conversations with your child’s medical team.

1. Lenmeldy Gene Therapy (Autologous)

Lenmeldy (atidarsagene autotemcel) is a one-time gene therapy that uses the patient’s own stem cells. Those cells are collected, genetically modified in a lab to carry functional copies of the ARSA gene, and then infused back into the patient. Because the cells are the child’s own, there is no risk of graft-versus-host disease.

Lenmeldy was approved by the FDA in March 2024 and by the European Medicines Agency in late 2020 (marketed as Libmeldy in the EU). It is the treatment of choice for pre-symptomatic late infantile MLD and for pre-symptomatic or early-symptomatic early juvenile MLD.

2. Hematopoietic Stem Cell Transplant (Allogeneic HSCT)

HSCT (commonly called bone marrow transplant) uses stem cells from a healthy donor — a matched sibling, parent, or unrelated registry donor. The donor’s cells produce functional ARSA enzyme, and over time, donor-derived immune cells (macrophages) migrate into the brain and release the enzyme to surrounding tissues.

HSCT is primarily considered for pre-symptomatic or early-symptomatic juvenile and adult-onset MLD. It is generally not recommended for late infantile MLD because gene therapy produces superior outcomes in that population.

Which Treatment for Which Situation

▼

The right treatment depends on the type of MLD, the stage of disease at diagnosis, and the child’s age. Here is the general guidance from current medical consensus — but your child’s medical team will make the final recommendation based on their specific situation.

Late Infantile MLD (onset typically 6 months–4 years)

  • Pre-symptomatic: Lenmeldy gene therapy is the treatment of choice. This is the population with the strongest clinical evidence and the best outcomes.
  • Symptomatic: No currently approved treatment can reverse damage that has occurred. Supportive care becomes the focus. Clinical trials may be an option.

Juvenile MLD (onset typically 4–16 years)

  • Pre-symptomatic early juvenile: Lenmeldy gene therapy is approved for this group (children age 6 and under who are pre-symptomatic).
  • Early-symptomatic early juvenile: Lenmeldy may be appropriate if the child is still walking and before significant cognitive decline.
  • Pre-symptomatic or early-symptomatic late juvenile: HSCT is typically recommended, as Lenmeldy is not currently approved for this group.

Adult MLD (onset after age 16)

  • Pre-symptomatic or early-symptomatic: HSCT may be considered. Adult-onset MLD often progresses more slowly, which can give HSCT more time to take effect.

What Treatment Cannot Do

▼

It is important to understand the limitations of current treatments so that families can set realistic expectations and make informed decisions:

  • Neither treatment can reverse existing damage. If a child has already lost the ability to walk, treatment will not restore walking. The goal is to prevent further deterioration.
  • Treatment outcomes vary. Even among pre-symptomatic children, outcomes are not identical. Some children develop entirely normally; others may have mild delays.
  • Conditioning chemotherapy carries real risks. Both treatments require chemotherapy to prepare the body, which brings its own side effects and health risks.
  • Long-term monitoring is required. Gene therapy requires at least 15 years of follow-up to monitor for potential late effects, including theoretical cancer risk from the viral vector.
  • Peripheral nervous system response may be incomplete. While both treatments address the central nervous system (brain and spinal cord), the peripheral nerves (arms, legs, organs) may respond less completely.

None of this should discourage treatment — the outcomes for early-treated children are genuinely remarkable. But understanding reality helps families prepare for the journey ahead.

Taking the First Step

▼

If your child has been diagnosed with MLD — or if a sibling has been identified as at-risk through family testing — the most important thing you can do right now is contact a qualified treatment center. The evaluation process takes time, manufacturing the gene therapy product takes months, and the earlier you start, the better the outcome.
