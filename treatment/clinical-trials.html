Clinical Trials

Clinical trials are how new treatments become proven treatments. Lenmeldy itself exists because families participated in clinical trials that demonstrated its safety and effectiveness. For families dealing with MLD today, clinical trials may offer access to next-generation therapies, expanded eligibility for existing treatments, or important contributions to the scientific understanding of the disease.

Clinical trials are not “experimental last resorts.” Many clinical trials represent the cutting edge of treatment — they are the path to next-generation therapies that could be better than what is currently available. Participating in a trial also contributes directly to the knowledge base that will help future MLD families. Talk with your medical team about whether any open trials are a good fit for your child.

Understanding the EMBOLDEN Trial Results

The EMBOLDEN trial (NCT03771898) tested intrathecal enzyme replacement therapy (ERT) — delivering recombinant ARSA enzyme directly into the cerebrospinal fluid. While the trial showed that the treatment successfully reduced sulfatide accumulation in the brain (biochemical correction), it did not meet its primary endpoint of improving motor function.

This does not necessarily mean the approach is a dead end. It may mean that the dosing, timing, or delivery method needs refinement. But it does illustrate a key challenge in MLD research: correcting the underlying biochemistry is necessary but may not be sufficient if damage has already occurred or if enzyme delivery to all affected areas is incomplete.

How to Search for Clinical Trials

▼

ClinicalTrials.gov

The most comprehensive database of clinical trials is maintained by the U.S. National Library of Medicine at ClinicalTrials.gov.

To search for MLD-related trials:

  1. Go to ClinicalTrials.gov
  2. In the search bar, enter “metachromatic leukodystrophy”
  3. Use the filters to narrow results:
    • Recruitment Status: Select “Recruiting” to see only trials currently accepting patients
    • Age Group: Filter by your child’s age group
    • Study Type: “Interventional” for treatment trials, “Observational” for natural history studies
  4. Click on individual trials to read eligibility criteria, study locations, and contact information

Other Resources

  • Cure MLD: Maintains a research page with curated information about active trials relevant to MLD families
  • National Organization for Rare Disorders (NORD): Lists clinical trials for rare diseases including MLD
  • Your treatment center: MLD specialists at qualified treatment centers are often involved in clinical research and may know about trials that are not yet publicly listed
  • EU Clinical Trials Register: For families in or willing to travel to Europe, clinicaltrialsregister.eu lists European trials

Questions to Ask About Clinical Trials

▼

If you are considering a clinical trial for your child, here are important questions to discuss with the trial investigators and your medical team:

About the Trial

  • What is the purpose of this trial? What question is it trying to answer?
  • What phase is the trial in? (Phase 1 = safety, Phase 2 = dosing/efficacy, Phase 3 = large-scale confirmation)
  • Is there a placebo group, or will all participants receive the experimental treatment?
  • How long will the trial last, and what is the follow-up commitment?
  • What has been observed so far in earlier phases or preclinical research?

About Eligibility and Logistics

  • Does my child meet the eligibility criteria?
  • Where are the trial sites? Will we need to travel?
  • How often will we need to visit the trial site?
  • Are travel and accommodation costs covered by the trial?
  • Will participation prevent my child from receiving other treatments?

About Risks and Benefits

  • What are the known risks and side effects of this experimental treatment?
  • What monitoring and safety measures are in place?
  • If the treatment works, will my child continue to have access to it after the trial ends?
  • What happens if we decide to withdraw from the trial?
  • How does this trial compare to currently available treatments?
You can always ask for time. You do not need to make a decision about clinical trial participation immediately. Ask for the full informed consent document, take it home, and discuss it with your family and medical team before deciding.

Understanding Trial Types

▼

Interventional Trials

These test a new treatment, drug, or procedure. Participants receive the experimental intervention and are monitored for safety and effectiveness. The gene therapy trials (like the original Lenmeldy pivotal trial and the late juvenile MLD expansion trial) are interventional trials.

Observational / Natural History Studies

These do not test a treatment. Instead, they carefully document how MLD progresses over time in different populations. This data is invaluable because:

  • It provides the baseline against which treatment outcomes are compared
  • It helps identify biomarkers that could predict disease course or treatment response
  • It helps characterize rare subtypes that are not well understood

Participating in a natural history study does not prevent your child from also receiving treatment. These studies often run in parallel with standard care.

Biorepositories

These collect and store biological samples (blood, tissue, genetic material) and clinical data from patients. The Myelin Disorders Biorepository, for example, provides researchers around the world with the samples they need to study MLD and develop new treatments. Contributing to a biorepository is one of the most impactful ways families can support MLD research.

The Bigger Picture

▼

Every family that participates in a clinical trial or natural history study is contributing to the body of knowledge that will help future MLD families. The 37 children in the original Lenmeldy trial — and their families who said yes to something unproven — are the reason that gene therapy is available today.

The research pipeline for MLD is more active now than at any point in history. Trials are exploring expanded gene therapy eligibility, improved viral vectors, alternative delivery methods, and entirely new therapeutic approaches. For more on what is coming next, see Emerging Research.

If you are interested in clinical trial participation, start the conversation with your child’s specialist. They can help you evaluate which trials might be appropriate and how participation would fit with your child’s overall treatment plan.
