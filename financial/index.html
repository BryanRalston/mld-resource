Understanding the Costs

The Honest Picture

▼

Let’s address this directly: Lenmeldy, the FDA-approved gene therapy for MLD, carries a wholesale price of $4.25 million — making it the most expensive drug in the world. If you’ve seen that number in a headline, it probably took your breath away. That reaction is completely understandable.

But here is what the headlines rarely explain: no family is expected to pay this out of pocket. Multiple overlapping systems — insurance coverage, manufacturer assistance programs, government benefits, and charitable organizations — exist specifically to ensure that cost is not a barrier to your child receiving treatment.

The financial landscape around MLD treatment is genuinely complex. This section of the resource exists to walk you through it clearly, so you can focus your energy on your child rather than on deciphering insurance paperwork.

The cost of Lenmeldy is staggering on paper. But access programs, insurance coverage, and financial assistance exist specifically to ensure cost is NOT a barrier to treatment. Your first call should be to Orchard Assist at 833-672-4278 — the manufacturer’s dedicated support program. They will help you navigate every financial step.

What Families Actually Face

▼

The cost of MLD extends well beyond the treatment itself. Understanding the full picture helps you plan ahead and identify which assistance programs apply to each category of expense. Here is what families commonly encounter:

Treatment Costs

Whether your child receives Lenmeldy gene therapy or a hematopoietic stem cell transplant (HSCT), the treatment itself is the largest single expense. Gene therapy carries the $4.25 million wholesale price. HSCT is less expensive but still involves hundreds of thousands of dollars in hospital charges. In both cases, insurance is the primary mechanism for coverage — and both treatments have established pathways for insurance approval.

Hospital Stays

MLD treatment requires extended hospitalization. Gene therapy involves a conditioning regimen (chemotherapy to prepare the body), the infusion itself, and a recovery period that typically spans several weeks to months in the hospital. HSCT has a similar timeline. These stays generate significant facility, nursing, pharmacy, and specialist charges — all of which are typically billed through your insurance along with the treatment.

Travel and Lodging

There are only a handful of qualified treatment centers in the United States. Your family may need to travel hundreds or thousands of miles and stay near the hospital for weeks or months. Travel costs include:

  • Airfare or driving expenses for the patient, parents, and potentially siblings
  • Lodging near the treatment center (hotels, extended-stay housing, or Ronald McDonald House)
  • Meals and daily expenses away from home
  • Multiple trips — pre-treatment evaluation, the treatment stay itself, and follow-up visits

Ongoing Specialist Care

After treatment, your child will need regular follow-up appointments with multiple specialists — neurologists, geneticists, developmental pediatricians, and others. These visits often involve travel to specialized centers and generate co-pays, lab fees, and imaging costs (MRIs, nerve conduction studies). This monitoring continues for years after treatment.

Equipment and Adaptive Devices

Depending on your child’s needs, equipment costs may include:

  • Wheelchairs, standers, and walkers
  • Orthotics and braces (AFOs, SMOs)
  • Communication devices (AAC)
  • Feeding equipment (G-tube supplies, feeding pumps)
  • Bath chairs, car seats, and positioning equipment
  • Hospital beds and mattresses

Insurance typically covers a portion of durable medical equipment (DME), but co-pays and items deemed “not medically necessary” by insurers can add up.

Lost Income

This is one of the most significant — and least discussed — costs of MLD. Caregiving for a child with MLD often requires one parent to significantly reduce or stop working entirely. During treatment, both parents may need to be away from work for months. After treatment, ongoing medical appointments, therapy sessions, and daily care needs continue to impact work capacity. The loss of one income can be financially devastating on top of increased medical expenses.

Home Modifications

As your child’s mobility needs change, your home may need modifications:

  • Wheelchair ramps and widened doorways
  • Accessible bathroom modifications (roll-in shower, grab bars)
  • Stair lifts or first-floor bedroom conversion
  • Vehicle modifications for wheelchair transport

Therapy Co-pays

Children with MLD often receive physical therapy, occupational therapy, and speech therapy multiple times per week. Even with insurance coverage, co-pays of $20–$50 per session add up quickly when therapy is this frequent. Some families face hundreds of dollars per month in therapy co-pays alone.

The Good News: Help Exists at Every Level

▼

The financial picture can feel overwhelming when you look at all these categories together. But the reality is that a combination of programs, benefits, and organizations can address most of these costs. No single program covers everything — but layered together, they create a safety net that has enabled families across the country to access treatment for their children.

Here is how the coverage layers work:

Cost Category Primary Coverage Additional Help
Treatment (gene therapy / HSCT) Private insurance or Medicaid Orchard Assist, hospital financial aid
Hospital stays Insurance (bundled with treatment) Out-of-pocket maximum caps your share
Travel & lodging Some insurance plans, Orchard Assist Ronald McDonald House, Cure MLD grants, charitable orgs
Specialist care Insurance co-pays Medicaid as secondary, HealthWell Foundation
Equipment Insurance DME benefits Easter Seals, state programs, charitable orgs
Lost income FMLA (job protection), SSI Emergency grants, community fundraising
Home modifications State disability programs Grants (Fred’s Footsteps, etc.), Medicaid waivers
Therapy co-pays Insurance + Medicaid secondary Early Intervention (free, birth to 3), therapy scholarships
Start with Orchard Assist and your hospital social worker. These two resources together can help you map out which programs apply to your specific situation and guide you through applications. You don’t have to figure this out alone.

Where to Start

▼

Financial planning for MLD treatment can feel like an impossible task when you’re also processing a diagnosis and making urgent medical decisions. Here is a practical sequence:

  1. Call Orchard Assist: 833-672-4278. This is the manufacturer’s patient support program. They will investigate your insurance benefits, help with prior authorization, estimate out-of-pocket costs, and connect you with financial assistance if needed. They should be your first call.
  2. Ask your treating hospital for a social worker. Hospital social workers specialize in helping families navigate financial assistance, government programs, and charitable support. They do this every day.
  3. Review your insurance plan details. Understand your deductible, out-of-pocket maximum, and any specific policies around gene therapy or transplant coverage.
  4. Apply for government programs. Medicaid, SSI, and state waiver programs can provide secondary coverage and cash assistance. Apply early — processing takes time.
  5. Reach out to charitable organizations. Cure MLD, Be The Match, and others provide grants for travel, emergency expenses, and specific needs.
