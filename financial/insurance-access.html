Private Insurance Coverage

▼

If your family has private health insurance through an employer or the ACA marketplace, here is what you need to know about Lenmeldy coverage:

Most Commercial Plans Have Covered Lenmeldy

Lenmeldy is FDA-approved, which is a significant factor in insurance coverage decisions. Since its approval in March 2024, most major commercial insurance plans have covered the treatment. FDA approval does not guarantee automatic coverage, but it creates a strong foundation for approval, and insurers have generally recognized Lenmeldy as medically necessary for eligible patients.

Prior Authorization Is Required

No insurer will pay for Lenmeldy without prior authorization. This is a standard process for high-cost specialty treatments, not a sign that coverage will be denied. The authorization process involves your treating physician submitting detailed medical records, test results, and a justification for why Lenmeldy is medically necessary for your child. Orchard Assist helps with this entire process.

Appeal Processes for Denials

If your initial prior authorization request is denied, do not accept the denial as final. Insurance denials for rare disease treatments are common and are frequently overturned on appeal. Many denials result from administrative issues — missing documentation, coding errors, or insufficient clinical information — rather than a fundamental coverage refusal.

Never accept a first denial as final. Insurance denials for rare disease treatments are common and frequently overturned on appeal. Ask Orchard Assist for help with the appeal process immediately.

The appeal process typically involves:

  1. Internal appeal — a formal written request to your insurer to reconsider. Your physician provides additional documentation and a peer-to-peer review (a conversation between your doctor and the insurer’s medical reviewer) may be arranged.
  2. External review — if the internal appeal is denied, you have the right to an independent external review by a third party not employed by your insurance company. This is a legal right under the ACA for all marketplace and employer plans.
  3. State insurance department complaint — you can file a complaint with your state’s Department of Insurance if you believe the denial is unjust. State regulators have the authority to intervene.

Out-of-Pocket Maximums

Every ACA-compliant insurance plan has an annual out-of-pocket maximum — the most you can be required to pay for covered services in a plan year. For 2026, the federal maximum is $10,600 for an individual or $21,200 for a family (many plans have lower limits). Once you hit this maximum, your insurance covers 100% of covered services for the rest of the plan year. This means that even for a $4.25 million treatment, your maximum direct cost is capped.

Case Management

Ask your insurance company about case management services. Most major insurers assign a case manager to complex or high-cost cases. Your case manager becomes your advocate within the insurance company, coordinating approvals and helping resolve issues. Request a case manager early in the process.

Tips for Insurance Navigation

▼

Dealing with insurance for a rare disease treatment is one of the most stressful parts of this journey. These practical tips come from families who have been through it:

Document Everything

Keep a written record of every interaction with your insurance company. For every phone call, note:

  • Date and time of the call
  • Name of the representative you spoke with
  • Reference number or case number for the call
  • What was discussed and any commitments made

Save all letters, emails, and explanation of benefits (EOB) statements. Create a dedicated folder (physical or digital) for all insurance documentation. This record becomes invaluable if you need to appeal a decision or escalate a dispute.

Get Pre-Authorization in Writing

Verbal approval over the phone is not enough. Always request written confirmation of any authorization, including the specific services approved, the authorization number, and the dates of coverage. If an insurer says something is approved verbally but later denies the claim, written confirmation protects you.

If Denied, Appeal Immediately

Insurance denials have deadlines for appeals — typically 30 to 180 days depending on the type of denial and your state’s laws. Do not delay. Contact Orchard Assist immediately upon receiving a denial; they have experience with the appeal process and can help your medical team prepare a strong appeal.

Request a Patient Advocate

Your treating hospital likely has a patient advocate or patient representative department. These professionals help patients resolve insurance disputes, billing issues, and financial concerns. They understand the hospital’s billing system and can often intervene with insurers on your behalf.

Your Hospital’s Billing Department and Social Worker Are Allies

Hospital billing departments deal with insurance companies every day. They know the coding and documentation requirements that lead to successful claims. The hospital’s social worker can help you identify financial assistance programs, navigate government benefits, and connect with charitable organizations. These people are on your side — use them.

You have a team. Orchard Assist, your hospital social worker, your hospital’s billing department, your insurance case manager, and your treating physician’s office all play roles in getting treatment approved and paid for. You are the coordinator, but you are not alone in this work.

Marketplace & ACA Plans

▼

If your family obtains insurance through the Health Insurance Marketplace (healthcare.gov or your state marketplace), there are important protections that apply:

  • No pre-existing condition exclusions. Under the Affordable Care Act, marketplace plans cannot deny coverage, charge higher premiums, or exclude treatment based on your child’s MLD diagnosis.
  • Essential health benefits. All marketplace plans must cover a set of essential health benefits including hospitalization, prescription drugs, laboratory services, and rehabilitative services. Gene therapy falls under these categories.
  • Out-of-pocket maximums apply. The same annual caps on your costs apply to marketplace plans.
  • Premium subsidies. If your family’s income qualifies, you may receive subsidies that reduce your monthly premium and out-of-pocket costs.
  • Special enrollment period. A new diagnosis of a serious medical condition may qualify you for a special enrollment period if you need to change plans outside the regular open enrollment window. Check with your state marketplace.

If you are between insurance plans or considering a switch, consult with Orchard Assist and your hospital social worker before making changes. The timing of insurance transitions relative to treatment can have significant financial implications.

Do not let insurance status delay treatment evaluation. If your child is potentially eligible for Lenmeldy, begin the medical evaluation process immediately regardless of your current insurance situation. Insurance and financial issues can be worked in parallel — the treatment window cannot wait for paperwork.

Employer-Sponsored Plans

▼

Most Americans receive health insurance through their employer. If this is your situation:

  • Contact your HR department. Ask about the specifics of your plan, including any gene therapy or specialty drug coverage policies. HR can also connect you with the plan administrator for detailed questions.
  • Self-funded vs. fully insured. Large employers often have self-funded plans (the employer pays claims directly) rather than fully insured plans (an insurance company pays claims). Self-funded plans may have more flexibility in coverage decisions but are regulated under federal (ERISA) law rather than state insurance law. Understanding which type you have affects your appeal rights.
  • COBRA coverage. If you or your spouse leaves employment (voluntarily or involuntarily) during treatment, COBRA allows you to continue your employer-sponsored coverage for up to 18 months (sometimes 36 months for certain events). COBRA premiums are expensive because you pay the full cost, but maintaining coverage continuity during treatment is critical.
  • Do not change jobs during treatment without careful planning. Switching employers means switching insurance plans, which can disrupt authorizations and coverage. If a job change is unavoidable, work with Orchard Assist to plan the transition.
