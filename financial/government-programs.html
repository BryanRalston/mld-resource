Government Programs

Medicaid

▼

Medicaid is a joint federal-state program that provides health coverage to eligible low-income individuals and families. For families dealing with MLD, Medicaid can be a critical source of coverage — either as primary insurance or as a supplement to private insurance.

Income-Based Medicaid

Standard Medicaid eligibility is based on household income, and thresholds vary significantly by state. Under the ACA’s Medicaid expansion (adopted by most but not all states), families with incomes up to 138% of the federal poverty level qualify. Even in non-expansion states, children typically qualify at higher income thresholds than adults — many states cover children in families earning up to 200–300% of the federal poverty level through a combination of Medicaid and CHIP.

Contact your state’s Department of Health and Human Services or Medicaid agency to determine eligibility. Applications can typically be submitted online, by phone, by mail, or in person.

TEFRA / Katie Beckett Waiver

This is one of the most important programs for MLD families to know about. The TEFRA option (Tax Equity and Fiscal Responsibility Act), also called the Katie Beckett waiver in some states, allows children with significant disabilities to qualify for Medicaid based on the child’s disability — not the family’s income.

Under standard Medicaid rules, a child living at home is assessed based on the parents’ income. TEFRA changes this: if the child has a disability severe enough that they would qualify for institutional (hospital or nursing facility) care, they can receive Medicaid while living at home, regardless of family income. MLD typically meets this threshold.

Key points about TEFRA / Katie Beckett:

  • Available in many states, but not all — and the name and specific rules vary by state
  • Income of the parents is not counted for the child’s eligibility
  • The child must have a disability that meets institutional-level care criteria
  • Once approved, the child receives full Medicaid benefits
  • Some states charge a monthly premium based on family income, but it is typically far less than the cost of private insurance or out-of-pocket care
Check your state’s specific program. Visit kidswaivers.com for a state-by-state guide to Medicaid waivers for children with disabilities, including TEFRA and Katie Beckett programs. Your hospital social worker can also help you determine what is available in your state.

Medicaid as Secondary Insurance

If your family has private insurance, Medicaid can serve as secondary insurance, picking up costs that your primary insurance does not cover — co-pays, deductibles, and services not covered by your primary plan. This dual coverage can dramatically reduce your out-of-pocket expenses. Many MLD families have both private insurance and Medicaid, using the private plan as primary and Medicaid as secondary.

Supplemental Security Income (SSI)

▼

SSI is a federal program administered by the Social Security Administration (SSA) that provides monthly cash payments to people with disabilities who have limited income and resources. Children with disabilities can qualify.

Eligibility for Children

  • Disability requirement: The child must have a medically determinable physical or mental impairment that results in marked and severe functional limitations and is expected to last at least 12 months or result in death. MLD qualifies under these criteria.
  • Income requirement: The family’s income and resources must fall below SSI’s limits. Part of the parents’ income is “deemed” to the child, which means higher-income families may not qualify. However, the income thresholds are more generous than many families expect — it is worth applying to find out.

What SSI Provides

  • Monthly cash benefit: The federal maximum SSI payment for 2026 is approximately $994 per month for an eligible individual, though the actual amount depends on other income and state supplementation. Many states add a state supplement on top of the federal payment.
  • Automatic Medicaid eligibility: In most states, approval for SSI automatically qualifies your child for Medicaid. This is one of the fastest paths to Medicaid coverage for children with disabilities.

How to Apply

Apply at your local Social Security Administration office, by calling SSA at 1-800-772-1213, or online at ssa.gov. Bring or have ready:

  • Child’s Social Security number and birth certificate
  • Medical records, test results, and physician contact information
  • Information about the child’s daily functioning and limitations
  • Family income and resource documentation

The application process can take several months. Apply as soon as possible — benefits can sometimes be backdated to the date of application.

Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI)

▼

SSDI is a separate program from SSI. While SSI is need-based, SSDI is based on a worker’s employment history and Social Security contributions.

For Parents Who Must Leave Work

If a parent must leave their job or significantly reduce their work hours to provide full-time care for a child with MLD, they may qualify for SSDI based on their own disability (caregiver burnout and associated health conditions) or through other provisions. However, SSDI eligibility is based on the worker’s own inability to work due to a disabling condition — it is not directly available for caregivers who are physically healthy but unable to work due to caregiving demands.

That said, many parents caring for children with severe disabilities do develop their own health conditions (depression, anxiety, physical injuries from caregiving) that may qualify. Consult with a Social Security attorney or advocate if you believe this applies to your situation.

For the Child as an Adult

If your child reaches age 18 and is disabled, they may qualify for SSDI as a disabled adult child based on a parent’s work record, even if they have never worked themselves. This can be an important benefit for long-term financial planning.

State Programs

▼

States offer a variety of programs for children with disabilities and their families. These programs vary significantly from state to state, so research your specific state’s offerings. Here are the major categories:

Early Intervention (Birth to Age 3)

Under the federal Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA), Part C, every state is required to provide early intervention services for infants and toddlers with developmental delays or disabilities. These services are provided at no cost to families and include:

  • Physical therapy
  • Occupational therapy
  • Speech-language therapy
  • Developmental monitoring and assessment
  • Family training and counseling
  • Assistive technology evaluation

Contact your state’s early intervention program as soon as possible after diagnosis. Services are delivered in the child’s natural environment (usually your home) and can begin immediately after evaluation. A child with MLD will qualify.

CHIP (Children’s Health Insurance Program)

CHIP provides health coverage for children in families whose income is too high for Medicaid but who cannot afford private insurance. Each state sets its own eligibility rules, but most cover children in families earning up to 200–300% of the federal poverty level. CHIP can provide comprehensive coverage at low or no cost. Apply through your state’s Medicaid/CHIP agency or at healthcare.gov.

State Disability Services

Most states have a Department of Developmental Disabilities (or similarly named agency) that provides services for children and adults with significant disabilities. These services may include:

  • Respite care (temporary relief for caregivers)
  • Home and community-based services
  • Assistive technology programs
  • Home modification assistance
  • Family support programs
  • Service coordination / case management

State-Specific Rare Disease Programs

Some states have programs specifically for rare disease patients or for patients requiring high-cost treatments. Your hospital social worker or state health department can help you identify any state-specific programs that apply.

Apply for everything you might qualify for. Government programs are designed to help families in exactly this situation. Do not self-select out of programs because you think you won’t qualify — let the program make that determination. A hospital social worker can help navigate applications for multiple programs simultaneously.

FMLA (Family and Medical Leave Act)

▼

The Family and Medical Leave Act is a federal law that provides job-protected leave for family medical situations. While it does not provide income replacement, it protects your job while you focus on your child’s treatment.

What FMLA Provides

  • Up to 12 weeks of unpaid leave per year for the care of a child with a serious health condition
  • Job protection — your employer must hold your position (or an equivalent one) for you to return to
  • Continuation of health insurance — your employer must maintain your group health insurance coverage during FMLA leave under the same terms as if you were still working
  • Intermittent leave — you can take FMLA leave in blocks of time or reduce your work schedule rather than taking all 12 weeks at once, when medically necessary

Eligibility Requirements

  • You must work for an employer with 50 or more employees within a 75-mile radius
  • You must have worked for the employer for at least 12 months
  • You must have worked at least 1,250 hours in the 12 months before taking leave

Important Notes

Both parents are independently eligible for FMLA leave, even if they work for the same employer. This means your family can potentially have 24 weeks of combined leave. Some states have their own family leave laws that provide additional protections or paid leave — check your state’s labor department.

FMLA leave is unpaid, but your health insurance continues. This is critical during treatment. Even though you may lose income during leave, maintaining insurance coverage means treatment and hospital costs continue to be covered. Some employers offer short-term disability insurance or paid family leave as additional benefits — check with your HR department.

Tax Considerations

▼

While tax benefits do not provide immediate relief, they can reduce your family’s overall financial burden over time. Consult with a tax professional experienced in medical expenses and disability-related tax provisions.

Medical Expense Deductions

You can deduct unreimbursed medical expenses that exceed 7.5% of your adjusted gross income (AGI) on your federal tax return. For MLD families, deductible expenses include:

  • Insurance premiums (if not paid pre-tax)
  • Co-pays, deductibles, and co-insurance
  • Travel costs for medical care (mileage, airfare, lodging up to $50/night per person)
  • Prescription medications and medical supplies
  • Therapy costs (PT, OT, speech)
  • Medical equipment and adaptive devices
  • Home modifications made for medical reasons

Keep receipts and records of all medical-related expenses throughout the year. With the volume of medical care an MLD family needs, these deductions can be substantial.

ABLE Accounts

ABLE (Achieving a Better Life Experience) accounts are tax-advantaged savings accounts for individuals with disabilities. Key features:

  • Contributions grow tax-free when used for qualified disability-related expenses
  • Qualified expenses include medical care, housing, transportation, education, assistive technology, and more
  • Up to $20,000 per year in contributions (2026 limit)
  • Account balances up to $100,000 do not count against SSI resource limits
  • The disability must have occurred before age 26
  • Available in most states — you can open an account in any state’s program, not just your own

ABLE accounts are one of the best tools for saving money for your child’s future needs without jeopardizing eligibility for means-tested government benefits.

Dependent Care Credits

The Child and Dependent Care Credit can offset some costs of care for a dependent with a disability while you work or look for work. The credit is worth up to 35% of qualifying expenses, depending on your income. If your child qualifies as a dependent due to disability, there is no age limit for this credit.

Finding All Available Benefits

▼

The number of government programs can be overwhelming. These tools help you identify what you qualify for:

  • Benefits.gov — the federal government’s official benefits eligibility finder. Answer a series of questions and it generates a list of federal and state programs you may qualify for.
  • KidsWaivers.com — a state-by-state guide to Medicaid waivers specifically for children with disabilities.
  • 211.org or dial 2-1-1 — United Way’s helpline connecting people with local health and human services, including government benefit enrollment assistance.
  • Your hospital social worker — they know the programs available in your state and can help you prioritize applications.
Apply early and apply broadly. Government program applications often take weeks to months to process. Submit applications as soon as you can, even if you are not sure you qualify. You can always decline a benefit you do not need, but you cannot speed up a process you have not started.
