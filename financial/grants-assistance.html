The table below lists organizations that may be able to help with specific expenses. Eligibility requirements, application processes, and available funding vary — contact each organization directly for current information.

Apply to multiple organizations simultaneously. Many have quick turnaround times, and you can use different grants for different needs. Do not wait for one response before applying to another.

Fundraising Considerations

▼

Many MLD families turn to community fundraising to help cover the gaps that insurance and grants do not reach. If you choose this path, here are practical considerations:

Online Fundraising Platforms

  • GoFundMe — the largest medical fundraising platform. Free to create a campaign; donors pay a small processing fee. GoFundMe has a dedicated medical fundraising category with tips for writing effective campaigns.
  • GiveSendGo — a faith-based crowdfunding platform. Free to use; donations go directly to the campaign creator.

Tips for effective campaigns:

  • Share your family’s story honestly and specifically — people respond to real stories more than general appeals
  • Be specific about what the funds will be used for (travel, lodging, equipment, lost income)
  • Update the campaign regularly with your child’s progress
  • Share across social media and ask friends and family to share as well
  • A photo or video of your child and family significantly increases engagement

Local Community Fundraisers

Community events can be both effective fundraisers and powerful sources of emotional support. Ideas that MLD families have used successfully:

  • Benefit dinners or spaghetti suppers at local churches or community centers
  • Charity runs, walks, or bike rides
  • Auction events (silent or live) with donated items from local businesses
  • School or workplace collection drives
  • Benefit concerts or community talent shows

If organizing a fundraiser feels overwhelming, ask a friend or family member to take the lead. Many communities rally around families facing medical crises — often all you need to do is let people know how they can help.

Tax Implications

Funds raised through personal fundraising campaigns (GoFundMe, community events) are generally considered gifts to the recipient and are not taxable income for the family receiving them. However:

  • If funds are raised through a registered 501(c)(3) nonprofit, donors may receive a tax deduction, but the organization must comply with nonprofit regulations
  • Large gifts (over $18,000 from a single donor in 2026) may require the donor to file a gift tax return, though they rarely owe tax
  • Fundraised money may affect eligibility for means-tested programs like SSI or Medicaid if it pushes your resources above program limits — consider depositing funds into an ABLE account to avoid this
  • Consult a tax professional for your specific situation

Help with Fundraising Setup

Some organizations offer help with organizing fundraising efforts:

  • Cure MLD can help connect you with other families who have run successful campaigns
  • Your treating hospital’s social worker may know local organizations that help families set up fundraising events
  • Some communities have “fundraising coordinators” through churches, schools, or civic organizations who volunteer their time

Long-Term Financial Planning

▼

Beyond the immediate costs of treatment, families should begin thinking about long-term financial planning for their child’s future. Even children who respond well to treatment may have ongoing needs that require financial resources. Planning now protects your child later.

Special Needs Trust

A special needs trust (also called a supplemental needs trust) is a legal arrangement that holds assets for a person with a disability without affecting their eligibility for government benefits like SSI and Medicaid. This is one of the most important long-term planning tools for MLD families.

  • Third-party trust: Funded by parents, grandparents, or other family members. Does not count against the beneficiary’s resource limits for government programs. No payback to Medicaid is required at the beneficiary’s death.
  • First-party trust: Funded by the disabled individual’s own assets (such as an inheritance or legal settlement). Also protects benefit eligibility, but must include a provision to repay Medicaid upon the beneficiary’s death from remaining assets.
  • Pooled trust: Managed by a nonprofit organization, combining funds from multiple beneficiaries for investment purposes while maintaining separate accounts. A good option if the amount to be placed in trust is relatively small.

An attorney specializing in special needs or elder law can help establish the right type of trust. Many offer free initial consultations. The Arc also provides resources on trust establishment and can connect you with qualified attorneys.

ABLE Accounts

As described in the Government Programs section, ABLE accounts provide a simpler, more accessible alternative to trusts for amounts up to $100,000 (above which SSI benefits may be suspended, though Medicaid eligibility continues). ABLE accounts are:

  • Easy to open — most state programs allow online enrollment
  • Flexible — funds can be used for a wide range of disability-related expenses
  • Tax-advantaged — earnings grow tax-free when used for qualified expenses
  • Benefit-protective — up to $100,000 does not count against SSI asset limits

Many families use both an ABLE account (for current and near-term expenses) and a special needs trust (for larger, long-term assets).

Estate Planning

Every family with a child who has a disability should address estate planning, even if your assets are modest. Key considerations:

  • Do not leave assets directly to your child. A direct inheritance can disqualify them from SSI, Medicaid, and other means-tested benefits. Instead, leave assets to a special needs trust established for their benefit.
  • Update beneficiary designations. Life insurance policies, retirement accounts, and other assets with beneficiary designations should name the special needs trust as beneficiary, not the child directly.
  • Name a guardian. Your will should designate a guardian for your child in the event both parents die or become incapacitated. Choose someone who understands your child’s needs and your wishes for their care.
  • Letter of intent. Write a detailed letter (not legally binding but practically essential) describing your child’s daily routine, medical needs, preferences, personality, and your wishes for their care. This document guides future caregivers in understanding your child as a person, not just a patient.

Working with a Financial Planner

Consider consulting a financial planner experienced in special needs planning. These professionals understand the intersection of government benefits, tax law, trust law, and investment planning for families with disabilities. Organizations that can help you find qualified planners:

  • Special Needs Alliance (specialneedsalliance.org) — a national network of disability and special needs attorneys
  • The Arc (thearc.org) — provides resources on financial planning and can connect you with local professionals
  • Academy of Special Needs Planners (specialneedsplanners.com) — directory of attorneys specializing in special needs planning
Long-term planning does not need to happen immediately. Focus first on your child’s treatment and the immediate financial needs. But within the first year after diagnosis, begin laying the groundwork for a special needs trust and ABLE account. These tools protect your child’s future without jeopardizing the benefits they need today.

Putting It All Together

▼

The financial landscape around MLD is complex, but it does not have to be navigated alone. Here is a practical summary of action steps:

  1. Immediate: Call Orchard Assist (833-672-4278) and request a hospital social worker
  2. Within the first week: Begin insurance pre-authorization process with Orchard Assist’s help
  3. Within the first month: Apply for Medicaid (including TEFRA/Katie Beckett if available in your state), SSI, and early intervention services
  4. Ongoing: Apply to charitable organizations for travel grants, emergency funds, and specific needs as they arise
  5. Within the first year: Open an ABLE account, explore special needs trust options, and begin long-term financial planning

Every family’s situation is different. Your hospital social worker and Orchard Assist representative can help you prioritize based on your specific circumstances, insurance status, and state of residence.

You are not in this alone. Every organization listed on this page, every program described in this section, exists because people and institutions recognized that families facing rare diseases should not bear the financial burden by themselves. The help is real, it is available, and it is there for you. Reach out.
