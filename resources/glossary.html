Glossary of Terms

Medical terminology can feel overwhelming, especially when you are learning about a new diagnosis. This glossary covers the terms you are most likely to encounter as you learn about MLD, its treatment, and your child's care.

▼ A

Allele
One of two copies of a gene inherited from each parent. Every person carries two alleles for the ARSA gene — one from their mother and one from their father. In MLD, both alleles must carry mutations for the disease to occur.

Apheresis
A medical procedure that collects specific blood components — such as stem cells — from the bloodstream. During apheresis, blood is drawn, the needed component is separated out, and the remaining blood is returned to the body. This is used to collect a patient's own stem cells before gene therapy.

ARSA (Arylsulfatase A)
The enzyme that is deficient in MLD. ARSA normally breaks down sulfatides — fatty substances in the nervous system. When ARSA is missing or not working properly, sulfatides accumulate to toxic levels, damaging the myelin sheath around nerves.

Ataxia
Lack of voluntary coordination of muscle movements. In MLD, ataxia often appears as difficulty with balance, walking, and fine motor tasks.

Autosomal recessive
The inheritance pattern of MLD. "Autosomal" means the gene is on a non-sex chromosome (chromosome 22). "Recessive" means both copies of the gene must be mutated to cause disease. Each parent of an affected child is typically a carrier — carrying one mutated and one normal copy.

▼ B

Blood-brain barrier (BBB)
A protective barrier formed by the cells lining blood vessels in the brain. It limits which substances can pass from the bloodstream into brain tissue. This barrier is a key challenge in treating brain diseases — many drugs cannot cross it. Gene therapy for MLD works in part because the corrected cells can cross this barrier and deliver enzyme to the brain.

Busulfan
A chemotherapy drug used for conditioning before gene therapy or stem cell transplant. Busulfan destroys existing bone marrow cells to make room for the new, gene-corrected or donor cells. Conditioning is a critical and physically demanding part of treatment.

▼ C

Carrier
A person who has one mutated copy of the ARSA gene and one normal copy. Carriers do not have MLD and typically show no symptoms, but they can pass the mutated gene to their children. When both parents are carriers, each pregnancy has a 25% chance of producing an affected child.

Central nervous system (CNS)
The brain and spinal cord. MLD primarily affects the central nervous system by destroying the myelin that insulates nerve fibers, leading to progressive loss of motor and cognitive function.

Conditioning
Chemotherapy given before gene therapy or stem cell transplant to destroy existing bone marrow and make room for new cells. Conditioning is necessary for the transplanted or gene-corrected cells to engraft and begin producing the missing enzyme. The conditioning regimen typically involves busulfan.

Contracture
A permanent tightening of muscles, tendons, or other tissues that restricts normal movement of a joint. Contractures can develop in MLD as spasticity progresses, and physical therapy and positioning are important for prevention.

▼ D

Demyelination
The loss or destruction of myelin — the protective fatty coating around nerve fibers. Demyelination is the hallmark of MLD and the primary cause of neurological symptoms. As myelin breaks down, nerve signals slow and eventually fail, leading to progressive loss of function.

Dysphagia
Difficulty swallowing. Dysphagia is common in MLD as the disease progresses and can lead to aspiration (food or liquid entering the airway), making feeding decisions an important part of care planning.

▼ E

Engraftment
The process by which transplanted or gene-modified stem cells begin growing and functioning in the bone marrow. Successful engraftment means the new cells are producing healthy blood cells — including the immune cells that deliver ARSA enzyme to the nervous system. Engraftment is typically confirmed through blood tests in the weeks following transplant.

Enzyme replacement therapy (ERT)
A treatment approach that delivers the missing enzyme directly into the body. For MLD, intrathecal (into the spinal fluid) ERT has been explored in clinical trials as a way to deliver ARSA enzyme directly to the central nervous system.

▼ G

G-tube (Gastrostomy tube)
A feeding tube placed directly through the abdominal wall into the stomach. A G-tube ensures safe, reliable nutrition and hydration when swallowing becomes difficult or unsafe. Many MLD families find that a G-tube reduces stress around feeding and improves their child's comfort.

Gene therapy
A treatment that introduces functional copies of a gene into a patient's own cells to correct a genetic disorder. For MLD, gene therapy (Lenmeldy) involves collecting a patient's stem cells, inserting a working copy of the ARSA gene using a lentiviral vector, and returning the corrected cells to the patient.

Genetic counselor
A healthcare professional specially trained to help families understand genetic conditions, inheritance patterns, testing options, and their implications. Genetic counselors play a key role in family planning decisions for MLD families.

Gross Motor Function Classification (GMFC-MLD)
A classification system used to describe and track motor function in MLD patients. It provides a standardized way to measure disease progression and treatment response.

▼ H

Hematopoietic stem cell transplant (HSCT)
A procedure that replaces a patient's bone marrow stem cells with healthy cells from a matched donor. For MLD, HSCT provides a source of cells that produce ARSA enzyme. While less effective than gene therapy for late infantile MLD, HSCT remains an important option — particularly for juvenile and adult forms.

Hypotonia
Abnormally low muscle tone, often described as "floppiness." Hypotonia can be an early sign of MLD, affecting a child's ability to sit, stand, and maintain posture.

▼ I

IEP (Individualized Education Program)
A legally binding document that outlines the special education services, accommodations, and goals for a child with a disability in the US public school system. Children with MLD are entitled to an IEP, and organizations like Cure MLD can help with the IEP process.

Intrathecal
Delivered directly into the cerebrospinal fluid (the fluid surrounding the brain and spinal cord), typically via lumbar puncture. Some experimental MLD treatments use intrathecal delivery to bypass the blood-brain barrier.

▼ L

Lenmeldy
The brand name for atidarsagene autotemcel, the FDA-approved gene therapy for MLD. Lenmeldy uses a lentiviral vector to insert a working ARSA gene into the patient's own stem cells, which are then transplanted back after conditioning chemotherapy.

Lentiviral vector
A modified virus used as a delivery vehicle to carry functional gene copies into cells during gene therapy. The lentivirus is engineered to be safe — it can insert genes into cells but cannot cause disease. In Lenmeldy, the lentiviral vector carries the ARSA gene into the patient's stem cells.

Leukodystrophy
A group of genetic diseases that affect the white matter (myelin) of the brain. MLD is one of the most common leukodystrophies. "Leuko" means white, "dystrophy" means impaired growth — together meaning impaired development of white matter.

Lysosomal storage disease (LSD)
A group of inherited diseases where the body cannot properly break down certain substances inside lysosomes — small compartments within cells that act as the cell's recycling center. MLD is classified as a lysosomal storage disease because the missing ARSA enzyme normally works inside lysosomes.

▼ M

MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)
A brain imaging technique that uses magnetic fields to create detailed pictures of brain structure. MRI is essential for diagnosing and monitoring MLD — it can show the pattern and extent of demyelination in the brain's white matter.

Myelin
The fatty protective coating (sheath) around nerve fibers that enables fast, efficient signal transmission. Think of myelin like the insulation around an electrical wire — without it, signals slow down, become unreliable, and eventually fail. MLD destroys myelin, which is why it causes progressive neurological decline.

▼ N

Nerve conduction study (NCS)
A test that measures how quickly electrical signals travel through peripheral nerves. Slowed nerve conduction is a hallmark finding in MLD and is used for both diagnosis and monitoring.

Neuropathy
Damage to the peripheral nerves (nerves outside the brain and spinal cord). Peripheral neuropathy is common in MLD and can cause pain, numbness, weakness, and loss of reflexes in the arms and legs.

▼ O

Oligodendrocytes
Specialized cells in the brain and spinal cord that produce and maintain myelin. In MLD, the accumulation of toxic sulfatides damages oligodendrocytes, impairing their ability to maintain the myelin sheath and leading to progressive demyelination.

▼ P

Peripheral nervous system (PNS)
The network of nerves outside the brain and spinal cord that connects the CNS to the limbs and organs. MLD affects both the central and peripheral nervous systems, which is why patients can experience both brain-related symptoms and peripheral neuropathy.

Pseudodeficiency
A condition where ARSA enzyme activity is very low — low enough to appear abnormal on screening tests — but the person is not affected by MLD and will not develop symptoms. Pseudodeficiency alleles are common in the general population (estimated 1-2%) and are an important reason why genetic confirmation testing is needed after enzyme-level screening.

PSAP (Prosaposin)
The gene that produces saposin B, a protein required for ARSA to function properly. Mutations in PSAP cause a rare variant of MLD (saposin B deficiency) where ARSA enzyme levels are normal but the enzyme cannot work without its activator protein.

▼ R

RUSP (Recommended Uniform Screening Panel)
The federal list of conditions that the US Department of Health and Human Services recommends all states screen newborns for. MLD was added to the RUSP in December 2025, a landmark achievement for the MLD community.

▼ S

Schwann cells
Cells in the peripheral nervous system that produce myelin for peripheral nerves. While oligodendrocytes myelinate nerves in the brain, Schwann cells serve the same function in the arms, legs, and rest of the body. Both cell types are affected in MLD.

Spasticity
Increased muscle tone that causes stiffness, tightness, and difficulty with movement. Spasticity is a common symptom of MLD as demyelination progresses in the central nervous system. It is managed with physical therapy, positioning, medications (such as baclofen), and sometimes botulinum toxin injections.

Sulfatides
Fatty substances (lipids) that are a normal component of myelin. In a healthy body, ARSA enzyme breaks down sulfatides as part of normal myelin maintenance. In MLD, the missing or deficient ARSA enzyme causes sulfatides to accumulate to toxic levels, damaging the nervous system.

▼ W

White matter
The tissue in the brain and spinal cord composed primarily of myelinated nerve fibers. White matter gets its color from myelin. MLD is classified as a leukodystrophy because it primarily affects the white matter — this is visible on MRI as abnormal signal in white matter regions.

Tip: Many of these terms will come up in conversations with your child's medical team. Do not hesitate to ask your doctors and nurses to explain any term you do not understand — they expect and welcome these questions. You can also bring this glossary to appointments as a reference.
