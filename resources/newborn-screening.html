Newborn Screening

Newborn screening for MLD is one of the most significant developments in the history of this disease. Early identification — before symptoms appear — opens the door to treatments that can fundamentally change a child's outcome.

▼ The RUSP Milestone

On December 16, 2025, HHS Secretary Kennedy officially added MLD to the federal Recommended Uniform Screening Panel (RUSP). This was the result of years of scientific evidence, clinical advocacy, and tireless work by MLD families and organizations — particularly the MLD Foundation.

What the RUSP means: The RUSP is the federal list of conditions that all US states are recommended to screen newborns for. With MLD now on this list, every state is expected to add MLD to their newborn screening panel.

Why This Matters Enormously

For MLD, timing is everything. Gene therapy and stem cell transplant work best — and in many cases only work — when given before symptoms appear. Once a child shows signs of MLD, the window for the most effective treatment may already be closing.

Newborn screening identifies affected babies at birth, when the treatment window is widest. This means:

  • Babies with late infantile MLD can be identified and treated before the typical symptom onset at 12-30 months.
  • Pre-symptomatic treatment with Lenmeldy gene therapy has shown dramatically better outcomes than treatment after symptoms begin.
  • Families have time to understand the diagnosis, explore options, and make informed decisions about treatment.
  • Siblings and extended family members can be tested for carrier status.
▼ How Screening Works

MLD newborn screening uses a 3-tier approach applied to dried blood spots collected from the baby's heel within the first few days of life — the same blood spots already collected for other newborn screening conditions.

Tier Test What It Measures Purpose
Tier 1 Sulfatide detection Elevated sulfatide levels in blood Initial screen — identifies babies who may have MLD
Tier 2 ARSA enzyme activity Activity level of the ARSA enzyme Confirms low enzyme activity — narrows down positive results
Tier 3 Genetic confirmation DNA sequencing of the ARSA gene Definitive genetic diagnosis — identifies specific mutations

This tiered approach is designed to minimize false positives while ensuring no affected baby is missed. Not every baby who flags on Tier 1 will have MLD — confirmatory testing through all three tiers is essential.

Implementation Timeline

After a condition is added to the RUSP, states typically take 1 to 3 years to fully implement screening. This timeline includes developing laboratory protocols, training staff, establishing follow-up procedures, and securing funding. Some states move faster than others depending on existing infrastructure and legislative processes.

Check your state: Visit mldnewbornscreening.org for the most current information on which states are screening and where implementation efforts stand.
▼ Understanding a Positive Screen

A positive newborn screen is not a diagnosis. It means further testing is needed. This is an important distinction — many babies who screen positive will not ultimately have MLD.

What Happens After a Positive Screen

  • You will be contacted by your state's newborn screening follow-up program or your pediatrician.
  • Confirmatory testing will be ordered, including ARSA enzyme activity measurement and genetic testing.
  • You will likely be referred to a specialist — typically a geneticist or pediatric neurologist with experience in leukodystrophies.
  • The confirmatory process may take several weeks, which can be an anxious time. This is normal.

Pseudodeficiency: A Common Cause of False Positives

Pseudodeficiency is a condition where ARSA enzyme activity is very low — low enough to trigger a positive screen — but the person is not affected by MLD and will never develop symptoms. Pseudodeficiency is surprisingly common, occurring in an estimated 1-2% of the general population.

Genetic testing (Tier 3) is essential to distinguish true MLD mutations from pseudodeficiency alleles. This is why confirmatory testing is so important and why a positive screen alone should not be treated as a diagnosis.

Important: If your baby has a positive newborn screen, request referral to a specialist familiar with MLD and leukodystrophies. Do not rely solely on your general pediatrician for interpretation — the nuances of pseudodeficiency and MLD genotype-phenotype relationships require specialized knowledge.
▼ For Families Diagnosed Through Other Means

If your child was diagnosed with MLD through clinical symptoms or family testing rather than newborn screening, the expansion of newborn screening is still important for your family:

  • Future siblings: If you have more children, they will be screened at birth, allowing for immediate identification and early treatment if affected.
  • Extended family: Newborn screening can identify affected babies in your extended family who carry the same genetic mutations.
  • Advocacy impact: Your family's experience contributes to the body of knowledge that makes newborn screening programs more effective.
▼ Advocacy & Resources

The MLD Foundation has been a driving force behind newborn screening advocacy for MLD, working for years to compile the evidence, build the case, and push for RUSP inclusion. Their efforts — along with the work of many MLD families who shared their stories and participated in research — made this milestone possible.
