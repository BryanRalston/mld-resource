What Is MLD?

Metachromatic Leukodystrophy (MLD) is a rare autosomal recessive lysosomal storage disorder caused by mutations in the ARSA gene (chromosome 22q13.33), which encodes the enzyme arylsulfatase A. Deficiency of this enzyme leads to accumulation of sulfatides in the central and peripheral nervous system, causing progressive demyelination — the destruction of the myelin sheath that insulates nerve fibers. Estimated incidence is approximately 1 in 40,000 to 160,000 live births.

MLD presents in three forms based on age of onset: late infantile (onset 6–30 months, most common and most rapidly progressive), juvenile (onset 3–16 years), and adult (onset >16 years). All forms result in progressive loss of motor function, cognitive abilities, and sensory function. Without disease-modifying treatment, late infantile MLD typically leads to a vegetative state within several years of symptom onset.

Key Clinical Features

  • Neurological: Progressive motor decline, gait abnormalities (often the first sign in late infantile), spasticity, hypotonia progressing to hypertonia, seizures, peripheral neuropathy with reduced nerve conduction velocities.
  • Cognitive: Regression of previously acquired language and cognitive skills. Behavioral changes may be the presenting symptom in juvenile and adult forms.
  • Vision: Optic atrophy, cortical visual impairment, nystagmus. Visual loss is progressive.
  • Hearing: Sensorineural hearing loss due to auditory nerve demyelination.
  • Gastrointestinal: Swallowing dysfunction (dysphagia), aspiration risk, feeding difficulties, gastroesophageal reflux. Many patients eventually require gastrostomy tube placement.
  • Pain: Neuropathic pain is common and may be underrecognized, especially in non-verbal patients. May present as irritability, agitation, or posturing rather than typical pain behaviors.
  • Gallbladder: Sulfatide accumulation in the gallbladder wall can cause polypoid lesions and dysfunction. Annual ultrasound screening is recommended.

Critical Considerations for Any Provider

  1. Seizure Management. This patient may have breakthrough seizures. Please review current anti-epileptic medications and rescue protocol (e.g., rectal diazepam, intranasal midazolam). Status epilepticus requires aggressive treatment. Benzodiazepines are first-line for acute seizures but use with caution regarding respiratory depression in patients with impaired airway protection.
  2. Airway & Anesthesia. Impaired swallowing and reduced airway protective reflexes increase aspiration risk. Assess airway thoroughly before any sedation or general anesthesia. Bulbar dysfunction may make intubation more complex. Video laryngoscopy recommended. Have suction immediately available. Consider post-procedure monitoring in a higher-acuity setting.
  3. Aspiration Precautions. Dysphagia is common and progressive in MLD. Follow NPO guidelines strictly. Maintain upright positioning (30–45 degrees) during and after any oral intake or medication administration. Suction equipment should be at bedside. If the patient has a gastrostomy tube, confirm tube patency and use it for medications when oral route is unsafe.
  4. Medications to Use with Caution. Avoid metoclopramide — can worsen dystonia and extrapyramidal symptoms. Use sedatives with extreme caution — respiratory depression risk is heightened in patients with impaired respiratory drive and airway protection. Use anticholinergics cautiously — can worsen constipation, urinary retention, and secretion management. Ototoxic drugs (aminoglycosides, high-dose loop diuretics) should be avoided when possible given existing or potential hearing loss.
  5. Pain Assessment. Neuropathic pain is a significant component of MLD and may not present with typical pain behaviors. Non-verbal patients require behavioral pain assessment scales (FLACC, r-FLACC, or similar). Consider gabapentin or pregabalin for neuropathic pain. Opioids may be necessary for severe pain but require careful monitoring given respiratory risks. Irritability, agitation, and increased tone may indicate pain.
  6. Mobility & Positioning. High risk for contractures, pressure injuries, and hip subluxation if immobile. Proper positioning in bed and wheelchair is critical. Reposition at minimum every 2 hours when immobile. Inspect skin at pressure points. Use appropriate support surfaces. Consult physical therapy for positioning recommendations.
  7. Temperature Regulation. Autonomic dysfunction in MLD may impair thermoregulation. Monitor temperature closely. Both hypothermia and hyperthermia may occur without typical physiological compensation. Avoid prolonged exposure to temperature extremes.
  8. Immune Considerations. If the patient has received hematopoietic stem cell transplant (HSCT) or gene therapy (Lenmeldy/atidarsagene autotemcel): the patient may be immunosuppressed or on immunosuppressive medications. Check current immunosuppression regimen and vaccination status. Live vaccines may be contraindicated. Monitor for signs of graft-versus-host disease (GVHD) in post-HSCT patients. Febrile episodes require prompt evaluation and may warrant empiric antibiotics depending on time since transplant and immune reconstitution status.

Recommended Monitoring Schedule

  • Neurology: Every 3–6 months — clinical exam, brain MRI (T2/FLAIR sequences), assessment of motor and cognitive function.
  • Audiology: Every 6–12 months — comprehensive hearing evaluation including brainstem auditory evoked response (BAER) as indicated.
  • Ophthalmology: Every 6–12 months — visual acuity, fundoscopy for optic atrophy, visual evoked potentials as indicated.
  • Nerve Conduction Study: Annually — to monitor peripheral neuropathy progression or stability.
  • Nutritional Assessment: Every 3–6 months — weight, growth parameters, swallowing function, G-tube site if applicable.
  • Developmental/Cognitive: Every 6 months — standardized developmental testing (Bayley, GMFM, or age-appropriate measures).
  • Laboratory: Per treatment protocol — ARSA enzyme activity, neurofilament light chain (NfL), CBC, comprehensive metabolic panel. Post-treatment patients require additional monitoring per their treatment center’s protocol.
  • Gallbladder Ultrasound: Annually — screening for polypoid lesions and dysfunction.
